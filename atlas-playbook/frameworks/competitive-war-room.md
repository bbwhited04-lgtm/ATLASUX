# Competitive War Room

## Purpose
A continuous competitive intelligence system that monitors, analyzes, and responds to
competitive movements. This isn't a one-time analysis — it's an always-on radar.

## Competitive Intelligence Framework

### 1. Competitor Classification

```
TIER 1 — DIRECT COMPETITORS (same problem, same audience):
Monitor: Weekly. Track every move.
Examples: If you're Swiggy, Tier 1 = Zomato.

TIER 2 — INDIRECT COMPETITORS (same problem, different approach):
Monitor: Bi-weekly.
Examples: If you're Swiggy, Tier 2 = cloud kitchens, meal kit deliveries.

TIER 3 — POTENTIAL COMPETITORS (adjacent players who could enter):
Monitor: Monthly.
Examples: If you're Swiggy, Tier 3 = Amazon (grocery+food), Google (search+local).

TIER 4 — SUBSTITUTES (different solution to same problem):
Monitor: Quarterly.
Examples: If you're Swiggy, Tier 4 = cooking at home, eating out, office cafeterias.
```

### 3. Monitoring Cadence

```
DAILY (automated):
□ Google Alerts for competitor brand names
□ App Store / Play Store new reviews (1-star reviews = their problems = your opportunities)
□ Social media mentions (Twitter/X, Reddit, LinkedIn)
□ Product changelog / blog updates (RSS feeds)

WEEKLY (15-minute review):
□ Competitor feature changes (use VisualPing or manual check)
□ Pricing changes
□ Job postings (indicate where they're investing — ML hiring = AI features coming)
□ Press coverage and blog posts

MONTHLY (1-hour deep dive):
□ Full competitive landscape update
□ Feature comparison matrix update
□ User sentiment analysis (review mining)
□ Strategic assessment: Are they moving toward us or away?

QUARTERLY (half-day strategic review):
□ Porter's Five Forces refresh
□ Competitive positioning map update
□ Win/loss analysis (why did users choose us vs. them?)
□ Strategy implications: Do we need to adjust our roadmap?
```

### 4. Competitive Response Playbook

```
SCENARIO: Competitor launches a feature we don't have
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. ASSESS (within 48 hours):
   - Is this feature actually good, or just announced?
   - Do our users care? (Check: are users asking us for this?)
   - Does it threaten our core value proposition?

2. DECIDE (within 1 week):
   - IGNORE: Feature doesn't matter to our users → No action
   - ACKNOWLEDGE: Users are asking → Add to roadmap, communicate timeline
   - ACCELERATE: Threatens our position → Fast-track development
   - LEAPFROG: Build a better version that goes further → Strategic investment

3. COMMUNICATE (if needed):
   - Internal: Brief the team on the competitive move and our response
   - External: If users are asking, "We're aware and working on our approach to X"
   - NEVER: Publicly trash competitors. Compete on product, not words.

SCENARIO: Competitor cuts prices significantly
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. Don't panic. Price wars kill everyone.
2. ASSESS: Is this sustainable for them? (Check their funding, unit economics)
3. DECIDE: Can we compete on value instead of price?
   - If our product is genuinely better → Hold price, emphasize differentiation
   - If products are similar → Consider targeted discounting (not blanket cuts)
   - If we're losing deals on price → Introduce a lower tier (don't cut existing)
4. LONG GAME: Build switching costs and brand loyalty that make price less relevant

SCENARIO: Well-funded competitor enters our market
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
1. ASSESS: Are they serious or experimenting? (Check: dedicated team or side project?)
2. If serious:
   - ACCELERATE product development on your core differentiator
   - LOCK IN users: Increase switching costs, long-term contracts (B2B), loyalty programs (B2C)
   - NICHE DOWN: Be the best for a specific segment rather than competing broadly
   - PARTNER: Form partnerships that create barriers to entry
3. REMEMBER: Big companies are slow. Your advantage is speed and focus.
```

### 5. Win/Loss Analysis

```
FOR EVERY SIGNIFICANT DEAL WON OR LOST (B2B) / CHURNED USER (B2C):

INTERVIEW TEMPLATE:
1. What problem were you trying to solve?
2. What alternatives did you consider?
3. What was the deciding factor in your choice?
4. What almost made you choose differently?
5. What's one thing you wish was better?

ANALYSIS:
- Win reasons: Group by theme (features, price, support, brand, integration)
- Loss reasons: Group by theme (same categories)
- Frequency: Which reasons appear most often?
- Actionability: Which reasons can we address? At what cost?
- Trend: Are win/loss reasons changing over time?

REPORT (monthly):
- Win rate: % of competitive deals won
- Top 3 win reasons with frequency
- Top 3 loss reasons with frequency
- Competitor most frequently lost to
- Actionable recommendations (max 3)
```
