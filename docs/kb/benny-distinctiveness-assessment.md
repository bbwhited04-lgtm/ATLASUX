# Distinctiveness Assessment Framework

## Purpose

This document provides Benny (IP Counsel agent) with a comprehensive framework for assessing the distinctiveness of proposed trademarks. Distinctiveness is the threshold requirement for federal trademark registration — a mark must be capable of identifying and distinguishing the source of the applicant's goods or services.

---

## The Distinctiveness Spectrum (Abercrombie Classification)

The foundational framework comes from Abercrombie & Fitch Co. v. Hunting World, Inc., 537 F.2d 4 (2d Cir. 1976). Marks are classified along a spectrum of increasing distinctiveness:

### 1. Generic — NOT Registrable

**Definition:** The common name for the goods/services themselves. A generic term is what the product or service IS, not where it comes from.

**Test:** Is this term understood by the relevant public as referring to the genus of goods/services? Marvin Ginn test: (1) What is the genus of goods/services? (2) Is the term understood by the relevant public primarily as referring to that genus? In re Marvin Ginn Corp., 782 F.2d 987 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

**Examples:**
- COMPUTER for computers
- BOOKING.COM for hotel booking services (but see USPTO v. Booking.com B.V., 591 U.S. 381 (2020) — adding ".com" to a generic term may create a protectable mark if consumers perceive it as a source identifier, determined by consumer perception evidence)
- LIGHT BEER for low-calorie beer
- APP STORE for a digital application marketplace (disputed; found generic by some courts)

**Key principle:** Generic terms can NEVER be registered, not even with acquired distinctiveness. Genericness is a permanent bar and a ground for cancellation at any time (Lanham Act §14(3)).

**Genericide warning:** A formerly distinctive mark can become generic through widespread misuse (e.g., ASPIRIN, ESCALATOR, THERMOS). Advise clients on proper trademark usage (always as adjective, never as noun or verb).

### 2. Descriptive — Registrable Only with Acquired Distinctiveness (§2(f))

**Definition:** A mark that immediately conveys information about a quality, characteristic, function, feature, purpose, or use of the goods/services.

**Test:** Does the mark, when used in connection with the goods/services, immediately convey knowledge of a quality or characteristic of the goods/services? In re Gyulay, 820 F.2d 1216 (Fed. Cir. 1987). The determination is made in relation to the goods/services, not in the abstract.

**Examples:**
- CREAMY for yogurt
- VISION CENTER for optical services
- BEST BUY for retail electronics (registered with acquired distinctiveness)
- SHARP for televisions (originally descriptive of image quality; now has secondary meaning)
- 5-MINUTE for quick-service offerings

**Registration path:** Supplemental Register (immediate, but limited rights) or Principal Register with §2(f) claim (requires evidence of acquired distinctiveness/secondary meaning).

### 3. Suggestive — Inherently Distinctive

**Definition:** A mark that suggests or hints at a quality or characteristic of the goods/services but requires imagination, thought, or perception to reach a conclusion about the nature of the goods/services.

**Test:** Does the mark require a mental leap or exercise of imagination to connect the mark to the goods/services? If yes, it is suggestive rather than descriptive. Zatarains, Inc. v. Oak Grove Smokehouse, Inc., 698 F.2d 786 (5th Cir. 1983).

**Examples:**
- COPPERTONE for sunscreen (suggests tanning/sun protection, but requires a mental step)
- CITIBANK for banking services
- NETFLIX for streaming entertainment
- AIRBUS for aircraft
- GREYHOUND for bus transportation
- JAGUAR for automobiles

**Key principle:** The line between descriptive and suggestive is often the most litigated and difficult distinction. When in doubt, consider: Would a competitor need to use this term to describe their goods/services? If so, it leans descriptive. If not, it leans suggestive.

### 4. Arbitrary — Inherently Distinctive

**Definition:** A mark that uses a common word in a context unrelated to its ordinary dictionary meaning.

**Examples:**
- APPLE for computers (apple is a common word, but arbitrary for technology products)
- AMAZON for retail/e-commerce
- CAMEL for cigarettes
- SHELL for gasoline
- DOVE for chocolate (or soap)
- DELTA for airlines

**Strength:** Very strong marks — easy to register and enforce.

### 5. Fanciful — Inherently Distinctive (Strongest)

**Definition:** A coined or invented word with no prior meaning. Created solely to function as a trademark.

**Examples:**
- XEROX
- KODAK
- EXXON
- PEPSI
- ROLEX
- GOOGLE
- HAAGEN-DAZS

**Strength:** The strongest category of marks. Easiest to register, hardest for competitors to argue they need to use the term.

## Acquired Distinctiveness / Secondary Meaning (§2(f))

### What It Is

When a mark is not inherently distinctive (i.e., it is descriptive, geographically descriptive, or primarily merely a surname), the applicant may still register on the Principal Register by demonstrating that the mark has **acquired distinctiveness** — that through use, the primary significance of the mark in the minds of relevant consumers is as a source identifier.

### Statutory Basis

15 U.S.C. §1052(f): "Nothing herein shall prevent the registration of a mark used by the applicant which has become distinctive of the applicant's goods in commerce."

### Evidence Types and Strength

**Prima facie case (presumptive):**
- A claim of **5 years of substantially exclusive and continuous use** in commerce creates a prima facie showing of acquired distinctiveness. 37 CFR §2.41(b). The examining attorney may accept this without further evidence, but may also require additional proof.

**Direct evidence (strongest):**
- **Consumer surveys**: The gold standard. A Teflon-format survey or similar methodology showing that a significant percentage of relevant consumers associate the mark with a single source.
- **Declarations from consumers or industry members** testifying to recognition of the mark as a source identifier.

**Circumstantial evidence:**
- Advertising expenditures (dollar amounts and duration)
- Sales volume and revenue
- Length of use
- Exclusivity of use (absence of third-party use of the same/similar mark)
- Unsolicited media coverage
- Intentional copying by competitors (probative of secondary meaning)
- Social media following and engagement
- Market share data
- Awards and recognition

### §2(f) Claim Procedure

1. In the trademark application, check the box for §2(f) claim OR add the claim in response to an Office action.
2. Provide a declaration and supporting evidence.
3. The examining attorney evaluates the totality of the evidence.
4. If the evidence is insufficient, the examining attorney will issue a refusal.

### §2(f) in Part

- For composite marks where only part of the mark is descriptive, a **§2(f) in part** claim may be appropriate.
- Example: A mark consisting of a distinctive design element plus a descriptive word. The applicant claims acquired distinctiveness for the descriptive word portion while the design element is inherently distinctive.

## Functionality Doctrine

### Utilitarian Functionality

A product feature is functional if it is essential to the use or purpose of the article, or if it affects the cost or quality of the article. TrafFix Devices, Inc. v. Marketing Displays, Inc., 532 U.S. 23 (2001).

**Test:** (1) Whether a utility patent has been issued disclosing the feature, (2) whether the originator touts the utilitarian advantages of the feature, (3) whether alternative designs are available, (4) whether the feature results from a comparatively simple or cheap method of manufacture. In re Morton-Norwich Products, Inc., 671 F.2d 1332 (CCPA 1982).

**Key principle:** Functionality is an absolute bar. No amount of acquired distinctiveness overcomes it. 15 U.S.C. §1052(e)(5).

### Aesthetic Functionality

A feature is aesthetically functional if granting exclusive rights would put competitors at a significant non-reputation-related disadvantage. Qualitex Co. v. Jacobson Products Co., 514 U.S. 159 (1995).

**Example:** A color that is functional in context (green-gold for dry cleaning press pads was NOT functional; the color of farm equipment may be functional if it serves a purpose like visibility).

## Trade Dress Distinctiveness

### Product Packaging

- **May be inherently distinctive** under the Seabrook test: (1) whether the design is a common basic shape or design, (2) whether it is unique or unusual in the field, (3) whether it is a mere refinement of a commonly-adopted form of ornamentation, (4) whether it creates a commercial impression distinct from the accompanying words. Seabrook Foods, Inc. v. Bar-Well Foods, Ltd., 568 F.2d 1342 (CCPA 1977).

### Product Design

- **Never inherently distinctive.** Always requires proof of acquired distinctiveness. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Samara Bros., Inc., 529 U.S. 205 (2000).
- This applies to the shape or configuration of the product itself.

### Trade Dress Analysis Checklist

- [ ] Is this product packaging or product design?
- [ ] If product design: acquired distinctiveness evidence is mandatory
- [ ] If product packaging: assess under Seabrook factors
- [ ] Is any element functional? (If yes, that element cannot be protected)
- [ ] Is the overall commercial impression distinctive?
- [ ] Can you articulate specific elements that make it distinctive?

## Surname Marks — §2(e)(4)

### Test

Is the **primary significance** of the mark to the purchasing public that of a surname? In re Benthin Mgmt. GmbH, 37 USPQ2d 1332 (TTAB 1995).

### Factors

1. **Rareness or commonness** of the surname in the population (checked via census data, phone directories, etc.)
2. Whether anyone connected with the applicant has the surname
3. Whether the term has any recognized **meaning other than as a surname** (e.g., KING has an independent meaning; PATEL is primarily a surname)
4. Whether the mark has the **look and feel** of a surname (structure, pronunciation)
5. Whether any **stylization or design** alters the primarily-surname significance

### Overcoming Surname Refusal

- §2(f) acquired distinctiveness (5 years of use or evidence of secondary meaning)
- Argue the term has a recognized non-surname meaning
- Show the term is so rare as a surname that the public would not perceive it as one

## Geographic Marks

### Primarily Geographically Descriptive — §2(e)(2)

Three-part test:
1. The mark is the name of a place **generally known** to the public
2. The goods/services **originate** from that place
3. Consumers would likely believe the goods/services originate from that place

Overcomable with §2(f) evidence.

### Primarily Geographically Deceptively Misdescriptive — §2(e)(3)

Same test as above, EXCEPT the goods/services do NOT actually originate from the named place, AND the geographic significance is material to the purchasing decision.

**Permanent bar** — no §2(f) claim available (post-NAFTA).

### Geographic Terms That Are NOT Geographic

- If the geographic term is used in an arbitrary or fanciful way (e.g., NORTH FACE for outdoor apparel — not descriptive of a geographic origin), it may be inherently distinctive.
- If the place is so obscure that consumers would not make a geographic association, the refusal does not apply.

## Composite Marks Analysis

### Assessing Distinctiveness of Multi-Element Marks

When a mark consists of multiple elements (words, designs, colors, etc.), analyze:

1. **The mark as a whole**: The overall commercial impression determines registrability.
2. **Dominant element**: Which element is most likely to be perceived as the source identifier?
3. **Disclaimed matter**: Generic or descriptive elements may require disclaimer but remain part of the mark.
4. **Unitary mark doctrine**: If the elements create a single, unitary commercial impression, the mark is analyzed as a whole rather than element by element.

### Decision Framework

- If the dominant element is distinctive (suggestive/arbitrary/fanciful): The composite mark is likely registrable.
- If the dominant element is descriptive: The composite may still register if the overall impression is distinctive or with §2(f) evidence.
- If the dominant element is generic: Registration is problematic; the non-generic elements must carry the mark.

## Assessment Decision Tree

```
START: Evaluate the proposed mark for the stated goods/services

1. Is the term the generic name for the goods/services?
   YES → STOP. Not registrable. Advise rebranding.
   NO → Continue to 2.

2. Does the term immediately describe a feature/quality/characteristic?
   YES → It is DESCRIPTIVE. Go to 3.
   NO → Go to 4.

3. Does the applicant have evidence of acquired distinctiveness?
   YES → Registrable with §2(f) claim. Assess evidence strength.
   NO → Consider Supplemental Register or advise building evidence.

4. Does the term require imagination to connect to the goods/services?
   YES → It is SUGGESTIVE. Inherently distinctive. Registrable.
   NO → Continue to 5.

5. Is it a common word used in an unrelated context?
   YES → It is ARBITRARY. Strong mark. Registrable.
   NO → Continue to 6.

6. Is it a coined/invented word?
   YES → It is FANCIFUL. Strongest mark. Registrable.
   NO → Re-evaluate using the factors above.

ADDITIONAL CHECKS (apply regardless of spectrum position):
- Is the mark functional? → If yes, permanent bar.
- Is it primarily merely a surname? → If yes, needs §2(f).
- Is it primarily geographically descriptive? → If yes, needs §2(f).
- Is it geographically deceptively misdescriptive? → If yes, permanent bar.
- Does it contain government insignia? → If yes, permanent bar.
- Does it falsely suggest connection with a person/institution? → If yes, bar.
```

## Distinctiveness Assessment Checklist

For each proposed mark, complete:

- [ ] **Spectrum classification**: Generic / Descriptive / Suggestive / Arbitrary / Fanciful
- [ ] **Competitor necessity test**: Would competitors legitimately need to use this term?
- [ ] **Dictionary meaning analysis**: What are the ordinary meanings? Are they related to the goods/services?
- [ ] **Imagination test**: How many mental steps between the mark and the goods/services?
- [ ] **Third-party use check**: Are others in the industry using this term descriptively?
- [ ] **Functionality screen**: Does any element serve a utilitarian or aesthetic function?
- [ ] **Surname screen**: Could consumers perceive this primarily as a surname?
- [ ] **Geographic screen**: Does this reference a geographic location connected to the goods?
- [ ] **Composite analysis**: For multi-element marks, identify the dominant element and assess separately
- [ ] **Trade dress screen**: If claiming product design or packaging, assess inherent distinctiveness or §2(f) evidence
- [ ] **Overall recommendation**: Register as-is / Register with §2(f) / Supplemental Register / Advise rebranding
