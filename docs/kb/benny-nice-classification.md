# Nice Classification Reference — 12th Edition (2025)

## Purpose

This document provides Benny (IP Counsel agent) with a comprehensive reference to the Nice Classification system (12th Edition, as updated), used internationally for the classification of goods and services in trademark registrations. The Nice Classification is maintained by WIPO and adopted by the USPTO.

---

## Overview

- **34 classes for goods** (Classes 1–34)
- **11 classes for services** (Classes 35–45)
- Updated annually; the 12th Edition has been in effect since January 1, 2023, with annual updates.
- Each class has a **class heading** (general description) and an **alphabetical list** of specific goods/services.
- A single trademark application may cover **multiple classes** (multi-class application), with a separate fee per class.

## Goods Classes (1–34)

### Class 1 — Chemicals

**Heading:** Chemicals for use in industry, science, and photography, as well as in agriculture, horticulture, and forestry; unprocessed artificial resins, unprocessed plastics; fire extinguishing and fire prevention compositions; tempering and soldering preparations; substances for tanning animal skins and hides; adhesives for use in industry; putties and other paste fillers; compost, manures, fertilizers; biological preparations for use in industry and science.

**Common items:** Industrial chemicals, chemical reagents, adhesives (industrial), fertilizers, biological preparations for industrial use, unprocessed resins.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 5 (pharmaceutical chemicals), Class 2 (chemical coatings/paints), Class 17 (processed plastics/resins).

**Coordination groups:** Coordinates with Classes 2, 5, 17.

### Class 2 — Paints

**Heading:** Paints, varnishes, lacquers; preservatives against rust and against deterioration of wood; colorants, dyes; inks for printing, marking, and engraving; raw natural resins; metals in foil and powder form for use in painting, decorating, printing, and art.

**Common items:** House paints, wood stains, printing inks, food dyes, anti-rust preparations, artist pigments.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 1 (industrial chemicals), Class 16 (artists' materials).

### Class 3 — Cosmetics and Cleaning Preparations

**Heading:** Non-medicated cosmetics and toiletry preparations; non-medicated dentifrices; perfumery, essential oils; bleaching preparations and other substances for laundry use; cleaning, polishing, scouring, and abrasive preparations.

**Common items:** Soap, shampoo, skin lotion, perfume, toothpaste (non-medicated), laundry detergent, cleaning products, essential oils (cosmetic use).

**Commonly confused with:** Class 5 (medicated cosmetics/toiletries), Class 21 (cleaning tools/sponges).

### Class 4 — Lubricants and Fuels

**Heading:** Industrial oils and greases, wax; lubricants; dust absorbing, wetting, and binding compositions; fuels and illuminants; candles and wicks for lighting.

**Common items:** Motor oil, lubricating grease, gasoline, diesel, candles, wax (industrial), biofuels.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 1 (industrial chemicals), Class 11 (lighting apparatus).

### Class 5 — Pharmaceuticals

**Heading:** Pharmaceuticals, medical and veterinary preparations; sanitary preparations for medical purposes; dietetic food and substances adapted for medical or veterinary use; food for babies; dietary supplements for human beings and animals; plasters, materials for dressings; material for stopping teeth, dental wax; disinfectants; preparations for destroying vermin; fungicides, herbicides.

**Common items:** Prescription drugs, OTC medications, vitamins/supplements, medical bandages, disinfectants, baby food, medicated shampoo, herbicides, insecticides.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 3 (non-medicated versions), Class 10 (medical devices), Class 29/30 (non-medical foods), Class 31 (non-chemical agricultural products).

### Class 6 — Metal Goods

**Heading:** Common metals and their alloys, ores; metal materials for building and construction; transportable buildings of metal; non-electric cables and wires of common metal; small items of metal hardware; metal containers for storage or transport; safes.

**Common items:** Steel pipes, aluminum sheets, metal screws/bolts, metal building materials, wire fencing, metal safes, metal shipping containers.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 19 (non-metal building materials), Class 20 (non-metal hardware).

### Class 7 — Machinery

**Heading:** Machines, machine tools, power-operated tools; motors and engines (except for land vehicles); machine coupling and transmission components (except for land vehicles); agricultural implements (other than hand-operated); incubators for eggs; automatic vending machines.

**Common items:** Industrial machines, manufacturing equipment, electric motors, power tools, 3D printers, robots for manufacturing, vending machines.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 8 (hand tools), Class 12 (vehicle engines).

### Class 8 — Hand Tools

**Heading:** Hand tools and implements, hand-operated; cutlery; side arms, except firearms; razors.

**Common items:** Knives, forks, spoons, scissors, hand saws, wrenches, pliers, razors.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 7 (power tools), Class 21 (kitchen utensils).

### Class 9 — Electrical and Scientific Apparatus

**Heading:** Scientific, research, navigation, surveying, photographic, cinematographic, audiovisual, optical, weighing, measuring, signalling, detecting, testing, inspecting, life-saving, and teaching apparatus and instruments; apparatus and instruments for conducting, switching, transforming, accumulating, regulating, or controlling the distribution or use of electricity; apparatus and instruments for recording, transmitting, reproducing, or processing sound, images, or data; recorded and downloadable media, computer software, blank digital or analogue recording and storage media; mechanisms for coin-operated apparatus; cash registers, calculating devices; computers and computer peripheral devices; diving suits, divers' masks, ear plugs for diving, nose clips for divers and swimmers, gloves for divers, breathing apparatus for underwater swimming; fire-extinguishing apparatus.

**Common items:** Computer software (downloadable), mobile apps, computer hardware, smartphones, tablets, cameras, headphones, GPS devices, electrical switches, laboratory instruments, smart watches, VR headsets, encoded cards.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 42 (SaaS — software as a service is a service, not goods), Class 38 (telecommunications services vs. telecom equipment), Class 35 (computerized data processing as a service).

**Critical note for software:** Downloadable software = Class 9 (goods). Software as a service (SaaS)/cloud-based software = Class 42 (services). Many applicants need both classes.

### Class 10 — Medical Apparatus

**Heading:** Surgical, medical, dental, and veterinary apparatus and instruments; artificial limbs, eyes, and teeth; orthopaedic articles; suture materials; therapeutic and assistive devices adapted for persons with disabilities; massage apparatus; apparatus, devices, and articles for nursing infants; sexual activity apparatus, devices, and articles.

**Common items:** Surgical instruments, stethoscopes, prosthetics, hearing aids, orthopedic braces, dental equipment, blood pressure monitors.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 5 (pharmaceutical preparations), Class 9 (diagnostic electronic equipment).

### Class 11 — Environmental Control Apparatus

**Heading:** Apparatus and installations for lighting, heating, cooling, steam generating, cooking, drying, ventilating, water supply, and sanitary purposes.

**Common items:** Light fixtures, ovens, refrigerators, air conditioners, water heaters, water purifiers, toilets, faucets, solar panels (for heating).

**Commonly confused with:** Class 7 (machines), Class 21 (kitchen containers/utensils).

### Class 12 — Vehicles

**Heading:** Vehicles; apparatus for locomotion by land, air, or water.

**Common items:** Automobiles, motorcycles, bicycles, boats, aircraft, strollers, shopping carts, tires, vehicle parts.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 7 (engines not for vehicles), Class 9 (vehicle electronics/GPS).

### Class 13 — Firearms

**Heading:** Firearms; ammunition and projectiles; explosives; fireworks.

### Class 14 — Jewelry

**Heading:** Precious metals and their alloys; jewellery, precious and semi-precious stones; horological and chronometric instruments.

**Common items:** Gold jewelry, silver, watches, clocks, gemstones, costume jewelry, cufflinks, key rings of precious metal.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 26 (costume jewelry accessories like hair clips), Class 6 (base metal items).

### Class 15 — Musical Instruments

**Heading:** Musical instruments; music stands and stands for musical instruments; conductors' batons.

### Class 16 — Paper Goods and Printed Matter

**Heading:** Paper and cardboard; printed matter; bookbinding material; photographs; stationery and office requisites (except furniture); adhesives for stationery or household purposes; drawing materials and materials for artists; paintbrushes; instructional and teaching materials; plastic sheets, films, and bags for wrapping and packaging; printers' type, printing blocks.

**Common items:** Books, magazines, calendars, greeting cards, stationery, pens, pencils, art supplies, packaging materials, stickers, posters.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 9 (electronic publications/downloadable content), Class 41 (publishing services).

### Class 17 — Rubber Goods

**Heading:** Unprocessed and semi-processed rubber, gutta-percha, gum, asbestos, mica, and substitutes for all these materials; plastics and resins in extruded form for use in manufacture; packing, stopping, and insulating materials; flexible pipes, tubes, and hoses, not of metal.

### Class 18 — Leather Goods

**Heading:** Leather and imitations of leather; animal skins and hides; luggage and carrying bags; umbrellas and parasols; walking sticks; whips, harness, and saddlery; collars, leashes, and clothing for animals.

**Common items:** Handbags, wallets, backpacks, suitcases, briefcases, purses, trunks, umbrellas, pet leashes/collars.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 25 (clothing), Class 22 (bags of textile).

### Class 19 — Non-Metallic Building Materials

**Heading:** Materials, not of metal, for building and construction; rigid pipes, not of metal, for building; asphalt, pitch, tar, and bitumen; transportable buildings, not of metal; monuments, not of metal.

### Class 20 — Furniture

**Heading:** Furniture, mirrors, picture frames; containers, not of metal, for storage or transport; unworked or semi-worked bone, horn, whalebone, or mother-of-pearl; shells; meerschaum; yellow amber.

**Common items:** Tables, chairs, desks, beds, mattresses, shelves, picture frames, plastic containers, coat hangers, pillows.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 6 (metal furniture), Class 21 (household containers).

### Class 21 — Housewares and Glass

**Heading:** Household or kitchen utensils and containers; cookware and tableware, except forks, knives, and spoons; combs and sponges; brushes, except paintbrushes; brush-making materials; articles for cleaning purposes; unworked or semi-worked glass, except building glass; glassware, porcelain, and earthenware.

**Common items:** Pots, pans, plates, cups, mugs, water bottles, brooms, toothbrushes, vases, decorative figurines.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 8 (cutlery — knives/forks/spoons), Class 11 (cooking apparatus).

### Class 22 — Cordage and Fibers

**Heading:** Ropes and string; nets; tents and tarpaulins; awnings of textile or synthetic materials; sails; sacks for the transport and storage of materials in bulk; padding, cushioning, and stuffing materials, except of paper, cardboard, rubber, or plastics; raw fibrous textile materials and substitutes therefor.

### Class 23 — Yarns and Threads

**Heading:** Yarns and threads for textile use.

### Class 24 — Fabrics

**Heading:** Textiles and substitutes for textiles; household linen; curtains of textile or plastic.

**Common items:** Bed sheets, blankets, towels, tablecloths, curtains, fabric (by the yard).

**Commonly confused with:** Class 25 (clothing textiles), Class 20 (pillows — if stuffed).

### Class 25 — Clothing

**Heading:** Clothing, footwear, headwear.

**Common items:** T-shirts, pants, dresses, shoes, boots, hats, scarves, gloves (clothing), socks, underwear, athletic wear.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 18 (leather accessories), Class 10 (orthopedic footwear), Class 28 (protective sports equipment).

**Critical note:** One of the most commonly filed classes. Specimens must show the mark used as a source identifier (typically on a label or tag), NOT merely as ornamentation.

### Class 26 — Fancy Goods

**Heading:** Lace, braid, and embroidery, and haberdashery ribbons and bows; buttons, hooks and eyes, pins, and needles; artificial flowers; hair decorations; false hair.

### Class 27 — Floor Coverings

**Heading:** Carpets, rugs, mats, and matting, linoleum and other materials for covering existing floors; wall hangings, not of textile.

### Class 28 — Toys and Sporting Goods

**Heading:** Games, toys, and playthings; video game apparatus; gymnastic and sporting articles; decorations for Christmas trees.

**Common items:** Board games, video game consoles, action figures, dolls, playing cards, exercise equipment, golf clubs, fishing tackle.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 9 (video game software — downloadable), Class 41 (entertainment/gaming services).

### Class 29 — Meats and Processed Foods

**Heading:** Meat, fish, poultry, and game; meat extracts; preserved, frozen, dried, and cooked fruits and vegetables; jellies, jams, compotes; eggs; milk, cheese, butter, yoghurt, and other milk products; oils and fats for food.

### Class 30 — Staple Foods

**Heading:** Coffee, tea, cocoa, and artificial coffee; rice, pasta, and noodles; tapioca and sago; flour and preparations made from cereals; bread, pastries, and confectionery; chocolate; ice cream, sorbets, and other edible ices; sugar, honey, treacle; yeast, baking powder; salt, seasonings, spices, preserved herbs; vinegar, sauces, and other condiments; ice for refreshment.

### Class 31 — Natural Agricultural Products

**Heading:** Raw and unprocessed agricultural, aquacultural, horticultural, and forestry products; raw and unprocessed grains and seeds; fresh fruits and vegetables, fresh herbs; natural plants and flowers; bulbs, seedlings, and seeds for planting; live animals; foodstuffs and beverages for animals; malt.

### Class 32 — Light Beverages

**Heading:** Beers; non-alcoholic beverages; mineral and aerated waters; fruit beverages and fruit juices; syrups and other non-alcoholic preparations for making beverages.

**Common items:** Beer, soft drinks, energy drinks, water (bottled), juice, kombucha, non-alcoholic cocktails.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 33 (alcoholic beverages — beer is Class 32, not 33), Class 30 (coffee, tea).

### Class 33 — Wines and Spirits

**Heading:** Alcoholic beverages, except beers; alcoholic preparations for making beverages.

**Critical note:** Beer = Class 32. Wine, spirits, liqueurs, hard seltzers (with alcohol) = Class 33.

### Class 34 — Smokers' Articles

**Heading:** Tobacco and tobacco substitutes; cigarettes, cigars, and cigarillos; electronic cigarettes and oral vaporizers for smokers; smokers' articles; matches.

## Service Classes (35–45)

### Class 35 — Advertising and Business

**Heading:** Advertising; business management, organization, and administration; office functions.

**Common items:** Retail store services, online retail, marketing/advertising services, business consulting, data processing, import/export agencies, franchising services, employment agencies, customer loyalty programs.

**Critical note:** Online retail of goods = Class 35. This does NOT replace classification of the goods themselves.

### Class 36 — Insurance and Financial

**Heading:** Insurance; financial affairs; monetary affairs; real estate affairs.

**Common items:** Banking, investment services, insurance brokerage, real estate, financial consulting, cryptocurrency exchange services, payment processing.

### Class 37 — Building Construction and Repair

**Heading:** Construction services; installation and repair services; mining extraction, oil and gas drilling.

**Common items:** Building construction, plumbing, electrical installation, vehicle repair, computer hardware repair, cleaning of buildings.

### Class 38 — Telecommunications

**Heading:** Telecommunications services.

**Common items:** ISP services, broadcasting, streaming (transmission), video conferencing, telephone services, messaging services.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 9 (telecom equipment), Class 42 (hosting/SaaS).

### Class 39 — Transportation and Storage

**Heading:** Transport; packaging and storage of goods; travel arrangement.

**Common items:** Shipping, freight, courier services, travel agencies, warehousing, parking services, ride-sharing.

### Class 40 — Treatment of Materials

**Heading:** Treatment of materials; recycling of waste and trash; air purification and treatment of water; printing services; food and drink preservation.

**Common items:** Custom manufacturing, printing, food processing, textile treatment, 3D printing services.

### Class 41 — Education and Entertainment

**Heading:** Education; providing of training; entertainment; sporting and cultural activities.

**Common items:** Schools, tutoring, online courses, publishing, live performances, movie production, fitness training, video game services (online), conferences, museums.

**Commonly confused with:** Class 9 (downloadable educational content), Class 42 (research services).

### Class 42 — Computer and Scientific Services

**Heading:** Scientific and technological services and research and design relating thereto; industrial analysis, industrial research, and industrial design services; quality control and authentication services; design and development of computer hardware and software.

**Common items:** SaaS, cloud computing, web hosting, software development, IT consulting, cybersecurity, UX/UI design, scientific research, engineering services.

**Critical note:** SaaS = Class 42. Downloadable software = Class 9. Many tech companies need BOTH.

### Class 43 — Hotels and Restaurants

**Heading:** Services for providing food and drink; temporary accommodation.

**Common items:** Restaurants, bars, cafes, catering, hotels, motels, bed and breakfasts, food trucks.

### Class 44 — Medical, Beauty, and Agricultural

**Heading:** Medical services; veterinary services; hygienic and beauty care for human beings or animals; agriculture, aquaculture, horticulture, and forestry services.

**Common items:** Hospitals, medical clinics, dentistry, pharmacy services, beauty salons, landscaping, veterinary care.

### Class 45 — Legal and Personal

**Heading:** Legal services; security services for the physical protection of tangible property and individuals; personal and social services rendered by others to meet the needs of individuals.

**Common items:** Legal services, private investigation, security guard services, online social networking, dating services, funeral services, personal shopping, astrology.

## Multi-Class Application Strategy

### When to File Multi-Class

- The applicant uses (or intends to use) the mark across multiple categories of goods/services.
- Example: A tech company selling downloadable software (Class 9), providing SaaS (Class 42), and offering training (Class 41) should file in all three classes.

### Cost Considerations

- Each additional class adds $250 (TEAS Plus) or $350 (TEAS Standard) to the filing fee.
- A specimen is required per class (for use-based filings).
- Maintenance fees (§8, §9) are also per class.

### Strategic Considerations

1. **Cover current use**: File in every class where the mark is currently used.
2. **Anticipate expansion**: Use §1(b) (intent-to-use) for classes where use is planned within 3 years.
3. **Avoid over-filing**: Filing in classes where the applicant has no genuine intent to use may result in a fraudulent application or vulnerability to cancellation.
4. **Coordinate related classes**: Goods and services that travel together in commerce should be covered (e.g., Class 25 clothing + Class 35 retail store services featuring clothing).

## Coordination Groups

Coordination groups are sets of classes that the USPTO examines together for likelihood of confusion because consumers commonly encounter the same or similar marks across these classes.

Key coordination groups:
- **Food/Beverage**: Classes 29, 30, 31, 32, 33, 43
- **Clothing/Accessories**: Classes 14, 18, 25, 26
- **Technology**: Classes 9, 35, 38, 41, 42
- **Health/Medical**: Classes 3, 5, 10, 44
- **Construction/Materials**: Classes 6, 17, 19, 37
