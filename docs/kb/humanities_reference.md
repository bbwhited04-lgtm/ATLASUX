## 1. History

### Periodization
| Era | Dates | Key Events |
|---|---|---|
| Ancient Near East | ~3500–539 BCE | Sumer, Akkad, Babylon, Assyria, Persia |
| Classical Greece | ~800–146 BCE | Polis, Persian Wars, Peloponnesian War, Alexander |
| Roman Republic/Empire | 509 BCE–476 CE | Punic Wars, Julius Caesar, Pax Romana, fall of West |
| Early Medieval | 476–1000 | Charlemagne, Viking Age, Byzantine continuity |
| High Medieval | 1000–1300 | Crusades, Magna Carta (1215), Scholasticism |
| Late Medieval | 1300–1500 | Black Death (1347), Hundred Years' War, fall of Constantinople (1453) |
| Early Modern | 1500–1800 | Reformation, Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment |
| Modern | 1800–1945 | Industrial Revolution, World Wars |
| Contemporary | 1945–present | Cold War, decolonization, globalization |

### Key Treaties & Documents
- **Treaty of Westphalia (1648)**: Ended Thirty Years' War, established state sovereignty
- **Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)**: Spain-Portugal division of New World
- **Treaty of Versailles (1919)**: Ended WWI, imposed reparations on Germany
- **Congress of Vienna (1814-15)**: Post-Napoleonic European order, balance of power
- **Magna Carta (1215)**: Limited English royal power, due process origins
- **Peace of Augsburg (1555)**: cuius regio, eius religio (ruler determines religion)

### Major Revolutions
- **English Civil War (1642-51)**: Parliamentary vs Royalist, Cromwell's Commonwealth
- **American Revolution (1775-83)**: Colonial independence, Constitution (1787)
- **French Revolution (1789-99)**: Estates General → National Assembly → Terror → Napoleon
- **Haitian Revolution (1791-1804)**: Only successful slave revolt → independent nation
- **Russian Revolution (1917)**: February (abdication) → October (Bolshevik seizure)
- **Chinese Revolution (1949)**: CCP victory, Mao Zedong, People's Republic

### Historiographic Concepts
- **Primary vs secondary sources**: Contemporary documents vs later analyses
- **Annales School**: Longue durée, mentalities, social/economic structures
- **Whig history**: Progressive narrative fallacy
- **Presentism**: Judging the past by present standards
- **Periodization debate**: Arbitrary boundaries, Eurocentric bias

## 2. Philosophy

### Major Branches
- **Metaphysics**: Nature of reality, existence, identity, causation
- **Epistemology**: Knowledge, justification, belief, skepticism
- **Ethics**: Right/wrong, virtue, duty, consequences
- **Logic**: Valid reasoning, formal systems
- **Aesthetics**: Beauty, art, taste
- **Political philosophy**: Justice, rights, authority, liberty

### Epistemology
- **Justified True Belief (JTB)**: S knows P iff S believes P, P is true, S is justified
- **Gettier problem (1963)**: JTB is insufficient — cases of justified true belief that aren't knowledge
- **Empiricism**: Knowledge from sensory experience (Locke, Hume, Berkeley)
- **Rationalism**: Knowledge from reason alone (Descartes, Leibniz, Spinoza)
- **Kant's synthesis**: A priori synthetic judgments, categories of understanding
- **Skepticism**: Cartesian doubt, brain-in-a-vat, Pyrrhonian suspension

### Ethics
- **Consequentialism**: Morality determined by outcomes
  - **Utilitarianism**: Greatest good for greatest number (Bentham, Mill)
  - **Act vs Rule utilitarianism**: Evaluate individual acts vs general rules
- **Deontology**: Morality from duty/rules regardless of consequences
  - **Kant**: Categorical imperative — act only on maxims universalizable as law
  - **Prima facie duties** (Ross): Non-absolute duties that can be overridden
- **Virtue ethics**: Character traits, eudaimonia (Aristotle)
- **Social contract**: Hobbes (Leviathan), Locke (natural rights), Rousseau (general will)
- **Rawls**: Veil of ignorance → justice as fairness, difference principle

### Logic
- **Propositional**: AND (∧), OR (∨), NOT (¬), IF-THEN (→), IFF (↔)
- **Predicate**: Quantifiers ∀ (for all), ∃ (there exists)
- **Modus ponens**: P, P→Q ⊢ Q
- **Modus tollens**: ¬Q, P→Q ⊢ ¬P
- **Disjunctive syllogism**: P∨Q, ¬P ⊢ Q
- **Reductio ad absurdum**: Assume ¬P, derive contradiction, conclude P

### Common Fallacies
- **Ad hominem**: Attacking the person, not the argument
- **Straw man**: Misrepresenting the opponent's position
- **Begging the question**: Assuming what you're trying to prove
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc**: Correlation ≠ causation
- **Appeal to authority**: Expert opinion ≠ proof
- **False dilemma**: Presenting only two options when more exist
- **Equivocation**: Using a word with different meanings in same argument

## 3. Economics

### Microeconomics
- **Supply and demand**: P* where S(p) = D(p), equilibrium
- **Elasticity**: E = %ΔQ/%ΔP, elastic |E|>1, inelastic |E|<1
- **Consumer surplus**: Area below demand curve, above price
- **Producer surplus**: Area above supply curve, below price
- **Marginal analysis**: Optimal at MC = MR (marginal cost = marginal revenue)
- **Diminishing returns**: Additional input yields less additional output

### Market Structures
| Structure | Firms | Product | Entry | Price Power |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Perfect competition | Many | Identical | Free | None (price taker) |
| Monopolistic competition | Many | Differentiated | Free | Some |
| Oligopoly | Few | Any | Barriers | Significant |
| Monopoly | One | Unique | Blocked | Full |

### Macroeconomics
- **GDP**: C + I + G + (X - M), consumption + investment + govt + net exports
- **Fiscal policy**: Government spending and taxation
- **Monetary policy**: Central bank controls money supply, interest rates
- **Phillips curve**: Inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment (short-run)
- **IS-LM model**: Goods market (IS) and money market (LM) equilibrium
- **Quantity theory of money**: MV = PY (money × velocity = price × output)

### Key Economic Concepts
- **Comparative advantage (Ricardo)**: Trade benefits even when one party is absolutely better at everything
- **Opportunity cost**: Value of next-best alternative forgone
- **Externalities**: Costs/benefits to third parties (positive: education, negative: pollution)
- **Moral hazard**: Risk-taking increases when insured against consequences
- **Adverse selection**: Information asymmetry in markets (Akerlof's lemons)
- **Nash equilibrium**: No player can improve by unilateral strategy change
- **Pareto efficiency**: No reallocation can improve one person without harming another

### Game Theory
- **Prisoner's dilemma**: Dominant strategy leads to suboptimal outcome
- **Nash equilibrium**: Each player's strategy is best response to others
- **Zero-sum**: One player's gain = another's loss
- **Coordination games**: Multiple equilibria, need focal point
- **Mechanism design**: Design rules to achieve desired outcomes (reverse game theory)

## 4. Political Science

### Political Theory
- **Sovereignty**: Supreme authority within a territory (Bodin, Hobbes)
- **Legitimacy**: Right to rule — traditional, charismatic, rational-legal (Weber)
- **Democracy**: Direct vs representative, liberal vs illiberal
- **Authoritarianism**: Concentrated power, limited pluralism
- **Totalitarianism**: State controls all aspects of life (Arendt)

### International Relations Theories
- **Realism**: States are primary actors, power and security drive behavior (Morgenthau, Waltz)
- **Liberalism**: Institutions, trade, and democracy promote peace (Kant, Keohane)
- **Constructivism**: Identities and norms shape state behavior (Wendt)
- **Democratic peace theory**: Democracies rarely fight each other

### Key Concepts
- **Balance of power**: States ally to prevent hegemony
- **Security dilemma**: One state's defensive buildup threatens others
- **Collective action problem**: Individual incentives undermine group goals
- **Soft power vs hard power**: Attraction vs coercion (Nye)

## 5. Psychology

### Major Perspectives
- **Behaviorism**: Observable behavior, conditioning (Pavlov, Skinner, Watson)
- **Cognitive**: Mental processes, schemas, information processing
- **Psychoanalytic**: Unconscious drives, id/ego/superego (Freud)
- **Humanistic**: Self-actualization, hierarchy of needs (Maslow, Rogers)
- **Evolutionary**: Adaptive behaviors, fitness-enhancing traits

### Key Concepts
- **Classical conditioning (Pavlov)**: NS + UCS → NS becomes CS
- **Operant conditioning (Skinner)**: Reinforcement/punishment shapes behavior
- **Cognitive dissonance (Festinger)**: Discomfort from conflicting beliefs → attitude change
- **Milgram experiment**: Obedience to authority (65% delivered max shock)
- **Stanford prison experiment (Zimbardo)**: Situational factors override personality
- **Bystander effect**: Diffusion of responsibility in groups
- **Fundamental attribution error**: Overattribute behavior to disposition vs situation

### Developmental Psychology
- **Piaget's stages**: Sensorimotor → Preoperational → Concrete operational → Formal operational
- **Erikson's stages**: 8 psychosocial stages (trust vs mistrust → integrity vs despair)
- **Kohlberg's moral development**: Preconventional → Conventional → Postconventional
- **Attachment theory (Bowlby/Ainsworth)**: Secure, anxious-ambivalent, avoidant, disorganized
- **Vygotsky**: Zone of proximal development, social learning, scaffolding

### Research Methods
- **Experimental**: Random assignment, IV → DV, control group
- **Correlational**: Association, r = -1 to +1, no causation
- **Effect size**: Cohen's d (small 0.2, medium 0.5, large 0.8)
- **p-value**: Probability of result given null hypothesis is true
- **Type I error**: False positive (reject true null)
- **Type II error**: False negative (fail to reject false null)

## 6. Sociology

### Founding Figures
- **Durkheim**: Social facts, anomie, mechanical vs organic solidarity, suicide study
- **Weber**: Verstehen (understanding), ideal types, Protestant ethic, bureaucracy
- **Marx**: Historical materialism, class conflict, base/superstructure, alienation

### Key Concepts
- **Social stratification**: Class, caste, estate systems
- **Social mobility**: Intergenerational vs intragenerational, absolute vs relative
- **Socialization**: Primary (family), secondary (school, peers, media)
- **Deviance**: Violation of social norms (Merton's strain theory: conformity, innovation, ritualism, retreatism, rebellion)
- **Symbolic interactionism**: Meaning through social interaction (Mead, Blumer)
- **Structural functionalism**: Society as system of interrelated parts (Parsons)
- **Conflict theory**: Competition for resources drives social change (Marx, Dahrendorf)

## 7. Law

### Legal Systems
- **Common law**: Case law, precedent (stare decisis) — UK, US, Australia
- **Civil law**: Codified statutes — France, Germany, Japan
- **Religious law**: Sharia, Halakha, Canon law
- **Customary law**: Traditional practices, indigenous systems

### Key Legal Concepts
- **Habeas corpus**: Right to challenge unlawful detention
- **Due process**: Fair procedures before deprivation of life, liberty, property
- **Burden of proof**: Beyond reasonable doubt (criminal), preponderance of evidence (civil)
- **Mens rea**: Guilty mind (intent, knowledge, recklessness, negligence)
- **Actus reus**: Guilty act (physical element of crime)
- **Stare decisis**: Let the decision stand — follow precedent
- **Judicial review**: Courts can invalidate unconstitutional laws (Marbury v. Madison, 1803)

### Major World Religions (by adherents)
| Religion | Founded | Key Text | Core Concept |
|---|---|---|---|
| Christianity | ~30 CE | Bible | Salvation through Christ |
| Islam | 610 CE | Quran | Submission to Allah, Five Pillars |
| Hinduism | ~1500 BCE | Vedas, Upanishads | Dharma, karma, moksha |
| Buddhism | ~5th c. BCE | Tripitaka | Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path |

### Key Concepts
- **Theodicy**: Problem of evil — why does suffering exist if God is omnipotent and good?
- **Eschatology**: Study of end times, afterlife, final judgment
- **Soteriology**: Study of salvation, liberation, enlightenment
- **Comparative religion**: Phenomenological approach vs theological approach
- **Secularization thesis**: Modernization leads to decline of religion (contested)
