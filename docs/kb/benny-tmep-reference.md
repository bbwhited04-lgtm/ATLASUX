# TMEP Reference — Trademark Manual of Examining Procedure

## Purpose

This document provides Benny (IP Counsel agent) with a comprehensive, actionable reference to the Trademark Manual of Examining Procedure (TMEP). The TMEP is the USPTO's internal guide for trademark examining attorneys and serves as the authoritative procedural resource for prosecution of trademark applications.

---

## Chapter 700 — Examination of the Application

### 700 Overview

Chapter 700 governs how examining attorneys review trademark applications upon initial receipt and throughout prosecution.

### 710 — Assignment and First Action

- Applications are assigned to examining attorneys within the relevant technology center based on international class and goods/services type.
- The examining attorney must issue a first Office action within **approximately 3 months** of the filing date (or **6–7 months** if there is a backlog in that technology center). TMEP §710.
- First actions must address **all substantive and procedural issues** identified; piecemeal refusals are disfavored.

### 711 — Priority Action

- Certain matters receive expedited examination: petitions to the Director, letters of protest, concurrent use proceedings.

### 714 — Completeness of the Record

- The examining attorney must ensure the application contains: (1) the mark, (2) the applicant's name/entity type, (3) a valid basis, (4) a listing of goods/services, (5) the required filing fee, (6) a verified statement/declaration.
- Missing elements trigger a deficiency notice rather than a substantive refusal. TMEP §714.

### 718 — Search of USPTO Records

- Examining attorneys must search the **USPTO database (X-Search)** for conflicting marks.
- Searches cover live registrations and pending applications.
- The search must include: exact matches, phonetic equivalents, design search codes (for design marks), and translations of foreign-language terms. TMEP §718.01–718.04.

### 719 — Examination of the Mark

- The examining attorney evaluates the mark against all statutory bars under 15 U.S.C. §1052 (Lanham Act §2).
- Key areas: likelihood of confusion (§2(d)), descriptiveness (§2(e)(1)), geographic descriptiveness (§2(e)(2)), surname (§2(e)(4)), functionality (§2(e)(5)), deceptiveness (§2(a)), scandalous/immoral matter (§2(a)).

## Chapter 800 — Amendments

### 806 — Amendment of the Mark

- Amendments to the mark drawing are permitted only if they do **not materially alter** the commercial impression of the mark. TMEP §807.14.
- Material alteration standard: Would the average consumer view the amended mark as a substantially different mark?
- Permissible: minor spelling corrections, removal of generic matter, addition of a standard character claim.
- Impermissible: adding design elements, changing dominant literal elements, altering the fundamental character.

### 808 — Amendment of the Identification of Goods/Services

- Goods/services may be **narrowed or clarified** but never **broadened** beyond the scope of the original filing. TMEP §808.02.
- Acceptable narrowing: "Clothing" → "T-shirts and hats."
- Unacceptable broadening: "T-shirts" → "Clothing, namely, t-shirts, hats, and jackets."

### 810 — Amendment of Basis

- Applicants may change or add a filing basis at any time before registration. TMEP §810.
- Common change: §1(b) intent-to-use → §1(a) use in commerce (by filing a Statement of Use with a specimen).
- Adding a §44(e) basis requires a certified copy of the foreign registration.

### 812 — Amendment of Applicant Information

- Name changes, entity type corrections, and address updates are generally permitted.
- Assignment of the application to a new owner requires a separate recordation at the Assignment Recordation Branch.

## Chapter 900 — Marks with Special Characteristics

### 902 — Color Marks

- Color claimed as a feature of the mark must be described in the description of the mark.
- Color per se marks (color alone, no design) require proof of acquired distinctiveness under §2(f). TMEP §1202.05(a).

### 903 — Sound Marks

- Must be represented by a detailed written description of the sound.
- An audio file (MP3 or WAV) must accompany the application.
- Sound marks are never inherently distinctive for the goods/services themselves; acquired distinctiveness is typically required unless the sound is arbitrary in context.

### 904 — Scent Marks

- Extremely difficult to register. Must be non-functional.
- Require clear evidence of acquired distinctiveness.
- Must be described in words since graphic representation is impossible.

### 906 — Trade Dress

- Product design trade dress is **never** inherently distinctive; always requires §2(f) evidence. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Samara Bros., Inc., 529 U.S. 205 (2000).
- Product packaging trade dress **may** be inherently distinctive under the Seabrook test.

### 907 — Motion Marks

- Require a description of the motion and a series of still images showing key frames.
- May also submit a video file.

## Chapter 1200 — Substantive Examination of the Application

### 1201 — Specimens of Use

- Specimens must show the mark as actually used in commerce in connection with the identified goods/services.
- **Goods specimens** (§1(a) or SOU): tags, labels, packaging, product displays, screenshots of website showing the mark associated with the goods and a means to order/purchase. TMEP §904.03.
- **Service specimens**: advertising, website pages showing the mark used to promote/render the services, business signage, promotional materials. TMEP §1301.04.
- **Digitally altered or mockup specimens** are unacceptable. TMEP §904.04(a).
- One specimen per class is required.

### 1202 — Use as a Mark

- The mark must function as a source identifier, not merely as ornamentation, informational matter, or a common phrase.
- Ornamental use: If a design or phrase is used primarily as decoration (e.g., large graphic on the front of a t-shirt), it does not function as a trademark. TMEP §1202.03.

### 1207 — Likelihood of Confusion — §2(d)

- This is the most common substantive refusal.
- The examining attorney applies the **du Pont factors** (In re E. I. du Pont de Nemours & Co., 476 F.2d 1357 (CCPA 1973)).
- Key factors: (1) similarity of marks, (2) similarity of goods/services, (3) similarity of trade channels, (4) conditions of purchase, (5) fame of prior mark, (6) number and nature of similar marks in use.
- The test is whether an ordinary consumer would be likely to be confused as to the source, sponsorship, or affiliation. TMEP §1207.01.
- **Practical note**: Marks do not need to be identical; similar marks for related goods suffice for a refusal.

### 1209 — Descriptiveness — §2(e)(1)

- A mark is "merely descriptive" if it immediately conveys information about a quality, feature, function, or characteristic of the goods/services.
- Test: Does the mark immediately convey knowledge of a quality or characteristic? In re Gyulay, 820 F.2d 1216 (Fed. Cir. 1987).
- Descriptive marks may be registered on the Supplemental Register or with a §2(f) claim.

### 1210 — Geographic Descriptiveness — §2(e)(2)

- Primarily geographically descriptive: The mark identifies a geographic location, the goods/services originate from that location, and consumers would believe they originate there.
- Primarily geographically deceptively misdescriptive (§2(e)(3)): Same test but the goods do NOT originate from the named location. This is a **permanent bar** — no §2(f) claim available.

### 1211 — Surname Marks — §2(e)(4)

- If the primary significance of the mark to the purchasing public is a surname, registration is refused.
- Factors: (1) whether the surname is rare or common, (2) whether anyone connected with the applicant has that surname, (3) whether the mark has any recognized meaning other than as a surname, (4) whether it has the structure and pronunciation of a surname, (5) whether any stylization would alter the surname significance.

### 1301 — Non-Substantive Refusals

- Failure to function as a mark (informational matter, ornamentation, common phrases). TMEP §1202.
- Unacceptable specimen of use.
- Identification of goods/services indefinite or overly broad.

## Chapter 1400 — Actions After Examination

### 1401 — Approval for Publication

- If no refusal is maintained and all requirements are met, the examining attorney approves the mark for publication in the Official Gazette.
- Publication period: **30 days** for opposition. Extensions of time to oppose can extend this to up to **180 days**.

### 1402 — Notice of Allowance (Intent-to-Use Applications)

- §1(b) applications receive a Notice of Allowance instead of direct registration.
- Applicant must file a **Statement of Use (SOU)** within **6 months** of the NOA date.
- Up to **5 extensions** of time to file the SOU are available (6 months each), for a total of **36 months** from the NOA. TMEP §1108.03.
- Each extension requires a showing of good cause and payment of the applicable fee.

### 1403 — Post-Registration Requirements

- **Section 8 Affidavit**: Declaration of continued use, due between the **5th and 6th year** after registration. TMEP §1604.
- **Section 9 Renewal**: Filed every **10 years** from the registration date. TMEP §1606.
- **Section 15 Declaration of Incontestability**: Filed after **5 consecutive years** of use after registration. TMEP §1605.

### 1404 — Suspension and Abandonment

- If an applicant fails to respond to an Office action within **3 months** (for non-final actions) or **6 months** (for final actions), the application is **abandoned**.
- Abandoned applications may be revived by petition showing unintentional delay. The petition must be filed within **2 months** of the date of abandonment (or actual notice). TMEP §1712.

## Chapter 1500 — Disclaimers

### 1504 — Compulsory Disclaimers

- If a composite mark contains unregistrable matter (descriptive or generic terms), the examining attorney may require a **disclaimer** of that matter. TMEP §1213.
- A disclaimer does not remove the term from the mark; it simply acknowledges no exclusive right to that element apart from the composite mark.
- Example: For the mark "ATLAS UX SOFTWARE," a disclaimer of "SOFTWARE" might be required because it is generic for software products.

### 1505 — When Disclaimers Are Not Required

- If the unregistrable element is so insignificant in the overall mark that it need not be disclaimed.
- If the term has acquired distinctiveness and the applicant provides §2(f) evidence.

## Practical Examination Guidance for Benny

### When Reviewing a Proposed Mark

1. **Run the distinctiveness spectrum analysis** — Is the mark generic, descriptive, suggestive, arbitrary, or fanciful for the stated goods/services?
2. **Search for conflicts** — Apply the du Pont factors to any similar prior marks.
3. **Check the identification** — Is the goods/services description clear, specific, and within the scope of the selected class(es)?
4. **Evaluate specimen eligibility** — Does the proposed specimen show trademark use (not mere ornamentation or informational use)?
5. **Assess disclaimer needs** — Does the composite mark contain generic or descriptive elements that require disclaimer?
6. **Flag special mark issues** — Color claims, sound, motion, trade dress, or non-traditional marks trigger additional requirements.

### Common Examiner Mistakes to Watch For

- Failing to consider the mark in its entirety (focusing on one element only).
- Applying an overly broad or narrow construction to the identification of goods/services.
- Not accounting for the fallacy of predetermining likely confusion by rearranging the du Pont factors.
- Requiring disclaimers for terms that have acquired distinctiveness.
