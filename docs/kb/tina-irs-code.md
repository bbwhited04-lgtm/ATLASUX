# Tina KB: Key IRS Code Sections for Business Operations

## Purpose

This document provides Tina (Treasurer/CFO agent) with practical, actionable knowledge of the most impactful Internal Revenue Code sections for business operations. Each section includes what it does, who qualifies, current limits/thresholds, and how to apply it in practice.

---

## Section 199A — Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction

### What It Does

Allows owners of pass-through entities (sole proprietorships, partnerships, S-Corps, LLCs taxed as pass-throughs) to deduct up to 20% of their qualified business income from their personal tax return. This was enacted under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) and is currently set to expire after December 31, 2025, unless extended by Congress.

### Who Qualifies

- Individuals, trusts, and estates with income from a qualified trade or business
- The business must be operated through a pass-through entity (not a C-Corp)
- Specified service trades or businesses (SSTBs) — law, accounting, health, consulting, athletics, financial services, performing arts — face phase-out limits

### Key Limits and Thresholds (2025/2026)

- **Full deduction available** when taxable income is below $191,950 (single) / $383,900 (MFJ)
- **Phase-out range**: $191,950–$241,950 (single) / $383,900–$483,900 (MFJ)
- Above the phase-out, the deduction is limited to the greater of:
  - 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, OR
  - 25% of W-2 wages plus 2.5% of the unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition (UBIA) of qualified property
- SSTBs get zero deduction above the phase-out range
- REIT dividends and publicly traded partnership (PTP) income qualify separately (no wage/UBIA test)

### Practical Application

- For S-Corp owners near the threshold, increasing W-2 wages (reasonable compensation) can actually increase the 199A deduction
- Splitting businesses into SSTB and non-SSTB components may preserve deductions (but watch aggregation rules under Reg 1.199A-4)
- Rental real estate can qualify if it meets the safe harbor (250+ hours of rental services annually, maintained contemporaneous records)
- Always calculate whether C-Corp election would produce a lower overall tax burden than the 199A deduction

## Section 179 — Expensing of Depreciable Business Assets

### What It Does

Allows businesses to immediately deduct the full purchase price of qualifying equipment, software, and certain property improvements in the year placed in service, rather than depreciating over multiple years.

### Who Qualifies

- Any business that purchases, finances, or leases qualifying new or used equipment
- Must be used more than 50% for business purposes
- Qualifying property: machinery, equipment, off-the-shelf software, certain improvements to nonresidential real property (roofs, HVAC, fire protection, alarm/security systems)

### Key Limits and Thresholds (2025/2026)

- **Maximum deduction**: $1,250,000 (indexed for inflation; check IRS Rev. Proc. for exact 2026 figure)
- **Phase-out threshold**: begins when total equipment placed in service exceeds $3,130,000; deduction reduced dollar-for-dollar above this amount
- **Cannot create a loss** — limited to taxable income from all active trades or businesses
- SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR: limited to $30,500 for Section 179 (remaining cost eligible for bonus depreciation)
- Passenger vehicles subject to luxury auto limits under Section 280F

### Practical Application

- Ideal for small and mid-size businesses that want to front-load deductions
- Combine with bonus depreciation: use Section 179 first (to control the deduction amount), then apply bonus depreciation to remaining basis
- Carryforward is allowed if limited by taxable income — unused 179 carries forward indefinitely
- For businesses expecting higher income in future years, it may be strategic to skip 179 and use bonus depreciation instead

## Section 1031 — Like-Kind Exchanges

### What It Does

Allows deferral of capital gains tax when real property held for business or investment is exchanged for other real property of like kind. Post-TCJA, this applies only to real property (no longer personal property, equipment, vehicles, artwork, etc.).

### Who Qualifies

- Any taxpayer holding real property for productive use in a trade or business or for investment
- Property must be exchanged for like-kind property (broadly defined — commercial for residential, improved for unimproved, etc.)
- Primary residences and dealer property (held for sale) do not qualify

### Key Limits and Thresholds

- **No dollar limit** on the amount of gain that can be deferred
- **45-day identification period**: replacement property must be identified within 45 days of transferring relinquished property
- **180-day exchange period**: replacement must be received within 180 days (or tax return due date, whichever is earlier)
- **Three-property rule**: can identify up to 3 replacement properties regardless of value, OR any number if total FMV does not exceed 200% of relinquished property
- **Boot**: any cash or non-like-kind property received is taxable

### Practical Application

- Use Qualified Intermediaries (QIs) — never take constructive receipt of funds
- Reverse exchanges are allowed (acquire replacement before selling relinquished) but complex
- Improvement exchanges (building on replacement property during exchange period) possible through Exchange Accommodation Titleholders
- Track deferred gain and adjusted basis carefully for future dispositions
- State conformity varies — some states do not follow federal 1031 rules

## Section 401(k) — Retirement Plans

### What It Does

Establishes rules for employer-sponsored defined contribution retirement plans that allow employees to defer a portion of their salary on a pre-tax (traditional) or after-tax (Roth) basis.

### Key Limits (2025/2026)

- **Employee elective deferral**: $23,500 (2025); check for 2026 indexed amount
- **Catch-up contribution** (age 50+): $7,500
- **Super catch-up** (ages 60–63, effective 2025 under SECURE 2.0): $11,250
- **Total annual addition** (employee + employer): $70,000 (2025)
- **Compensation limit** for calculating contributions: $350,000 (2025)
- **Highly compensated employee (HCE) threshold**: $160,000 (2025)
- Roth 401(k) contributions: no income limit (unlike Roth IRA)
- Required minimum distributions: age 73 (SECURE 2.0); Roth 401(k) no longer subject to RMDs starting 2024

### Practical Application

- S-Corp owner-employees: maximize deferrals plus employer profit-sharing contributions
- Safe harbor 401(k) plans avoid ADP/ACP nondiscrimination testing — either 3% nonelective contribution or 4% match
- Solo 401(k) for self-employed individuals: both employee and employer contributions
- Consider auto-enrollment features (SECURE 2.0 requires it for plans established after 12/29/2022)
- Employer contributions are deductible under Section 404

## Section 162 — Trade or Business Expenses

### What It Does

The foundational provision allowing deduction of "ordinary and necessary" expenses paid or incurred in carrying on a trade or business. This is the primary authority for most business expense deductions.

### Key Requirements

- **Ordinary**: common and accepted in the industry
- **Necessary**: helpful and appropriate (not indispensable)
- Must be **directly connected** to the trade or business
- Must be **paid or incurred** during the taxable year (cash vs. accrual method)
- Cannot be a capital expenditure (must expense, not capitalize — see Section 263)

### Common Deductible Items

- Salaries and wages (must be reasonable — especially for related parties)
- Rent, utilities, insurance, office supplies
- Professional services (legal, accounting, consulting)
- Travel expenses (away from tax home overnight)
- Business meals: 50% deductible (the 100% restaurant meal deduction expired after 2022)
- Marketing and advertising
- Education that maintains or improves skills in current business

### What Is NOT Deductible Under 162

- Fines and penalties (Section 162(f)) — no deduction for government fines
- Lobbying expenses (Section 162(e))
- Bribes and kickbacks
- Club dues (country clubs, social clubs)
- Commuting expenses (home to regular place of business)
- Entertainment expenses (post-TCJA — no deduction)

### Practical Application

- Document the business purpose of every expense
- Substantiation requirements under Section 274: receipts for expenses over $75, contemporaneous records for travel/meals
- Reasonable compensation for owner-employees is critical for S-Corps (IRS scrutinizes low salaries)
- Startup costs before business begins are capitalized under Section 195 (first $5,000 deductible, phased out above $50,000, remainder amortized over 180 months)

## Section 263A — Uniform Capitalization Rules (UNICAP)

### What It Does

Requires certain direct and indirect costs to be capitalized into inventory or self-constructed assets rather than expensed currently. Applies to manufacturers, retailers, and other businesses that produce or acquire property for resale.

### Who Must Comply

- Manufacturers and producers (any business that produces real or tangible personal property)
- Retailers and wholesalers with average annual gross receipts exceeding $29 million (3-year average, indexed)
- Self-constructed assets (buildings, machinery built for own use)
- Exemption: businesses with average gross receipts of $29 million or less (small business exception under TCJA)

### Costs That Must Be Capitalized

- Direct materials and direct labor
- Indirect costs: factory overhead, rent, depreciation on production equipment, utilities, quality control, purchasing, warehousing, officers' compensation allocable to production
- Interest on debt-financed production (Section 263A(f))

### Practical Application

- Use the simplified production method or simplified resale method for easier compliance
- The Section 471(c) small business inventory method (for businesses under the gross receipts threshold) is an alternative that avoids UNICAP entirely
- Changing to or from UNICAP requires Form 3115 (change in accounting method)
- UNICAP adjustments are a common IRS audit target — maintain detailed cost allocation workpapers

## Section 1202 — Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS)

### What It Does

Excludes up to 100% of gain on the sale of qualified small business stock from federal income tax, with a per-issuer exclusion of the greater of $10 million or 10x the adjusted basis.

### Who Qualifies

- Stock must be in a domestic C-Corporation
- Corporation's aggregate gross assets must not exceed $50 million at the time of issuance (and immediately after)
- Stock must be acquired at original issuance (not secondary market) for money, property, or services
- Must be held for more than 5 years
- Corporation must be an active business (at least 80% of assets used in qualified trade or business)
- Excluded industries: professional services (law, accounting, health, engineering, consulting), banking, insurance, farming, mining, hospitality (hotels, restaurants)

### Key Benefits

- **100% exclusion** for stock acquired after September 27, 2010
- Excluded gain is also exempt from the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax
- **Per-issuer limit**: greater of $10 million or 10x adjusted basis per taxpayer per issuer
- Can be stacked across multiple issuers
- Section 1045 allows tax-free rollover of QSBS gain into new QSBS (within 60 days) if held less than 5 years

### Practical Application

- Critical for startup founders and early employees — plan entity structure as C-Corp from inception if QSBS is a goal
- Converting from LLC to C-Corp may preserve QSBS eligibility if done correctly
- Track the $50 million gross asset test carefully — contributions to capital, retained earnings, and assets from stock issuance all count
- Gift and inheritance rules: QSBS eligibility can transfer, but the holding period tacks for gifts (not inheritance)
- State conformity varies significantly — California does not conform to QSBS exclusion

## Section 469 — Passive Activity Loss Rules

### What It Does

Limits the ability of individuals, trusts, estates, and personal service corporations to deduct losses from passive activities against non-passive income (wages, portfolio income, active business income).

### Key Rules

- **Passive activity**: trade or business in which the taxpayer does not materially participate
- **Material participation** tests (7 tests under Reg 1.469-5T): most common are 500+ hours or substantially all participation
- Rental activities are **per se passive** (with exceptions)
- Suspended passive losses carry forward and are released upon complete disposition of the activity

### Special Rules

- **Real estate professional exception**: if more than 50% of personal services and 750+ hours in real property trades or businesses, rental activities are not automatically passive
- **$25,000 rental loss allowance**: active participation in rental real estate allows up to $25,000 of losses against non-passive income; phases out between $100,000–$150,000 AGI
- Grouping elections under Reg 1.469-4 can combine activities to meet material participation

### Practical Application

- Track hours meticulously — contemporaneous logs are the best defense in an audit
- Grouping elections are irrevocable once made; plan carefully
- For real estate investors with significant rental losses, qualifying as a real estate professional can unlock substantial deductions
- Passive income generators (PIGs) can absorb suspended passive losses
- At-risk rules under Section 465 also apply and must be considered in tandem

## Section 280A — Home Office Deduction

### What It Does

Restricts deductions for business use of a dwelling unit (home office). Allows a deduction only when a portion of the home is used regularly and exclusively as the principal place of business or for meeting clients.

### Who Qualifies

- Self-employed individuals and independent contractors
- Employees: **no deduction** under TCJA (2018–2025) — the employee home office deduction was suspended
- The space must be used **regularly and exclusively** for business (no dual-use areas)
- Must be either the principal place of business, a place to meet clients, or a separate structure

### Methods

- **Regular method**: actual expenses (mortgage interest/rent, utilities, insurance, depreciation, repairs) prorated by square footage or room count
- **Simplified method**: $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft = maximum $1,500 deduction
- Home office deduction cannot create a business loss (limited to gross income from the business, with carryforward)

### Practical Application

- The simplified method is easier but often yields a smaller deduction
- Regular method requires allocating and tracking actual expenses
- Depreciation on the home office portion (regular method) triggers depreciation recapture upon sale, even if the deduction was not claimed
- S-Corp owners: the corporation can reimburse home office expenses under an accountable plan (not a personal deduction)
- Daycare facilities have a special exception to the exclusive-use rule

## Notes for Tina

- Always verify current-year thresholds — inflation adjustments are published annually in IRS Revenue Procedures (typically in the fall for the following year)
- When advising on any of these sections, consider the interplay between them (e.g., 179 + 199A wage calculations, 469 + 280A loss limitations)
- State conformity is never guaranteed — always flag potential state-level differences
- The TCJA provisions (199A, entertainment disallowance, employee home office suspension) are scheduled to sunset after 2025 unless extended by legislation; monitor closely
