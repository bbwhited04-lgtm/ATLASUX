# Tina KB: Treasury Regulations Reference

## Purpose

Treasury Regulations are the official interpretations of the Internal Revenue Code issued by the IRS and the Department of the Treasury. They carry significant legal weight and provide the detailed rules that practitioners must follow. This document covers the regulations most relevant to business operations, with practical compliance notes.

---

## Reg 1.199A — Qualified Business Income Deduction

### Overview

These regulations interpret Section 199A and provide the detailed mechanics for calculating the QBI deduction. They were finalized in January 2019 and span multiple subparts.

### Key Regulatory Provisions

**Reg 1.199A-1: Operational Rules**
- Defines qualified business income as net amount of qualified items of income, gain, deduction, and loss from a qualified trade or business
- QBI does not include: capital gains/losses, interest income not allocable to a trade or business, wage income, guaranteed payments for services under Section 707(c), reasonable compensation from S-Corp
- Loss netting rules: if one business has QBI loss, it offsets QBI from other businesses proportionally; net QBI loss carries forward

**Reg 1.199A-2: W-2 Wages and UBIA**
- W-2 wages: only wages properly allocable to QBI count; three methods for calculating (unmodified Box 1, modified Box 1, tracking wages to QBI)
- UBIA of qualified property: unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition; property must be held at close of tax year; depreciable period is the greater of 10 years or the MACRS recovery period
- Property acquired in like-kind exchanges or involuntary conversions has special UBIA tracking rules

**Reg 1.199A-3: QBI, Qualified REIT Dividends, and Qualified PTP Income**
- Items must be effectively connected with a US trade or business
- Section 751(a) gain (hot assets) in partnership sales is QBI
- Reasonable compensation and guaranteed payments are excluded from QBI

**Reg 1.199A-4: Aggregation**
- Taxpayers may aggregate multiple businesses if they share: common ownership (50%+), and at least two of three factors — shared facilities/resources, coordinated/shared employees, interdependent operations
- Aggregation election is made annually but consistency is required once made
- Aggregation can help meet W-2 wage or UBIA thresholds

**Reg 1.199A-5: Specified Service Trades or Businesses (SSTBs)**
- Detailed definitions of each SSTB category
- The "incidental to" rule: if a trade or business has both SSTB and non-SSTB components, and the SSTB portion is less than 10% of gross receipts (or 5% for businesses with $25M+ receipts), the entire business is treated as non-SSTB
- Anti-abuse rule: cannot crack out non-SSTB portions to avoid the limitation if they would not exist but for the SSTB

**Reg 1.199A-6: RPEs (Relevant Pass-through Entities)**
- Partnerships and S-Corps must report QBI, W-2 wages, and UBIA to owners on Schedule K-1
- Each owner calculates their own 199A deduction using their share of these items

### Compliance Notes

- Document aggregation elections carefully — include all required information on the return
- Maintain W-2 wage allocation workpapers showing how wages were assigned to QBI
- Track UBIA of all qualified property with acquisition dates and depreciable periods
- For SSTBs near the income threshold, consider strategies to stay below (retirement contributions, timing of income/deductions)

## Reg 1.162 — Business Expense Regulations

### Key Provisions

**Reg 1.162-1: Business Expenses Generally**
- Expenses must be directly connected with or pertain to the taxpayer's trade or business
- The "carrying on" requirement: expenses incurred before a business begins are startup costs (Section 195), not Section 162 deductions
- Distinguishes between current expenses and capital expenditures

**Reg 1.162-4: Repairs vs. Improvements (Tangible Property Regulations)**
- The tangible property regulations (finalized 2013) provide a comprehensive framework:
  - **De minimis safe harbor**: expense items costing $2,500 or less per invoice/item (or $5,000 with applicable financial statement/AFS) without capitalization
  - **Routine maintenance safe harbor**: recurring activities that keep property in ordinarily efficient operating condition are deductible
  - **Betterment, restoration, adaptation (BRA) tests**: an expenditure must be capitalized if it results in a betterment, restores the property to working condition after deterioration beyond normal wear, or adapts the property to a new or different use
  - Unit of property rules define the level at which BRA analysis applies (building systems are separate units of property: HVAC, plumbing, electrical, elevators, escalators, fire protection, security, gas distribution, and structural components)

**Reg 1.162-5: Education Expenses**
- Deductible if education maintains or improves skills required in current employment/business
- Not deductible if education qualifies the taxpayer for a new trade or business or meets minimum requirements for current position
- No deduction for MBA programs if they qualify for a new career (but see case law — this is fact-specific)

**Reg 1.162-7: Reasonable Compensation**
- Compensation must be reasonable in amount and paid for services actually rendered
- Factors: nature of services, qualifications, comparable compensation in similar businesses, size and complexity of business, prevailing rates
- Critical for S-Corp owner-employees and family members on payroll

### Compliance Notes

- Implement the de minimis safe harbor election annually (attached to tax return)
- Maintain a written capitalization policy for amounts up to $5,000 (required for the higher threshold with AFS)
- Document all travel and meal expenses per Section 274 substantiation rules: amount, date, place, business purpose, business relationship
- For related-party compensation, obtain comparable salary studies or use industry benchmarks

## Reg 1.263A — UNICAP Regulations

### Key Provisions

**Reg 1.263A-1: General Rules**
- Section 263A requires capitalization of direct costs and an allocable share of indirect costs to inventory and self-constructed assets
- Applicable to producers of real or tangible personal property and resellers with gross receipts exceeding the small business threshold ($29M average over 3 prior years)

**Reg 1.263A-2: Production Activities**
- Direct costs: direct materials and direct labor must be fully capitalized
- Indirect costs: categorized as Section 263A costs (must capitalize) or non-Section 263A costs (may expense)
  - Must capitalize: indirect labor, officers' compensation allocable to production, rent, depreciation, utilities, quality control, purchasing, handling, storage, insurance on production assets
  - Need not capitalize: selling costs, distribution costs, R&D (Section 174), marketing, general and administrative costs not allocable to production

**Reg 1.263A-3: Resale Activities**
- Resellers capitalize acquisition costs plus purchasing, handling, warehousing, and storage costs
- Simplified resale method: calculate a ratio of additional Section 263A costs to Section 471 costs; apply to ending inventory

**Reg 1.263A-7 through -15: Simplified Methods**
- Simplified production method: uses absorption ratio applied to ending inventory/WIP
- Modified simplified production method: separates pre-production and production costs
- These methods reduce compliance burden but must be elected and consistently applied

### Compliance Notes

- The small business exception (under $29M gross receipts) eliminates UNICAP for most small businesses — verify eligibility annually
- Changing UNICAP methods requires Form 3115 (Application for Change in Accounting Method)
- Common audit adjustments: failure to include officer compensation, rent, or depreciation in the UNICAP calculation
- Interest capitalization rules (Reg 1.263A-8 through -15) apply to assets with production periods exceeding 2 years or costing over $1 million

## Reg 301.7701 — Entity Classification (Check-the-Box)

### Overview

These regulations establish the default classification rules for business entities and allow certain entities to elect their tax classification. This is one of the most powerful planning tools available.

### Default Classification Rules

**Reg 301.7701-2: Business Entities; Definitions**
- A "business entity" is any entity recognized for federal tax purposes that is not a trust
- Per se corporations: entities formed under a statute that describes them as incorporated or as a corporation (plus listed foreign entities)
- Eligible entities: entities not automatically classified as corporations can elect their classification

**Reg 301.7701-3: Classification of Certain Business Entities**
- Default rules (if no election filed):
  - Domestic entity with 2+ members: partnership
  - Domestic entity with 1 member: disregarded entity (reported on owner's return)
  - Foreign entity with 2+ members: partnership (if at least one member has unlimited liability)
  - Foreign entity with limited liability for all members: association (corporation)
- **Check-the-box election (Form 8832)**: eligible entities can elect to be classified as:
  - Corporation (association)
  - Partnership (if 2+ members)
  - Disregarded entity (if 1 member)
- Election is effective on the date specified (up to 75 days before or 12 months after filing)
- Cannot change classification again for 60 months after an election

### Practical Application

- Single-member LLC: defaults to disregarded entity but can elect S-Corp treatment (Form 2553) or C-Corp treatment (Form 8832)
- Multi-member LLC: defaults to partnership but can elect corporate treatment
- S-Corp election requires Form 2553 and compliance with S-Corp eligibility requirements (100 shareholders, one class of stock, eligible shareholders only)
- The 60-month limitation on re-election prevents entity classification whipsawing

### Compliance Notes

- Form 8832 must be filed within 75 days before or 12 months after the desired effective date
- Late election relief is available under Rev. Proc. 2009-41 if certain conditions are met
- When converting from disregarded entity to corporation, the deemed contribution of assets follows Section 351 rules (generally tax-free if 80% control)
- When converting from corporation to disregarded entity, the deemed liquidation triggers gain/loss recognition

## Transfer Pricing Regulations (Reg 1.482)

### Overview

Section 482 and its regulations govern the allocation of income, deductions, and credits among commonly controlled entities (typically in international transactions) to reflect arm's-length pricing.

### Key Methods

**Reg 1.482-3: Tangible Property**
- Comparable uncontrolled price (CUP) method
- Resale price method
- Cost plus method
- Comparable profits method (CPM)
- Profit split method
- Best method rule: must use the method that provides the most reliable measure of arm's-length result

**Reg 1.482-4: Intangible Property**
- Comparable uncontrolled transaction (CUT) method
- CPM and profit split also applicable
- Commensurate with income standard: royalties for intangibles must be commensurate with the income attributable to the intangible
- Cost sharing arrangements (Reg 1.482-7): participants share costs and risks of developing intangibles in proportion to reasonably anticipated benefits

**Reg 1.482-9: Services**
- Services cost method (SCM): for routine, low-margin services — can use cost with no markup
- Comparable uncontrolled services price method
- Other methods (CPM, profit split) also applicable

### Documentation Requirements

- Contemporaneous documentation is required to avoid penalties (Section 6662(e))
- Documentation must include: business overview, organizational structure, controlled transactions, methods selected, comparable analysis, economic analysis
- Penalty protection: adequate documentation creates a rebuttable presumption of reasonable cause

### Compliance Notes

- Transfer pricing is one of the highest-priority IRS audit areas for mid-to-large companies
- Advance Pricing Agreements (APAs) can provide certainty but are time-consuming and expensive
- Country-by-country reporting (Form 8975) required for US-parented MNE groups with revenue exceeding $850 million
- Document intercompany transactions contemporaneously — do not wait until audit

## Employment Tax Regulations

### Key Provisions

**Reg 31.3121 — FICA (Social Security and Medicare)**
- Social Security tax: 6.2% employer + 6.2% employee on wages up to $176,100 (2025 wage base; indexed annually)
- Medicare tax: 1.45% employer + 1.45% employee on all wages (no cap)
- Additional Medicare tax: 0.9% on employee wages exceeding $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ) — employee only, no employer match
- Self-employment tax under Section 1401: 15.3% (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on net SE income; 50% deductible

**Reg 31.3401 — Federal Income Tax Withholding**
- Employers must withhold federal income tax from wages based on Form W-4 elections
- Supplemental wage withholding: flat 22% (or 37% for supplemental wages exceeding $1 million)
- Depositing rules: semi-weekly or monthly based on lookback period liability

**Reg 31.3306 — FUTA (Federal Unemployment)**
- 6.0% on first $7,000 of wages per employee
- Credit of up to 5.4% for state unemployment taxes paid, resulting in effective rate of 0.6%
- Additional credit reduction for states with outstanding federal loans

### Worker Classification

**Reg 31.3121(d)-1 and Rev. Rul. 87-41: Employee vs. Independent Contractor**
- Common law test: behavioral control, financial control, relationship of the parties
- IRS uses 20-factor test (consolidated into 3 categories above)
- Misclassification penalties: 100% of employee's share of FICA + penalties + interest
- Section 530 relief: provides penalty relief for consistent historical treatment and reasonable basis

### Compliance Notes

- Deposit employment taxes on time — trust fund taxes (employee's share of FICA and withheld income tax) create personal liability for responsible persons under Section 6672 (Trust Fund Recovery Penalty)
- File Forms 941 quarterly, Form 940 annually
- Issue W-2s by January 31 and file with SSA
- For S-Corp shareholders who are also employees, reasonable compensation must be paid — cannot take all income as distributions to avoid employment taxes
- Worker classification audits are common — maintain contracts, evidence of independence, and track behavioral/financial control factors

## Notes for Tina

- Treasury Regulations are cited as authority in tax research; they have the force of law when issued as final or temporary regulations
- Proposed regulations do not have the force of law but indicate the IRS's position
- Revenue Rulings and Revenue Procedures provide additional guidance but are not regulations
- When advising, always distinguish between mandatory compliance (regulations) and planning opportunities (elections within regulations)
- Monitor the Federal Register for regulatory updates, especially when new legislation is enacted
