# Lanham Act Reference — 15 U.S.C. §§1051–1141

## Purpose

This document provides Benny (IP Counsel agent) with a comprehensive, actionable reference to the Lanham Act (Trademark Act of 1946, as amended), the primary federal statute governing trademarks and unfair competition in the United States. Codified at 15 U.S.C. §§1051–1141.

---

## Section 1 — Registration on the Principal Register (15 U.S.C. §1051)

### §1(a) — Use-Based Applications

- An applicant who has **already used the mark in commerce** may apply for registration.
- Requirements: (1) the mark, (2) the applicant's domicile/citizenship, (3) the date of first use anywhere and date of first use in commerce, (4) the goods/services, (5) a specimen showing actual use, (6) a verified statement.
- "Commerce" means commerce that Congress may regulate — interstate, territorial, or between the U.S. and a foreign country. 15 U.S.C. §1127.

### §1(b) — Intent-to-Use Applications

- An applicant who has a **bona fide intention to use** the mark in commerce may file before actual use begins.
- No specimen is required at filing; a Statement of Use (SOU) with a specimen must be filed before registration.
- The constructive use date relates back to the filing date, providing priority over subsequent filers/users. 15 U.S.C. §1057(c).
- The applicant must demonstrate bona fide intent — not merely a subjective desire but an objective, good-faith plan to use the mark. TMEP §1101.01.

### §1(c) — Amendment to Allege Use

- Filed **before** approval for publication; converts an ITU application to a use-based application.
- Same requirements as an SOU: specimen + verified statement of use.

### §1(d) — Statement of Use

- Filed **after** the Notice of Allowance issues for a §1(b) application.
- Must be filed within 6 months (extendable to 36 months total with good cause).

## Section 2 — Bars to Registration (15 U.S.C. §1052)

### §2(a) — Immoral, Deceptive, Scandalous Matter; Disparagement; False Suggestion of Connection

- **Scandalous/immoral matter**: After Iancu v. Brunetti, 588 U.S. 388 (2019), the bar on "immoral or scandalous" marks was held unconstitutional as viewpoint discrimination under the First Amendment. However, the USPTO may still refuse marks that are "scandalous" in the sense of being obscene (vulgar). Current practice is evolving.
- **Deceptive marks**: A mark that misdescribes the goods/services and consumers are likely to believe the misdescription. This is a **permanent bar** — no acquired distinctiveness overcomes it.
- **False suggestion of connection**: The mark falsely suggests a connection with a person, institution, belief, or national symbol.
- **Disparagement**: After Matal v. Tam, 582 U.S. 218 (2017), the disparagement clause was held unconstitutional.

### §2(b) — Government Insignia

- Bars registration of marks consisting of or comprising the flag, coat of arms, or insignia of the United States, any state, municipality, or foreign nation.
- This is an absolute bar; no amount of evidence overcomes it.

### §2(c) — Name, Portrait, or Signature of a Living Individual

- Registration refused without the individual's **written consent**.
- Also applies to deceased U.S. Presidents during the life of the surviving spouse.
- Practical note: If the mark includes a living person's name, obtain and file a consent form.

### §2(d) — Likelihood of Confusion

- The most frequently applied bar. Registration refused if the mark so resembles a prior registered mark or prior pending application that confusion, mistake, or deception is likely.
- Analysis uses the **du Pont factors** (13 factors, though typically 2–4 are decisive). See `benny-conflict-search.md` for full factor analysis.
- Key principle: Marks need not be identical; goods/services need not be identical. The test is whether, considering all relevant factors, confusion is **likely** (not certain).
- Extends to prior common-law marks discovered during examination.

### §2(e)(1) — Merely Descriptive

- A mark that merely describes an ingredient, quality, characteristic, function, feature, purpose, or use of the goods/services.
- Test: Does the mark immediately convey information about the goods/services to a relevant consumer? In re Gyulay, 820 F.2d 1216 (Fed. Cir. 1987).
- **Not a permanent bar**: May be registered on the Supplemental Register or with acquired distinctiveness (§2(f)).
- Practical tip: Suggestive marks (which require imagination to connect the mark to the goods) are NOT descriptive.

### §2(e)(2) — Primarily Geographically Descriptive

- The mark is the name of a place, the goods/services originate from that place, and consumers would make the geographic connection.
- Overcomable with §2(f) evidence.

### §2(e)(3) — Primarily Geographically Deceptively Misdescriptive

- Same geographic analysis, but the goods do NOT actually come from the named place AND the geographic association is material to the consumer's purchasing decision.
- **Permanent bar** after the NAFTA amendments (effective 1994) — no §2(f) claim available.

### §2(e)(4) — Primarily Merely a Surname

- Registration refused if the primary significance of the mark to the purchasing public is a surname.
- Overcomable with §2(f) evidence.
- Analysis considers rareness of the surname, whether it has other meanings, the applicant's connection to the name, etc.

### §2(e)(5) — Functional Matter

- A mark that is functional (essential to the use or purpose of the product, or affects cost or quality) cannot be registered.
- **Permanent bar**. No amount of acquired distinctiveness overcomes functionality.
- Two types: utilitarian functionality (TrafFix Devices, Inc. v. Marketing Displays, Inc., 532 U.S. 23 (2001)) and aesthetic functionality.

### §2(f) — Acquired Distinctiveness (Secondary Meaning)

- Marks refused under §2(e)(1), (2), or (4) may be registered if the applicant demonstrates the mark has become distinctive through use.
- Evidence types: (1) five years of substantially exclusive and continuous use (creates a prima facie showing), (2) consumer surveys, (3) advertising expenditures, (4) sales volume, (5) media recognition, (6) unsolicited media coverage, (7) declarations from consumers/industry.
- Standard: The mark's primary significance to relevant consumers is as a source identifier, not a description.

## Section 7 — Certificates of Registration (15 U.S.C. §1057)

- §7(a): The USPTO issues a certificate of registration showing the mark, registration date, goods/services, and other pertinent data.
- §7(b): A certificate is **prima facie evidence** of the validity of the registration, the registrant's ownership, and exclusive right to use the mark in commerce.
- §7(c): Filing date serves as **constructive use** — nationwide priority date (subject to prior users and prior applicants).
- §7(d): Registered marks receive **constructive notice** — eliminates a good-faith defense by subsequent adopters.

---

## Section 8 — Affidavit of Continued Use or Excusable Nonuse (15 U.S.C. §1058)

- Must be filed between the **5th and 6th year** after registration, then in connection with each renewal (every 10 years).
- Must include a specimen of current use and a declaration that the mark is still in use in commerce for the identified goods/services (or that nonuse is excusable).
- **Grace period**: 6 months after the deadline, with a surcharge ($100 per class as of 2025).
- Failure to file results in **cancellation** of the registration.

---

## Section 9 — Renewal (15 U.S.C. §1059)

- Registration lasts **10 years** from the registration date; renewable indefinitely for additional 10-year periods.
- Renewal application filed within **1 year before** the expiration date.
- Grace period: **6 months** after expiration, with surcharge.
- Must be combined with a §8 affidavit.

---

## Section 14 — Cancellation (15 U.S.C. §1064)

- A petition to cancel a registration may be filed by any person who believes they are or will be damaged.
- **Within 5 years of registration**: Any ground that would have prevented registration (confusion, descriptiveness, etc.).
- **After 5 years**: Only on grounds of: (1) genericness, (2) functionality, (3) abandonment, (4) fraud, (5) the mark was obtained contrary to §§2(a), 2(b), or 2(c).
- Cancellation proceedings are heard by the **Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB)**.

---

## Section 15 — Incontestability (15 U.S.C. §1065)

- After **5 consecutive years** of continuous use following registration, the registrant may file a §15 declaration.
- Effect: The registration becomes **incontestable** — conclusive evidence of the right to use the mark (with limited exceptions).
- Incontestability does NOT protect against: (1) genericness, (2) functionality, (3) abandonment, (4) fraud, (5) prior use by another party, (6) fair use, (7) antitrust violations.
- Strategic value: Eliminates descriptiveness as a defense in infringement actions. Park 'N Fly, Inc. v. Dollar Park and Fly, Inc., 469 U.S. 189 (1985).

## Section 32 — Infringement (15 U.S.C. §1114)

- Civil liability for any person who, without consent, uses in commerce a reproduction, counterfeit, copy, or colorable imitation of a registered mark in connection with goods/services where such use is likely to cause confusion, mistake, or deception.
- Remedies: injunctive relief, actual damages, defendant's profits, costs, and in exceptional cases, attorney's fees.
- Treble damages available for willful counterfeiting.
- Criminal penalties apply to intentional trafficking in counterfeit goods/services. 18 U.S.C. §2320.

---

## Section 33 — Defenses to Infringement (15 U.S.C. §1115)

### §33(a) — Registrations That Are Not Incontestable

- The registration is prima facie evidence of validity, but the defendant may challenge validity on any ground.

### §33(b) — Incontestable Registrations

- The registration is conclusive evidence of the right to use, subject to these defenses:
  1. **Fraud** in obtaining registration.
  2. **Abandonment** of the mark.
  3. Use to misrepresent source (deception).
  4. **Fair use** — descriptive use in good faith of one's own name, geographic origin, or a descriptive term.
  5. **Prior use** — continuous use of the mark from a date prior to the registrant's filing or constructive use date.
  6. Registration obtained contrary to §§2(a), 2(b), or 2(c).
  7. The mark has been or is being used to violate **antitrust** laws.
  8. **Equitable defenses**: laches, estoppel, acquiescence.

## Section 43(a) — False Designation of Origin / Unfair Competition (15 U.S.C. §1125(a))

### §43(a)(1)(A) — Trademark Infringement (Unregistered Marks)

- Provides a federal cause of action for infringement of **unregistered marks** (common-law marks, trade dress, etc.).
- Same likelihood-of-confusion standard as §32.
- Practical significance: Even without a federal registration, a mark owner can bring a federal claim.

### §43(a)(1)(B) — False Advertising

- Prohibits false or misleading descriptions of fact or false designations of origin in commercial advertising.
- Standing requires: (1) the plaintiff is a competitor or has a commercial interest, (2) the advertising is literally false or misleading, (3) the misrepresentation is material, (4) the advertising is in interstate commerce, (5) the plaintiff has been or is likely to be injured.

### §43(c) — Dilution (15 U.S.C. §1125(c))

- Available only to owners of **famous marks** (widely recognized by the general consuming public).
- Two types: **dilution by blurring** (association that impairs distinctiveness) and **dilution by tarnishment** (association that harms reputation).
- Does NOT require likelihood of confusion.
- Defenses: fair use (comparative advertising, parody, criticism, news commentary), noncommercial use.

### §43(d) — Cybersquatting / ACPA (15 U.S.C. §1125(d))

- The Anticybersquatting Consumer Protection Act provides a cause of action against anyone who, with bad-faith intent to profit, registers, traffics in, or uses a domain name that is identical or confusingly similar to a distinctive or famous mark.
- Nine factors for bad-faith analysis. 15 U.S.C. §1125(d)(1)(B)(i).
- In rem jurisdiction available if the domain registrant cannot be found.

## Section 44 — International Provisions (15 U.S.C. §1126)

### §44(d) — Foreign Priority

- An applicant who has filed a trademark application in a foreign country that is party to a qualifying treaty may claim priority within **6 months** of the foreign filing date.
- Effect: The U.S. filing date relates back to the foreign filing date for priority purposes.

### §44(e) — Registration Based on Foreign Registration

- A foreign registrant may obtain a U.S. registration without showing use in U.S. commerce if they hold a valid registration in their country of origin.
- A certified copy of the foreign registration is required.
- The registrant must still comply with §8 and §9 maintenance requirements.

### §66(a) — Madrid Protocol (15 U.S.C. §1141)

- International registrations designating the United States under the Madrid Protocol are treated as §1(a) applications.
- Subject to the same examination standards as domestic applications.
- Five-year dependency period: If the home registration is cancelled within 5 years, the U.S. extension of protection may be cancelled.
- The U.S. designation can be transformed into a national application if the international registration is cancelled.

## Common Practical Issues

### Priority Disputes

- The first to **use** the mark in commerce generally has priority (not the first to file, unlike patent law).
- A §1(b) filing creates constructive use as of the filing date, but only if the application matures to registration.
- A foreign priority claim under §44(d) can defeat a domestic first-user if the foreign filing date precedes the domestic first use date.

### Abandonment

- A mark is deemed abandoned if: (1) use has been discontinued with **intent not to resume**, or (2) the mark has become generic. 15 U.S.C. §1127.
- **3 years** of nonuse creates a **prima facie presumption** of abandonment.
- Excusable nonuse (force majeure, government restriction) does not constitute abandonment.

### Assignment and Licensing

- A mark may be assigned only with the **goodwill** of the business associated with the mark. Assignment without goodwill (assignment "in gross") is invalid. 15 U.S.C. §1060.
- A licensor must maintain **quality control** over the licensee's use. Failure to do so constitutes "naked licensing" and may result in abandonment.

### Concurrent Use

- Two parties with legitimate rights to the same or similar mark may coexist if restricted to separate geographic territories. 15 U.S.C. §1052(d).
- Concurrent use proceedings are conducted by the TTAB.
