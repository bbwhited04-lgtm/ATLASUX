## Overview

Subchapter IV implements the Protocol Relating to the Madrid Agreement Concerning the International Registration of Marks (Madrid Protocol) in U.S. law. The Madrid Protocol is an international treaty administered by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) that allows trademark owners to seek protection in multiple countries through a single international application. The U.S. joined the Madrid Protocol effective November 2, 2003.

---

## Section 1141 (section 60) -- Definitions

- **International application**: An application for international registration filed under the Madrid Protocol
- **International Bureau**: The International Bureau of the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO)
- **International registration**: A registration of a mark granted under the Madrid Protocol
- **International Register**: The official collection of data concerning international registrations maintained by the International Bureau
- **Notification of refusal**: A declaration by the USPTO that protection of an international registration cannot be granted in the United States
- **Extension of protection**: A request that an international registration be given effect in the United States (essentially the Madrid equivalent of a national application)
- **Holder**: The natural or legal person in whose name the international registration is recorded in the International Register

---

## Section 1141a (section 61) -- International Applications Based on U.S. Applications or Registrations

### Filing Through USPTO
- The owner of a **U.S. application or registration** (the "basic application" or "basic registration") may file an international application through the USPTO
- The USPTO acts as the **Office of Origin** and certifies/transmits the application to the International Bureau

### Requirements
- The applicant must be a **national of the U.S.**, or **domiciled in the U.S.**, or have a **real and effective industrial or commercial establishment** in the U.S.
- The mark in the international application must be the **same mark** as in the basic U.S. application/registration
- The goods/services must be **contained within** those listed in the basic application/registration
- Must pay the required fee to the USPTO for transmitting the application

### Designating Countries
- The international application may designate one or more Madrid Protocol member countries where protection is sought
- Each designated country examines the request according to its own national law

---

## Section 1141b (section 62) -- Certification of International Application

### USPTO Certification
- The USPTO must certify that the information in the international application corresponds to the information in the basic application or registration
- If the international application does not correspond, the USPTO will not certify it
- The date of the international registration will be the date the international application was received by the USPTO (provided the International Bureau receives it within 2 months)

---

## Section 1141c (section 63) -- Restriction, Abandonment, Cancellation, or Expiration of Basic Application or Registration (Central Attack)

### Dependency Period (First 5 Years)
- For the **first 5 years** from the date of international registration, the international registration is **dependent on** the basic U.S. application/registration
- If the basic application/registration is restricted, abandoned, cancelled, or expires during this 5-year period, the international registration is cancelled or restricted to the same extent

### Central Attack
- A third party who successfully attacks the basic application/registration in the U.S. during the 5-year dependency period can effectively destroy the international registration and all designations flowing from it
- This is known as **"central attack"** and is a significant strategic risk

### Transformation
- After central attack, the holder may **transform** the cancelled international registration into national applications in each designated country
- Must be filed within **3 months** of cancellation of the international registration
- The national applications will retain the filing date (and priority date) of the international registration

---

## Section 1141d (section 64) -- Request for Extension of Protection to the United States

### How It Works
- A holder of an international registration may request that protection be extended to the United States
- The request may be made at the time of the international application or subsequently (subsequent designation)
- The International Bureau notifies the USPTO of the request

### Effect
- A request for extension of protection to the U.S. has the **same effect as a regular application** filed on the Principal Register under section 1 or section 44 of the Act
- The filing date of the international registration (or date of subsequent designation) is treated as the U.S. filing date

---

## Section 1141e (section 65) -- Extension of Protection of an International Registration to the United States Under the Madrid Protocol

### Prima Facie Entitlement
- Upon timely request, the holder of an international registration is entitled to the same benefits and subject to the same requirements as an applicant who files directly with the USPTO

### Examination
- The USPTO examines the request for extension of protection just as it would examine a national application
- All refusal grounds under section 2 of the Act apply
- Specimens and other requirements may apply depending on the filing basis

---

## Section 1141f (section 66) -- Filing Basis: Section 66(a)

### Section 66(a) as Filing Basis
- A request for extension of protection constitutes a filing basis under **section 66(a)** of the Act
- This is a distinct filing basis from sections 1(a), 1(b), 44(d), and 44(e)

### Key Characteristics
- No use in commerce required at time of filing (similar to section 1(b))
- Must eventually file a **statement of use** or demonstrate use before registration can issue (unless based on section 44(e))
- Cannot be filed directly through TEAS -- must come through the International Bureau

### Conversion to National Application
- Section 66(a) applications **CANNOT be converted** to other filing bases (1(a), 1(b), 44(d), 44(e))
- If the international registration is cancelled, the holder may file a **transformation** application (separate national application) retaining the international filing date

---

## Section 1141g (section 67) -- Notification of Refusal

### Refusal Process
- If the USPTO determines that protection cannot be granted, it sends a **notification of refusal** to the International Bureau
- Must be sent within **18 months** of the date of notification of the request for extension of protection
- The notification must specify the grounds for refusal and the provisions of law on which the refusal is based

### Response to Refusal
- The holder may respond to the refusal through a U.S.-licensed attorney
- Same response periods and procedures apply as for national applications (3-month response period, extendable to 6 months)

---

## Section 1141h (section 68) -- Examination of and Opposition to Request for Extension of Protection

### (a) Examination
- Examined same as national applications
- All grounds for refusal under the Act apply

### (b) Opposition
- After examination, if no refusal grounds found, mark published for opposition
- Same 30-day opposition period applies
- Extensions of time for opposition available same as for national applications

---

## Section 1141i (section 69) -- Effect of Protection of Extension to the United States

### Effect of Granting Protection
- When protection is granted, the international registration has the **same effect** as a registration on the Principal Register
- All rights and remedies under the Act are available to the holder

### Not a Basis for Section 44(e) Claims
- An international registration that has been extended to the U.S. cannot be used as a basis for filing a section 44(e) application

---

## Section 1141j (section 70) -- Dependence of Extension of Protection; Section 8 and 9 Requirements

### (a) Cancellation
- If the international registration is cancelled by the International Bureau (e.g., non-renewal with WIPO, or as a result of central attack), the extension of protection in the U.S. is also cancelled

### (b) Section 8 Requirements (Continued Use)
- The holder must file an affidavit/declaration of continued use (or excusable nonuse) with the USPTO
- Same timing as national registrations: Between 5th and 6th year, then every 10-year period
- Grace period of 6 months (with surcharge)
- **Failure to file**: Extension of protection cancelled

### (c) Section 9 NOT Applicable
- Section 9 (renewal) does NOT apply to extensions of protection
- Renewal of the international registration is handled through WIPO, not the USPTO
- The holder must renew the international registration with the International Bureau every 10 years

---

## Section 1141k (section 71) -- Affidavits and Fees

### Duration and Maintenance
- Extensions of protection subject to sections 8 and 71 of the Act
- Section 71 is the Madrid-specific equivalent of section 8, requiring:
  - Affidavit/declaration of continued use or excusable nonuse
  - Filed between 5th and 6th year after date of international registration
  - Then within year before end of every 10-year period
  - Specimen showing current use
  - Fee per class

### Renewal
- Handled through WIPO (not USPTO) every 10 years
- If international registration lapses with WIPO, U.S. protection is lost

---

## Section 1141l (section 72) -- Assignment of Extension of Protection
- Assignment of an extension of protection must be recorded with the International Bureau
- The International Bureau notifies the USPTO
- If the assignment is made to a person who is not eligible to hold an international registration (i.e., not a national of, domiciled in, or having an establishment in a Madrid Protocol member country), the extension of protection may be affected

---

## Section 1141m (section 73) -- Incontestability
- Extensions of protection may become incontestable under section 15 of the Act
- Same requirements: 5 years of continuous use after protection is granted, plus affidavit filing

---

## Section 1141n (section 74) -- Rights of Extension of Protection
- The holder of an extension of protection has the same rights as the owner of a registration on the Principal Register
- Subject to the same requirements, limitations, and remedies

---

## Madrid Protocol: Strategic Considerations

### Advantages
1. **Single application, multiple countries**: File one application through WIPO, designate any Madrid Protocol member country
2. **Central management**: Single registration number, single renewal, centralized assignment recording
3. **Cost efficiency**: Often cheaper than filing individual national applications in each country
4. **Subsequent designation**: Can add countries after the initial filing
5. **Filing date preservation**: International filing date serves as national filing date in designated countries

### Risks and Disadvantages
1. **Central attack vulnerability**: During the 5-year dependency period, if the basic U.S. application/registration is challenged and fails, ALL international designations may be lost
2. **Must use U.S.-licensed attorney**: Foreign holders must appoint a qualified U.S. attorney for all USPTO proceedings
3. **Cannot change filing basis**: Section 66(a) applications cannot be converted to other bases
4. **Renewal complexity**: Must renew both with WIPO (for international registration) and file section 71 affidavits with USPTO (for U.S. protection)
5. **Limited scope of goods/services**: International registration cannot exceed the scope of the basic application/registration

### Common Madrid Protocol Workflows
1. **U.S. company seeking international protection**: File U.S. application/registration as basic mark, then file international application through USPTO designating target countries
2. **Foreign company seeking U.S. protection**: File international application through home country office, designate U.S. as extension of protection -- creates section 66(a) filing basis at USPTO
3. **Central attack mitigation**: Consider filing direct national applications in key markets in addition to Madrid designations, as backup against central attack risk
4. **Transformation after cancellation**: If basic mark is cancelled within 5-year dependency period, promptly file transformation applications in all designated countries within the 3-month window

## Key Deadlines Summary

| Action | Deadline |
|--------|----------|
| International application transmitted to IB | USPTO certifies and transmits; IB date = USPTO receipt date if received within 2 months |
| USPTO refusal notification | Within 18 months of notification of extension request |
| Response to USPTO refusal | 3 months (extendable to 6 months) |
| Central attack dependency period | 5 years from date of international registration |
| Transformation applications after central attack | 3 months from cancellation of international registration |
| Section 71 affidavit (continued use) | Between 5th and 6th year; then every 10-year period (6-month grace) |
| International renewal with WIPO | Every 10 years |
| Opposition to extension of protection | 30 days from publication (same extensions available) |
