# TMEP Chapter 700 -- Procedure for Examining Applications

> Source: Trademark Manual of Examining Procedure (TMEP), 8th Edition, October 2011
> Sections: 701--720

---

## 701 -- Statutory Authority for Examination

The Commissioner of Patents and Trademarks (now the Director of the USPTO) has authority under 15 U.S.C. section 1062 to establish regulations for the examination of applications for registration. The examining attorney determines whether the mark is entitled to registration and whether any statutory grounds for refusal exist.

The examining attorney must examine each application to determine compliance with all applicable statutory and regulatory requirements, including whether the mark is registrable under sections 1, 2, 3, 4, and 45 of the Trademark Act.

---

## 702 -- Assignment of Applications

Applications are assigned to examining attorneys in the various law offices. The assignment is generally based on the international class of goods or services. Each law office handles specific classes, and individual examining attorneys within each office are assigned applications in the order received.

### 702.01 -- Order of Examination

Applications are generally examined in the order of their filing date. However, certain applications may receive priority treatment (see TMEP section 710).

### 702.02 -- "Special" Applications

The Director may order that an application be given special status and examined out of turn. Grounds include:

- The applicant's advanced age or health
- Litigation involving the mark
- An infringement action where the registration would be relevant
- Applications designated by the Commissioner/Director

A petition to make an application "special" must include a showing of good cause and supporting evidence.

### 702.03 -- Companion Applications

When an applicant has filed two or more applications for the same or similar marks for related goods/services, the applications should be examined together to ensure consistent treatment. If the examining attorney discovers companion applications in a different law office, the examining attorney should request transfer or coordinate with the other office.

### 702.04 -- Conflicting Applications

When two or more pending applications cover the same or confusingly similar marks for related goods/services, the examining attorney handling the later-filed application should suspend action on it until the earlier-filed application is either registered or abandoned. If both are assigned to the same examining attorney, both should be examined but the later-filed one suspended pending disposition of the earlier one.

### 702.05 -- Duplicate Registrations

The USPTO will not knowingly issue a duplicate registration -- that is, a registration for the same mark, same goods/services, to the same owner, when a prior registration is still live. If an examining attorney discovers a potential duplicate, the applicant must either amend the application to distinguish it from the prior registration or claim ownership of the cited registration.

## 704 -- Examination -- Complete Examination Required

### 704.01 -- Examining Attorney's Search

The examining attorney must conduct a search of USPTO records to identify any prior registrations or pending applications that may conflict with the applicant's mark. The search includes:

- Registered marks (live registrations)
- Pending applications
- Marks on the Supplemental Register
- Cancelled and expired registrations (for evidentiary purposes)

The search must cover phonetic equivalents, foreign-language translations, design elements (using design search codes), and other variations that could be confusingly similar.

The examining attorney is not required to search common-law sources, but may consider evidence of common-law use that comes to the examining attorney's attention during examination.

### 704.02 -- Complete Examination on First Action

The examining attorney must raise **all** issues in the first Office action. The purpose is to ensure that the applicant receives notice of every ground for refusal and every requirement at the earliest possible stage, so the applicant can make informed decisions about how to proceed.

Failure to raise an issue in the first action does not waive the examining attorney's right to raise it later, but the examining attorney should make every reasonable effort to identify all issues initially.

---

## 705 -- Examining Attorney's Letter or Office Action

The examining attorney communicates with the applicant through Office actions. An Office action may:

1. Refuse registration on one or more grounds
2. Require amendments to the application
3. Request additional information or evidence
4. Raise informational matters

### 705.01 -- Language of Office Actions

Office actions must be clear, professional, and complete. The examining attorney should:

- State each refusal or requirement with specificity
- Cite the statutory or regulatory basis for each refusal
- Explain the reasoning and provide supporting evidence
- Use approved form paragraphs where available
- Include the six-month response clause

### 705.02 -- Citations in Office Actions

When refusing registration based on a likelihood of confusion with a prior mark, the examining attorney must identify the cited registration or application and provide:

- The registration or serial number
- The mark
- The relevant goods/services
- The relevant class(es)

### 705.03 -- Form Paragraphs

The USPTO maintains a library of approved form paragraphs for common refusals and requirements. Examining attorneys should use form paragraphs where applicable, supplemented by case-specific analysis.

### 705.04 -- Six-Month Response Clause

Every Office action must include a notice that the applicant has **six months** from the date of issuance to respond. Failure to respond within six months results in abandonment of the application.

### 705.05 -- Attachments and Evidence

The examining attorney should attach to the Office action all evidence supporting a refusal, including:

- Copies of cited registrations
- Dictionary definitions
- Internet evidence (web pages, screenshots with URLs and dates)
- Third-party registration evidence

## 706 -- New Matter After First Action

### 706.01 -- General Rule

After the first Office action, the examining attorney generally should not raise new grounds for refusal or new requirements unless there is good cause. A new ground for refusal may be raised after the first action when:

1. The ground was not reasonably discoverable at the time of the first action
2. The applicant's response or amendment creates a new issue
3. The examining attorney made a "clear error" in failing to raise the issue initially

### 706.02 -- Clear Error Standard

A "clear error" exists when the examining attorney should have raised the issue in the first action and there is no reasonable excuse for the failure. The standard is strict -- mere oversight or inadvertence does not automatically constitute clear error.

When the examining attorney raises a new issue after the first action, the action must be designated as non-final to give the applicant a full opportunity to respond.

---

## 707 -- Examiner's Amendments

An examiner's amendment is a change to the application record made by the examining attorney, either with or without the applicant's authorization.

### 707.01 -- Examiner's Amendment With Authorization

The examining attorney may contact the applicant or the applicant's attorney to discuss and resolve issues by telephone or e-mail. If agreement is reached, the examining attorney prepares an examiner's amendment reflecting the agreed-upon changes. The applicant or attorney must authorize the changes.

Authorization may be obtained by telephone or e-mail. The examining attorney must document who authorized the amendment and when.

### 707.02 -- Examiner's Amendment Without Authorization

Certain minor, non-substantive amendments may be made without the applicant's authorization:

- Correcting obvious typographical errors
- Adding or correcting the international class number
- Updating the applicant's correspondence address based on a proper change request
- Reclassifying goods/services to the correct international class

The examining attorney should **not** make substantive changes without authorization, such as:

- Amending the identification of goods/services
- Adding or deleting a filing basis
- Amending the description of the mark

### 707.03 -- No-Call Situations

In certain circumstances, the examining attorney should issue a written Office action rather than attempt to resolve the matter by telephone:

- When the issues are complex and require detailed legal analysis
- When the applicant is not represented by an attorney and the matter involves substantive legal issues
- When the resolution would require the applicant to make a significant strategic decision

### 707.04 -- Form of Examiner's Amendment

The examiner's amendment must clearly identify every change made and who authorized it. It is entered into the application record as part of the prosecution history.

## 708 -- Priority Actions

### 708.01 -- Definition

A priority action is an Office action issued by the examining attorney that identifies a ground for refusal that the examining attorney considers likely to be dispositive. The purpose is to alert the applicant at the earliest stage that there is a significant issue that may prevent registration.

### 708.02 -- When to Issue a Priority Action

A priority action is appropriate when the examining attorney identifies a clear ground for refusal but wants to give the applicant the opportunity to respond before conducting a complete examination of all other issues. Common situations include:

- A clear likelihood of confusion with a prior registration
- The mark is clearly generic for the goods/services
- The mark is clearly merely descriptive without acquired distinctiveness

### 708.03 -- Requirements for Priority Actions

A priority action must:

1. Clearly state the ground(s) for refusal
2. Provide the legal basis and supporting evidence
3. Include the six-month response clause
4. Note that other issues may be raised in a subsequent action

The examining attorney must discuss the refusal with sufficient detail that the applicant can make an informed decision about whether to respond.

### 708.04 -- Refusal in Priority Action

The priority action is a non-final action. The applicant has six months to respond. If the applicant overcomes the refusal, the examining attorney then completes the examination and may raise additional issues.

---

## 709 -- Interviews

### 709.01 -- Personal Interviews

The examining attorney may grant a personal interview with the applicant or the applicant's attorney at the USPTO. Interviews should be scheduled in advance. A summary of the interview must be made part of the application record.

### 709.02 -- Telephone Interviews

Telephone calls are the most common form of interview. The examining attorney may initiate a call to resolve minor issues or the applicant may request a call. The examining attorney should document the substance of the call in the record, including:

- Who participated
- What was discussed
- What was agreed upon
- Whether any authorization was given for changes

### 709.03 -- E-mail Communications

Examining attorneys may communicate by e-mail with applicants who have authorized e-mail correspondence. E-mail should not be used for substantive legal discussions that should be documented in a formal Office action.

### 709.04 -- Informal Communications

Informal communications (phone calls, e-mails) are useful for resolving minor issues but do not substitute for formal Office actions. The examining attorney should not rely on informal communications to raise new grounds for refusal or impose new requirements.

---

## 710 -- Evidence

### 710.01 -- Evidence Supporting Refusal

The examining attorney bears the initial burden of establishing a prima facie case for any refusal. Evidence must be made part of the record. Common types of evidence include:

- **Dictionary definitions** -- from reputable dictionaries, with source identified
- **Internet evidence** -- web pages, articles, blog posts showing use of the term; must include URL and date accessed
- **Third-party registrations** -- showing that similar marks are registered for similar goods/services, to establish descriptiveness or the strength/weakness of a mark
- **Excerpts from publications** -- newspapers, trade journals, industry sources

### 710.02 -- Internet Evidence

Internet evidence is admissible and widely used. The examining attorney must capture and attach:

- The URL of the web page
- The date the page was accessed or printed
- Sufficient content to establish the relevant point

Web pages from well-known sources (major retailers, news outlets, government sites) generally carry more weight than obscure or unreliable sources.

### 710.03 -- Wikipedia

Wikipedia may be used as evidence of how a term is perceived by the public, but with the caveat that Wikipedia entries can be edited by anyone and may not be reliable. Wikipedia evidence should be corroborated with other sources when possible.

### 710.04 -- Third-Party Registrations

Third-party registrations are not evidence that the marks shown therein are in use or that the public is familiar with them. They are relevant to show:

- That a term is descriptive or generic (if many registrations disclaim or restrict the term)
- The meaning of a term in the relevant industry
- That the USPTO has registered similar marks for similar goods/services

### 710.05 -- Completeness of Record for Appeal

The examining attorney must ensure the record is complete before the application goes on appeal. All evidence supporting a refusal must be in the record. Evidence not made of record before the TTAB cannot generally be considered on appeal.

## 711 -- Response Time

### 711.01 -- Six-Month Response Period

The applicant has **six months** from the date of issuance of the Office action to file a response. This period is **not extendable**. Failure to respond within six months results in abandonment of the application.

The six-month period runs from the issue date of the Office action, not from the date the applicant receives it.

### 711.02 -- Supplemental Office Actions

If the examining attorney issues a supplemental Office action (e.g., to correct an error or provide additional information), the supplemental action resets the six-month response period from the date of the supplemental action.

---

## 712 -- Signature on Response

A response to an Office action must be signed by the applicant or the applicant's attorney of record. If the applicant is represented by an attorney, the attorney must sign the response. A response signed by an unauthorized person may be treated as unsigned.

For declarations and verifications, the applicant (or an authorized officer/partner/member for a juristic entity) must sign personally.

---

## 713 -- Examination of Amendments and Responses

When a response to an Office action is received, the examining attorney reviews it to determine whether all outstanding refusals and requirements have been satisfied. If the response satisfactorily addresses all issues, the application is approved for publication. If not, the examining attorney issues another Office action.

---

## 714 -- Final Action

### 714.01 -- When Final Action Is Appropriate

A final action may be issued after the applicant has had at least **one** opportunity to respond to the refusal or requirement. An action should be made final only when:

1. The applicant's response does not overcome the refusal or comply with the requirement
2. No new issues are raised by the examining attorney

If the examining attorney raises a new ground for refusal or a new requirement in the action, the action must be made **non-final**.

### 714.02 -- Form of Final Action

A final action must:

- Clearly state "THIS ACTION IS MADE FINAL"
- Restate or maintain each refusal and requirement not satisfied
- Respond to each of the applicant's arguments
- Advise the applicant of appeal rights and the six-month response deadline

### 714.03 -- New Issues in Final Actions

The examining attorney should **not** raise new issues in a final action. If a new issue arises after the first action (e.g., because of information in the applicant's response), the examining attorney must issue a non-final action.

### 714.04 -- Amendments to the Supplemental Register

If an applicant requests amendment to the Supplemental Register in response to a final refusal on the Principal Register, the examining attorney must consider whether the mark is eligible for the Supplemental Register and may need to issue a new non-final action addressing Supplemental Register requirements.

### 714.05 -- Consent Agreements and Substitute Specimens

If an applicant submits a consent agreement or substitute specimen after a final action, the examining attorney has discretion whether to consider it. The examining attorney should consider the submission if it clearly resolves the outstanding issue.

---

## 715 -- Action After Final Refusal

### 715.01 -- Proper Response to Final Action

After a final action, the applicant may:

1. **Comply** with all outstanding requirements and overcome all refusals
2. **Appeal** to the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB)
3. **Request reconsideration** by the examining attorney, with or without filing an appeal

### 715.02 -- Reconsideration After Final Action

A request for reconsideration does not stay or extend the six-month response period. The examining attorney has discretion to reconsider a final refusal based on:

- New arguments or evidence not previously presented
- A persuasive showing that the examining attorney erred

If the examining attorney denies reconsideration, the applicant must file an appeal within the six-month response period or the application will be abandoned.

### 715.03 -- Request for Reconsideration With Appeal Filed

If the applicant files both a notice of appeal and a request for reconsideration, the examining attorney should consider the request before the appeal brief is due. If the examining attorney reverses the refusal, the appeal is moot.

---

## 716 -- Suspension of Action

### 716.01 -- Form and Notice

When the examining attorney suspends action on an application, the applicant must be notified in writing. The suspension notice must state the reason for suspension and the anticipated duration.

### 716.02 -- Circumstances Warranting Suspension

Suspension is appropriate when:

1. **Conflicting application pending** -- A prior-filed application for a similar mark is pending; the later application is suspended until the earlier one is resolved
2. **Petition to cancel** -- A petition to cancel the cited registration has been filed at the TTAB
3. **Court proceeding** -- Litigation involving the mark is pending
4. **Foreign registration pending** -- For section 44 applications where a certified copy of the foreign registration has not yet been submitted
5. **Pending cancellation or expiration of cited registration** -- The cited registration may be cancelled for failure to file a section 8 affidavit or section 9 renewal
6. **Correction of international registration** -- For section 66(a) applications awaiting correction by the International Bureau

### 716.03 -- Duration and Review

Suspended applications are reviewed periodically (generally every six months) to determine whether the basis for suspension has been resolved. The examining attorney checks the suspended docket and takes appropriate action.

### 716.04 -- Suspension After Final Action

If grounds for suspension arise after a final action, the examining attorney may suspend the application. The six-month response period is generally tolled during suspension.

### 716.05 -- Removal From Suspension

When the basis for suspension is resolved, the examining attorney removes the application from suspension and takes the next appropriate action (approval for publication, issuance of a new Office action, etc.).

---

## 717 -- Reissuing Office Actions

If an Office action is returned as undeliverable or was not received by the applicant through no fault of the applicant, the examining attorney should reissue the action with a new response period.

If the examining attorney sent the action to the correct address and it was returned, the examining attorney will attempt to obtain the correct address and forward the action, but the response period is **not** automatically extended.

If the USPTO sent the action to the wrong address due to a USPTO error, the action will be reissued with a new six-month response period.

---

## 718 -- Abandonment

### 718.01 -- Express Abandonment

An applicant may expressly abandon an application at any time by filing a written statement of abandonment. Express abandonment is irrevocable once processed. The filing fee is not refunded.

### 718.02 -- Abandonment for Failure to Respond

If an applicant fails to respond to an Office action within the six-month response period, the application is abandoned. Abandonment is automatic -- no further notice from the USPTO is required, although a notice of abandonment is sent.

### 718.03 -- Partial Abandonment

If an applicant's response addresses some but not all of the goods/services or refusals, the application may be partially abandoned as to the unaddressed goods/services.

### 718.04 -- Incomplete Response

A response that does not address all outstanding refusals and requirements is an incomplete response. The examining attorney may treat it as a complete response and issue a final action on the unresolved issues, or may hold the response insufficient and require completion.

### 718.05 -- Failure to File Statement of Use

For intent-to-use applications, failure to file a statement of use within the statutory period (including any extensions) results in abandonment.

### 718.06 -- Failure to Perfect Appeal

If an applicant files a notice of appeal but fails to file an appeal brief within the time set by the TTAB, the appeal may be dismissed and the application abandoned.

### 718.07 -- Notice of Abandonment

The USPTO sends a notice of abandonment to the applicant's correspondence address. The notice informs the applicant of the right to petition for revival.

### 718.08 -- Revival of Abandoned Applications

Under 37 C.F.R. section 2.66, an abandoned application may be revived upon petition to the Director if the applicant can show that the failure to respond was **unintentional**. The petition must:

1. Be filed within **two months** of the date of the notice of abandonment (or within two months of actual knowledge of the abandonment if the notice was not received)
2. Include the required petition fee
3. Include a statement that the delay was unintentional
4. Include the response that was not previously filed

The Director has discretion to grant or deny the petition. The "unintentional" standard requires a showing that the applicant did not deliberately fail to respond.

---

## 719 -- Non-Responsive Communications

If a communication is received that does not constitute a proper response to an Office action (e.g., it does not address the refusal or requirement), the examining attorney should notify the applicant that the communication is not a proper response and that the six-month response period continues to run.

A letter requesting the status of an application or asking general questions is not a response to an Office action.

## 720 -- Fraud Upon the USPTO

### 720.01 -- Definition and Standard

Fraud in procuring a trademark registration occurs when the applicant knowingly makes material misrepresentations of fact in connection with the application. The standard for fraud requires:

1. A false representation regarding a material fact
2. Knowledge or belief that the representation is false (scienter)
3. Intent to induce the USPTO to act (or refrain from acting) based on the misrepresentation
4. Reasonable reliance by the USPTO
5. Damages (the issuance of a registration to which the applicant was not entitled)

### 720.02 -- Examining Attorney's Role

The examining attorney does not adjudicate fraud claims. If the examining attorney has reason to believe that an applicant has made a material misrepresentation, the examining attorney should:

- Request clarification or additional information from the applicant
- Note the discrepancy in the record
- Refer the matter to the Office of the Commissioner/Director if appropriate

Fraud is more commonly raised in inter partes proceedings before the TTAB or in federal court.

### 720.03 -- Common Fraud Issues

- **False claim of use in commerce** -- claiming use of the mark on goods/services when the mark is not actually being used
- **False specimen** -- submitting a specimen that has been fabricated or does not reflect actual use
- **Overbroad identification** -- claiming use on goods/services that the applicant does not offer and has no intent to offer
- **False ownership claim** -- claiming to own the mark when another entity is the true owner
- **Failure to disclose prior registrations** -- particularly when the applicant has a conflicting mark registered under a different entity name

### 720.04 -- Consequences of Fraud

A registration obtained through fraud is void from the outset and may be cancelled at any time. Fraud is a ground for cancellation under 15 U.S.C. section 1064.
