# TMEP Chapter 600 -- Attorney, Representative, and Signature

> Source: Trademark Manual of Examining Procedure (TMEP), 8th Edition, October 2011
> Sections: 601--611.06(h)

---

## 601 -- Representation of Applicants and Registrants

An applicant or registrant may prosecute a trademark application or maintain a registration either pro se (on their own behalf) or through a qualified practitioner. The owner of an application or registration is not required to be represented by an attorney to transact business with the USPTO.

---

## 602 -- Persons Authorized to Practice Before the USPTO in Trademark Matters

### 602.01 -- Attorneys

Under 37 C.F.R. section 11.14(a), an attorney who is a member in good standing of the bar of the highest court of any U.S. state may represent others before the USPTO in trademark matters. The attorney is not required to register with the Office of Enrollment and Discipline (OED).

### 602.02 -- Non-Attorneys

Under 37 C.F.R. section 11.14(b), individuals who are not attorneys may represent applicants or registrants before the USPTO in trademark matters only if they have been granted limited recognition by OED. This includes registered patent agents (limited to trademark matters only if granted specific recognition) and other individuals who have demonstrated competence.

### 602.03 -- Canadian Attorneys and Agents

Under 37 C.F.R. section 11.14(c), a Canadian attorney or agent may be recognized to represent a Canadian applicant or registrant if: (1) the attorney or agent is registered to practice before the Canadian Intellectual Property Office; and (2) the applicant or registrant is Canadian. OED maintains a list of recognized Canadian practitioners.

### 602.04 -- Standards of Professional Conduct

Practitioners authorized to practice before the USPTO are subject to the USPTO Rules of Professional Conduct in 37 C.F.R. sections 11.101 through 11.901. Violations may result in disciplinary action including suspension or exclusion from practice.

---

## 603 -- Recognition as Representative

### 603.01 -- How a Practitioner Is Recognized

The USPTO recognizes a qualified practitioner as the representative of an applicant or registrant when:

1. The applicant or registrant files a power of attorney designating the practitioner
2. The practitioner files a document on behalf of the applicant or registrant (recognition by appearance)
3. The practitioner's name and address appear in the application as filed

Once recognized, the USPTO communicates only with the appointed practitioner or another practitioner from the same firm.

---

## 604 -- Duration of Recognition as Representative

### 604.01 -- General Rule

Once recognized, a practitioner remains the representative of record until:

- The applicant or registrant revokes the power of attorney
- The practitioner withdraws
- The application or registration is abandoned, cancelled, or expired
- A new power of attorney is filed naming a different practitioner (treated as revocation of the prior power)

### 604.02 -- Duration in Relation to Registration

The USPTO considers a power of attorney to end with registration. If the power is filed in connection with a post-registration filing (affidavit under section 8, 12(c), 15, or 71, a section 9 renewal, or a section 7 request), the power is deemed to end upon acceptance or final rejection of that filing. 37 C.F.R. section 2.17(g)(2).

### 604.03 -- Change of Attorney During Prosecution

When the applicant or registrant wishes to change attorneys, a new power of attorney or a revocation of the existing power must be filed. A request to change the correspondence address alone does not revoke a power of attorney.

---

## 605 -- Power of Attorney

### 605.01 -- Requirements

A power of attorney must:

1. Designate by name at least one qualified practitioner
2. Be signed by the individual applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind them (e.g., corporate officer, general partner)
3. In the case of joint applicants or registrants, be signed by all

37 C.F.R. section 2.17(c). The power may name an individual practitioner, multiple practitioners, or a firm. A power that names only a firm and not an individual practitioner is insufficient.

### 605.02 -- Power of Attorney Relating to More Than One Application or Registration

A single power of attorney may cover multiple applications and registrations, provided the power: (1) identifies the qualified practitioner(s) by name; (2) identifies each application or registration by serial number, registration number, or a copy of the mark; and (3) specifies the filing date. If these requirements are met, the USPTO will accept the power.

### 605.03 -- Associate Powers of Attorney

Once the applicant or registrant has designated a qualified practitioner, the practitioner may sign an associate power of attorney appointing another qualified practitioner -- including one from a different law firm -- as an additional person authorized to represent the applicant or registrant. 37 C.F.R. section 2.17(c)(2).

If the applicant or registrant revokes the original power, the revocation also discharges any associate power signed by that practitioner. If the practitioner who signed an associate power withdraws, the withdrawal discharges the associate power upon acceptance of the withdrawal by the USPTO.

### 605.04 -- Powers of Attorney Filed After Registration

The USPTO recommends filing powers of attorney through TEAS. When filed through TEAS, data is automatically entered into the USPTO's automated records (TRAM and TARR).

When a new power is filed on paper after registration, the USPTO scans the document into TICRS and TDR but does not change the attorney information in automated records unless the registrant concurrently takes a separate action (e.g., filing an affidavit under section 8).

For recognition purposes, a power filed while an application is pending is deemed to end with registration. If the power is filed with a post-registration affidavit, renewal, or section 7 request, it ends upon acceptance or final rejection of that filing.

---

## 606 -- Revocation of Power of Attorney

Under 37 C.F.R. section 2.19(a), the authority to represent an applicant, registrant, or party may be revoked at any stage by written notification signed by the applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind them. Joint applicants or registrants must all sign.

**Key rules for revocation:**

- When a power is revoked, the USPTO communicates directly with the applicant/registrant or the new attorney or domestic representative
- Changing the correspondence address does **not** revoke a power of attorney
- Filing a new power of attorney naming a new practitioner is treated as a revocation of the prior power, even without an express revocation. 37 C.F.R. section 2.19(a)(4)

**Signature**: The revocation must be personally signed by the individual applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind a juristic applicant/registrant. A new qualified practitioner cannot sign the revocation itself but may submit a revocation and new appointment through TEAS by e-mailing the form to the applicant for electronic signature or by attaching a signed image.

**Effect on Correspondence Address**: Filing a new power with the revocation changes the correspondence address to that in the new power. Filing a revocation without a new power causes correspondence to be sent directly to the applicant/registrant or to the designated correspondence address.

**Processing After Registration**: Paper revocations filed after registration are scanned into TICRS/TDR but do not update automated records unless the registrant concurrently takes an action (e.g., filing a section 8 affidavit). TEAS-filed revocations after registration are processed automatically.

## 607 -- Withdrawal of Attorney of Record

Under 37 C.F.R. section 2.19(b), a qualified practitioner may withdraw from representing an applicant or registrant upon application to and approval by the Director (or, in Board proceedings, upon motion granted by the TTAB).

**Requirements for withdrawal request:**

1. The application serial number, registration number, or proceeding number
2. A statement of the reason(s) for the request to withdraw
3. Either:
   - A statement that the practitioner has given due notice to the client of the withdrawal, will file necessary documents with the USPTO, gave notice at least two months before expiration of any response period, has delivered all documents and property to the client, and has notified the client of any pending responses and deadlines; **or**
   - If more than one qualified practitioner is of record, a statement that representation by co-counsel is ongoing

**May not prejudice applicant or registrant**: A practitioner may not withdraw in a way that would prejudice the applicant or registrant. 37 C.F.R. section 10.40(a).

**Form**: The USPTO recommends filing withdrawal requests through TEAS. TEAS requests are processed electronically and automatically granted if they meet the requirements.

**Processing paper requests**: Before registration, handled by the managing attorney. After a notice of allowance, handled by the ITU/Divisional Unit Supervisor. After registration, handled by the Post Registration Section Supervisor.

**Abandoned applications**: The USPTO will not process a withdrawal request in an abandoned application.

## 608 -- Unauthorized Practice

### 608.01 -- Actions by Unauthorized Persons Not Permitted

Under 37 C.F.R. section 11.5(b)(2), practice before the USPTO in trademark matters includes: consulting with or advising a client about filing a trademark application; preparing and prosecuting an application; preparing amendments requiring written argument; and conducting oppositions, cancellations, concurrent use proceedings, or appeals.

An individual not authorized under 37 C.F.R. section 11.14 may **not**:
- Prepare applications, responses, or other documents to be filed in the USPTO
- Sign amendments, responses to Office actions, petitions to the Director, requests to change correspondence address, or letters of express abandonment
- Authorize examiner's amendments or priority actions
- Otherwise represent an applicant, registrant, or party

**Exception**: A paralegal, legal assistant, or employee of a qualified practitioner may prepare documents for review and signature by the practitioner.

Paralegals and legal assistants may relay information between the examining attorney and the appointed practitioner but are not authorized to conduct business before the USPTO (e.g., they cannot authorize examiner's amendments or priority actions).

### 608.02 -- Individuals Excluded, Suspended, or Unauthorized to Practice

The Director may suspend or exclude individuals from practice. Notice is published in the Official Gazette. A suspended or excluded individual is not a qualified practitioner.

When the USPTO discovers unauthorized practice, it will:

1. Notify the affected applicant/registrant that the individual may not represent them
2. State that any power of attorney is void ab initio
3. Indicate the individual may not sign responses, conduct interviews, or otherwise represent the party
4. Redirect correspondence to the domestic representative or applicant/registrant's address of record

If an Office action is outstanding, the examining attorney must issue a supplemental action addressed to the applicant/registrant with a new six-month response period.

## 609 -- Correspondence, With Whom Held

### 609.01 -- Establishing the Correspondence Address

When a trademark application is filed, the USPTO enters into its automated records an address where correspondence will be sent. This often differs from the applicant's address.

**Priority hierarchy for correspondence:**

1. If a written power of attorney is filed, correspondence goes to the practitioner designated in the power
2. If a qualified practitioner transmits documents on behalf of an unrepresented applicant, correspondence goes to that practitioner
3. If no practitioner is involved but the applicant designates a correspondence address in writing, the USPTO sends correspondence to that address
4. If no practitioner and no designated address but a domestic representative has been appointed, correspondence goes to the domestic representative
5. If none of the above, the USPTO sends correspondence directly to the applicant/registrant

Once the USPTO recognizes a qualified practitioner, it will communicate and conduct business only with that practitioner or another from the same firm. The USPTO will not undertake double correspondence.

### 609.01(a) -- Correspondence in Section 66(a) Applications

The first Office action in a section 66(a) application is sent to the International Bureau (IB), which forwards it to the applicant. Subsequent Office actions are sent directly to the applicant at the correspondence address in the request for extension of protection or at any properly changed address.

### 609.02 -- Changing the Correspondence Address

The mere transmittal of a response bearing a new address does not change the correspondence address, except when a new owner takes an action (files a response or statement of use) after recordation of a change of ownership.

A written request to change the correspondence address does **not** revoke a power of attorney.

### 609.02(a) -- Request to Change Correspondence Address Presumed

The USPTO will presume a request to change the correspondence address when:

1. A qualified practitioner transmits documents on behalf of an applicant not already represented by another practitioner from a different firm (however, filing an "associate power of attorney" does not change the address)
2. An applicant or registrant files a properly signed power of attorney designating a new qualified practitioner

### 609.02(b) -- Requirements for Request to Change Correspondence Address

The request must be:
- In writing
- Signed by the recognized qualified practitioner (or another from the same firm), or by the individual applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind them
- In the case of joint applicants/registrants not represented by a practitioner, signed by all

The correspondence address cannot be changed by examiner's amendment. Changes should be filed through TEAS for expedited processing.

### 609.02(c) -- Processing Before Registration

When filed through TEAS, the data is input directly into automated records. When filed on paper, the USPTO manually enters the new address but does not send a confirmation. The applicant may check TARR to verify the change.

The USPTO will not process a change of address in an abandoned application unless submitted with a petition to revive.

### 609.02(d) -- Changing Address in Multiple Applications or Registrations

The TEAS form can change the correspondence address in up to twenty (20) applications or registrations at one time. For registered marks, the form requires the original application serial number. Global address updates (changing an address in every application/registration in which it appears) will not be accepted.

### 609.02(e) -- Changing Correspondence Address After Registration

The USPTO reestablishes the correspondence address upon examination of a post-registration affidavit, renewal application, or section 7 request, even without a new power or written request to change. Owners of registrations are encouraged to promptly notify the USPTO of address changes through TEAS.

The USPTO will not process an address change in a cancelled or expired registration unless submitted with a petition or request for reinstatement.

### 609.02(f) -- Correspondence After Recordation of Change of Ownership

The USPTO considers a power of attorney to end when ownership changes. 37 C.F.R. section 2.17(g). Recording an assignment with the Assignment Services Branch does not automatically update the correspondence address in Trademark Operation records.

New owners should file a separate written request through TEAS to change the correspondence address. If the new owner instead takes an action bearing a new address, the USPTO may update records to reflect the new owner's or new practitioner's address.

### 609.03 -- Duty to Maintain Current Correspondence Address

The owner of an application or registration has a duty to maintain a current and accurate correspondence address. Changes must be promptly communicated to the USPTO in writing, preferably through TEAS. This includes e-mail addresses.

### 609.04 -- Correspondence with Parties Not Domiciled in the United States

For foreign applicants/registrants without a clearly stated correspondence address:
- If a qualified practitioner has been appointed, correspondence goes to the practitioner
- If no practitioner but a domestic representative has been appointed, correspondence goes to the domestic representative (though the domestic representative is not authorized to prosecute the application unless also recognized as a qualified practitioner)
- If no practitioner and no domestic representative, correspondence goes directly to the foreign address

## 610 -- Designation of Domestic Representative by Parties Not Domiciled in the United States

Under 37 C.F.R. section 2.24, an applicant not domiciled in the United States may designate a domestic representative -- a person residing in the United States on whom notices or process in proceedings affecting the mark may be served.

**Key points:**

- Designation is encouraged but not mandatory
- The domestic representative may be a natural person or a juristic person
- The designation is **not** the same as a power of attorney; a domestic representative is not authorized to practice before the USPTO, prepare or prosecute the application, or represent a party unless separately recognized as a qualified practitioner
- Similarly, a power of attorney does not serve as a designation of domestic representative unless it specifically states the attorney may receive service of process

**Requirements**: Must include the name and address of the person designated and a clear statement of intent to designate them as domestic representative for service of process. 37 C.F.R. section 2.24(a)(1).

**Signature**: Must be signed by the applicant/registrant, someone with legal authority to bind them, or a qualified practitioner. Joint applicants/registrants not represented by a practitioner must all sign.

**Duration**: Remains in effect unless specifically revoked or supplanted by appointment of a new domestic representative.

**Revocation**: Must be signed by the individual applicant/registrant, someone with legal authority to bind them, or a qualified practitioner.

**Withdrawal**: A domestic representative may withdraw in a written document signed by the individual representative or someone authorized to bind a juristic representative.

## 611 -- Signature on Correspondence Filed in the USPTO

### 611.01 -- Signature and Certificate

#### 611.01(a) -- Signature as Certificate

Under 37 C.F.R. section 2.193(f), the presentation of any document to the USPTO by any person constitutes a certification under section 11.18(b). The signer certifies that:

1. All statements of the party's own knowledge are true and believed to be true
2. The paper is not being presented for any improper purpose
3. Legal contentions are warranted by existing law or a nonfrivolous argument
4. Factual allegations have evidentiary support or are likely to after investigation
5. Denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence

Violations may jeopardize the validity of the application or registration and may result in sanctions including striking the paper, referral to OED, preclusion from presenting issues, affecting the weight of evidence, or terminating proceedings.

#### 611.01(b) -- Requirements for Signature

All correspondence requiring a signature must bear either:
- A handwritten signature in permanent ink by the named signatory; **or**
- An "electronic signature" meeting 37 C.F.R. section 2.193(c) requirements

All documents must be personally signed. Another person (e.g., paralegal, legal assistant, secretary) may not sign the name of an attorney or other authorized signatory. The signatory's name must be set forth in printed or typed form immediately below or adjacent to the signature, or identified elsewhere in the filing.

#### 611.01(c) -- Signature of Documents Filed Electronically

For TEAS or ESTTA filings, the signatory enters any combination of letters, numbers, spaces, and/or punctuation marks adopted as a signature, placed between two forward slash ("/") symbols. Examples: /john doe/, /drl/, /544-4925/.

Alternative methods:
1. The document is completed online, printed, signed by hand, scanned as .jpg or .pdf, and attached to the electronic submission
2. The document is completed online and e-mailed to the signatory from within TEAS for electronic signature, then automatically returned

The USPTO will also accept the forward-slash format on paper documents.

### 611.02 -- Signatures by Authorized Parties Required

All documents filed in the USPTO must be properly signed. The USPTO staff must review the record to determine whether the applicant or registrant is represented by a qualified practitioner and ensure all documents are properly signed.

**General guidelines:**
- If represented by a qualified practitioner, the practitioner must sign (except where the owner is required to sign, e.g., verifications)
- If not represented, the individual applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind them must sign
- Joint applicants/registrants not represented by a practitioner must all sign

#### 611.02(a) -- TEAS Checkoff Boxes

On certain TEAS forms, the person signing must confirm authorization by clicking one of three buttons indicating they are: (1) an unrepresented applicant/registrant; (2) an attorney member in good standing of the bar of a U.S. state; or (3) an authorized Canadian attorney or agent recognized by OED. The USPTO will accept these statements unless there is conflicting information in the record.

### 611.03 -- Proper Person to Sign

#### 611.03(a) -- Verification

A verification must be signed by the owner or a person properly authorized to verify facts on behalf of the owner:
- A person with legal authority to bind the owner
- A person with firsthand knowledge of the facts and actual or implied authority to act on behalf of the owner
- A qualified practitioner with an actual written or verbal power of attorney or implied power from the owner

The broad definition of "person properly authorized to sign on behalf of an owner" applies **only** to verifications (e.g., applications, affidavits under sections 8 or 71, statements of use, claims of acquired distinctiveness). It does **not** apply to responses to Office actions, amendments, petitions, or changes of correspondence address.

#### 611.03(b) -- Responses, Amendments, Requests for Express Abandonment, Reconsideration, and Requests to Divide

These documents must be signed by:
- The qualified practitioner (if represented) -- except where the owner must sign; **or**
- The individual applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind them (if not represented); joint applicants/registrants must all sign

#### 611.03(c) -- Powers of Attorney and Revocations

Must be signed by the individual applicant/registrant or someone with legal authority to bind them. Joint applicants/registrants must all sign. Once a practitioner is designated, the named practitioner may sign associate powers appointing additional practitioners.

#### 611.03(d) -- Petitions to Revive

Must be signed by someone with firsthand knowledge of the facts regarding unintentional delay. Any accompanying response to an Office action must be signed by a qualified practitioner or (if unrepresented) the individual applicant/registrant or someone with binding authority.

#### 611.03(e) -- Petitions to the Director

Must be signed by the petitioner, someone with legal authority to bind the petitioner, or a qualified practitioner.

#### 611.03(f) -- Amendment, Correction, or Surrender of Registration

Must be signed by the individual owner, someone with legal authority to bind the owner, or a qualified practitioner. Joint owners not represented by a practitioner must all sign.

#### 611.03(g) -- Renewal Applications

Must be signed by the registrant or the registrant's representative.

#### 611.03(h) -- Designations and Revocations of Domestic Representative

May be signed by the individual applicant/registrant, someone with legal authority to bind them, or a qualified practitioner. Joint applicants/registrants not represented by a practitioner must all sign.

#### 611.03(i) -- Requests to Change Correspondence Address

Must be signed by the individual applicant/registrant, someone with legal authority to bind them, or a qualified practitioner, following the same guidelines as other documents.

### 611.04 -- Examples of Authorized and Potentially Unauthorized Signatories

**Authorized Parties** (signatory presumed authorized):
- Officer of a pro se corporate applicant (President, VP, Treasurer, Secretary) or equivalent for other entities (Partner, Member, Principal)
- Attorney in good standing of a U.S. state bar signing for a previously pro se applicant
- Person signing for a foreign corporation as "general counsel" or "in-house counsel" **and** a corporate officer
- Attorney with a U.S. law firm or identified as in-house counsel of a U.S. applicant
- Different attorney from the same U.S.-based firm as the attorney of record
- Canadian trademark attorney or agent recognized by OED
- "Officer" of a pro se corporate applicant

**Potentially Unauthorized Parties** (signatory presumed unauthorized):
- Person with no title or position stated
- Foreign attorney ("solicitor," "barrister"), or foreign law consultant in a U.S. firm
- Non-attorney representing applicant as accountant, paralegal, trademark administrator, business manager, personal assistant, or legal secretary
- Person providing a foreign firm address
- Person identified as "attorney for applicant" for a foreign entity without specifying a U.S. address or firm
- Person identified as "attorney-in-fact"
- In-house counsel or general counsel of a foreign entity with no indication of U.S. bar membership or USPTO recognition
- Person identified as "representative of," "agent for," or "representing" with no other explanation
- "Authorized signatory" with no indication of relationship to applicant/registrant
- New attorney from a different firm when the previous practitioner's power has not been revoked

### 611.05 -- Processing Documents Signed by Unauthorized Persons

When a response to an Office action appears signed by an improper party, the USPTO must treat it as an incomplete response and grant additional time to perfect it. 37 C.F.R. section 2.65(b).

When a non-responsive document (e.g., amendment, petition, letter of express abandonment) is signed by an improper party, the USPTO will notify the applicant that no action will be taken unless the signatory's authority is established or a properly signed document is submitted.

#### 611.05(a) -- Notice of Incomplete Response

If a response to an Office action appears signed by an improper party, the examining attorney must issue a notice of incomplete response granting additional time to perfect it. The applicant must submit a response signed by the proper party.

#### 611.05(b) -- Replying to a Notice of Incomplete Response

A proper reply should state the nature of the signer's relationship to the applicant/registrant. If the signer has legal authority to bind the applicant/registrant, the reply should state this and set forth the title or position.

In a pending application, if the improperly signed response contains only amendments resolvable by examiner's amendment, the applicant or authorized person may telephone to authorize such an amendment.

#### 611.05(c) -- Unsatisfactory Response or Failure to Respond

In a pending application, if an unsatisfactory response or no response is received to the notice of incomplete response, the USPTO will abandon the application. In post-registration matters, the USPTO will notify the registrant that the filing remains unacceptable and the registration will be cancelled.

### 611.06 -- Guidelines on Persons With Legal Authority to Bind Certain Juristic Entities

#### 611.06(a) -- Joint Owners

Joint owners are individual parties. Documents must be signed by all owners.

#### 611.06(b) -- Partnership

A general partner must sign. Signature by all general partners is not necessary. If the partnership is comprised of juristic entities, someone with authority to bind that entity must sign.

#### 611.06(c) -- Joint Venture

Each party to the joint venture must sign, unless a general manager or other official is stated to have legal authority to bind the venture under relevant state law.

#### 611.06(d) -- Corporation

A corporate officer must sign. Officers include President, Vice-President, Secretary, Treasurer, CEO, COO, CFO. Modifications of these titles (e.g., Executive VP, Assistant Treasurer) are acceptable. "Chairman" or "Chairman of the Board of Directors" is also acceptable.

Titles generally **not** accepted: General Manager, or any other type of manager (these are usually employees, not officers). However, if the applicant states the person is authorized to bind the corporation under its bylaws or articles of incorporation, the USPTO will accept the signature.

A corporation cannot delegate signing authority to someone who is not a corporate officer.

#### 611.06(e) -- Foreign Companies and Corporations

The USPTO will accept the signature of a person considered to be equivalent to an officer under the law of the foreign country. Titles such as "Legal Officer," "Information Officer," "Manager," "Director," "Procurist," "Registrar," and "Confidential Clerk" are accepted for foreign entities.

#### 611.06(f) -- Unincorporated Association

For less formally organized entities (fraternal societies, unions, unincorporated associations, governmental bodies), the USPTO will accept the signature if the record shows the signer has authority equivalent to that of a corporate officer within the organization's framework. Accepted titles have included Director, National Director, National Commander, Permanent Chairman, International Sponsor, Supreme Ruler, Royal Impresario, and Chairman of the Steering Committee.

#### 611.06(g) -- Limited Liability Company

A "manager" generally has authority equivalent to a corporate officer and may sign. Anyone with a corporate-officer-type title may also sign. In some states, members (owners) also have authority to act on behalf of the LLC.

#### 611.06(h) -- Limited Liability Partnership

All partners are generally recognized as having general agency authority. Anyone identified as a partner may sign. If the individual is not a named partner but has been recognized as having authority to bind the LLP, that person may sign and should indicate their position as designated in the LLP's Statement of Partnership Authority.
