## Purpose

This document provides Tina with comprehensive knowledge of depreciation methods, cost recovery strategies, and related tax provisions. Proper depreciation planning can dramatically affect cash flow and tax liability. All thresholds reflect 2025/2026 parameters.

## MACRS — Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System

### Overview

MACRS is the primary depreciation system used for federal income tax purposes in the United States. It applies to most tangible depreciable property placed in service after 1986.

### Two Sub-Systems

**General Depreciation System (GDS)** — default and most commonly used
- Shorter recovery periods
- Accelerated methods (200% or 150% declining balance)
- Used unless ADS is required or elected

**Alternative Depreciation System (ADS)** — required in certain circumstances
- Longer recovery periods (generally based on class life)
- Straight-line method only
- Required for: property used predominantly outside the US, tax-exempt use property, tax-exempt bond-financed property, listed property not meeting >50% business use test, farming property if electing out of 263A, real property for earnings and profits calculations
- Elected for: QIP if electing out of interest expense limitation (Section 163(j)), real property trades or businesses making Section 163(j) election

### Recovery Periods (GDS)

| Property Class | Examples | Method |
|---------------|----------|--------|
| 3-year | Tractor units, racehorses (2+ years), special tools | 200% DB |
| 5-year | Automobiles, trucks, computers, office equipment, appliances, carpet, furniture (not structural) | 200% DB |
| 7-year | Office furniture, fixtures, agricultural machinery, any property without a designated class | 200% DB |
| 10-year | Water vessels, single-purpose agricultural structures, fruit trees/vines | 200% DB |
| 15-year | Land improvements (sidewalks, roads, fences, landscaping, parking lots, bridges), QIP, retail motor fuel outlets | 150% DB |
| 20-year | Farm buildings, municipal sewers (not 25-year water utility) | 150% DB |
| 25-year | Water utility property | Straight-line |
| 27.5-year | Residential rental property | Straight-line |
| 39-year | Nonresidential real property (commercial buildings) | Straight-line |

### Convention Rules

- **Half-year convention** (default): property placed in service or disposed of during the year is treated as placed in service/disposed at the midpoint of the year
- **Mid-quarter convention**: applies if more than 40% of total depreciable basis of property placed in service during the year is placed in service in Q4; all property is treated as placed in service at the midpoint of its respective quarter
  - Strategy: avoid triggering mid-quarter convention by ensuring Q4 acquisitions do not exceed 40% of total annual acquisitions; if triggered, it reduces first-year depreciation for Q4 property
- **Mid-month convention**: applies to real property (27.5-year and 39-year); property treated as placed in service at midpoint of the month

### Practical Application

- Always check whether an asset qualifies for a shorter recovery period than the default 7-year class
- Use MACRS percentage tables (IRS Publication 946) for exact depreciation amounts
- Switch from declining balance to straight-line when straight-line produces a larger deduction (the tables do this automatically)
- Track each asset's class, recovery period, method, convention, and placed-in-service date

## Section 179 Expensing

### Overview

Section 179 allows businesses to elect to deduct the full cost of qualifying property in the year placed in service, rather than depreciating over the recovery period.

### 2025/2026 Limits

- **Maximum deduction**: $1,250,000 (indexed annually for inflation)
- **Investment limit (phase-out threshold)**: $3,130,000 — the $1,250,000 deduction is reduced dollar-for-dollar for total property placed in service exceeding this amount
- **Complete phase-out**: at $4,380,000 in total qualifying property, no Section 179 deduction available
- **Taxable income limitation**: the 179 deduction cannot exceed the aggregate taxable income from all active trades or businesses (including W-2 wages of the taxpayer)
  - Excess carries forward indefinitely until sufficient taxable income exists

### Qualifying Property

- Tangible personal property (machinery, equipment, furniture)
- Off-the-shelf computer software
- Qualified improvement property (QIP)
- Certain improvements to nonresidential real property: roofs, HVAC, fire protection/alarm, security systems
- New or used property (used property became eligible under TCJA)
- Must be purchased for use in the active conduct of a trade or business

### Non-Qualifying Property

- Real property (land, buildings — except QIP and specific improvements listed above)
- Property used outside the US
- Property acquired from related parties (Section 179(d)(2))
- Property used 50% or less for business (listed property rule)
- Air conditioning and heating units (unless QIP)
- Property held for investment or rental (unless the rental is a trade or business)

### Vehicle Limitations

- **Passenger automobiles** (under 6,000 lbs GVWR): Section 179 deduction limited by Section 280F luxury auto limits
  - 2025 first-year limit (with bonus depreciation): approximately $20,400
  - 2025 first-year limit (without bonus depreciation): approximately $12,400
- **SUVs and trucks over 6,000 lbs GVWR but under 14,000 lbs**: Section 179 limited to $30,500 (2025)
  - Remaining cost eligible for bonus depreciation or regular MACRS
- **Vehicles over 14,000 lbs GVWR**: no Section 179 dollar limit (only the general $1,250,000 cap applies); full bonus depreciation also available
  - Strategy: vehicles over 14,000 lbs (certain heavy trucks, buses) can be fully expensed

### Strategic Use

- Use Section 179 to control the exact amount of first-year deduction (unlike bonus depreciation, which is all-or-nothing by class)
- Combine with bonus depreciation: elect Section 179 for specific assets, apply bonus depreciation to remaining qualified assets
- If income is expected to be higher in future years, consider skipping 179 and using regular MACRS to preserve deductions for higher-rate years
- The taxable income limitation makes 179 less useful for businesses with losses or low income — use bonus depreciation instead (which can create an NOL)

## Bonus Depreciation (Section 168(k))

### Overview

Bonus depreciation allows a first-year deduction of a specified percentage of the cost of qualifying property. Unlike Section 179, it can create or increase a net operating loss.

### Phase-Down Schedule

| Property Placed in Service | Bonus Percentage |
|---------------------------|-----------------|
| After September 27, 2017 through December 31, 2026 | 100% |
| Calendar year 2027 | 80% |
| Calendar year 2028 | 60% |
| Calendar year 2029 | 40% |
| Calendar year 2030 | 20% |
| After December 31, 2030 | 0% |

Note: For property with longer production periods (aircraft, certain vessels) and certain plants, the phase-down is delayed by one year.

### Qualifying Property

- MACRS property with a recovery period of 20 years or less
- Computer software (Section 167(f)(1))
- Water utility property
- Qualified film, television, and live theatrical productions
- Qualified improvement property (QIP) — 15-year MACRS, eligible for bonus depreciation (corrected by CARES Act)
- New AND used property (used property became eligible under TCJA; must be new to the taxpayer)
- No requirement that the property be new — but cannot be acquired from a related party or in certain nonrecognition transactions

### Property NOT Eligible

- Property with recovery periods longer than 20 years (27.5-year residential rental, 39-year commercial)
- Property required to use ADS (except QIP and certain other categories)
- Property for which the taxpayer elects out of bonus depreciation (election made by class of property, for all property in that class placed in service during the year)
- Floor plan financing interest property
- Listed property not meeting >50% business use test

### Election Out

- A taxpayer can elect out of bonus depreciation on a class-by-class basis
- Why elect out? To preserve deductions for future higher-income years, to avoid triggering AMT (less relevant post-TCJA), or for state tax purposes (many states do not conform to 100% bonus depreciation)
- Election is irrevocable for the tax year once made

### Interaction with Section 179

- Apply Section 179 first, then bonus depreciation on the remaining basis
- Section 179 is elected per asset; bonus depreciation applies per class of property
- Section 179 is limited by taxable income; bonus depreciation has no income limit
- Strategy: use Section 179 to fine-tune the deduction amount, then let bonus depreciation handle the rest

## Listed Property Rules (Section 280F)

### Definition

Listed property includes assets that are commonly used for both business and personal purposes:
- Passenger automobiles (under 6,000 lbs GVWR)
- Entertainment, recreation, and amusement property
- Computers and peripherals (removed from listed property for tax years after 2017 under TCJA)
- Cell phones (removed from listed property under TCJA)

### Key Rules

- Must use the asset more than 50% for business to claim Section 179 or bonus depreciation
- If business use is 50% or less: must use ADS straight-line depreciation; no Section 179 or bonus depreciation
- If business use drops to 50% or less in a subsequent year: recapture excess depreciation claimed in prior years (difference between accelerated depreciation claimed and straight-line ADS depreciation)
- Must substantiate business use percentage with adequate records (mileage logs for vehicles, usage logs for other property)

### Luxury Auto Limits (Section 280F) — 2025 Approximate Limits

**With Bonus Depreciation**
| Year | Limit |
|------|-------|
| Year 1 | $20,400 |
| Year 2 | $19,800 |
| Year 3 | $11,900 |
| Year 4+ | $7,160 |

**Without Bonus Depreciation**
| Year | Limit |
|------|-------|
| Year 1 | $12,400 |
| Year 2 | $19,800 |
| Year 3 | $11,900 |
| Year 4+ | $7,160 |

- After the recovery period ends, any unrecovered basis continues to be deducted at the Year 4+ rate until fully recovered
- These limits are multiplied by the business use percentage

## Cost Segregation Studies

### What It Is

A cost segregation study is an engineering-based analysis that reclassifies components of a building from 39-year (commercial) or 27.5-year (residential rental) property into shorter-lived asset classes (5, 7, or 15-year property), allowing accelerated depreciation.

### How It Works

- An engineer identifies building components that qualify as personal property or land improvements rather than structural components
- Examples of reclassified items:
  - **5-year property**: carpeting, decorative lighting, certain electrical (dedicated circuits for equipment), specialized plumbing, security systems
  - **7-year property**: certain fixtures, cabinetry, signage, specialty flooring
  - **15-year property**: site improvements (parking lots, sidewalks, landscaping, fences, retaining walls, site drainage, exterior lighting)
- Reclassified property becomes eligible for Section 179 and bonus depreciation

### When to Perform

- New construction or acquisition of commercial or residential rental property exceeding $1 million (general rule of thumb; smaller projects can still benefit)
- Significant renovations or tenant improvements
- Lookback studies: can be performed on previously acquired property; file Form 3115 to change depreciation method and claim cumulative catch-up adjustment in the current year (Section 481(a) adjustment)

### Cost-Benefit Analysis

- Typical study costs: $5,000–$25,000+ depending on property value and complexity
- Typical benefit: 15–45% of the building's depreciable basis can be reclassified
- With 100% bonus depreciation, reclassified components generate immediate deductions
- ROI is often 5:1 to 15:1 or higher

### Practical Application

- Always consider cost segregation for commercial property purchases and new construction
- Lookback studies can generate significant current-year deductions on properties placed in service years ago
- Document the engineering analysis thoroughly — IRS Audit Techniques Guide specifically addresses cost segregation
- Partial disposition election: when components identified in a cost segregation study are later replaced, the remaining basis of the old component can be written off as a loss

## Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)

### Definition

QIP is any improvement to an interior portion of a nonresidential building if the improvement is placed in service after the building was first placed in service by any taxpayer. Does NOT include: enlargement of the building, elevators/escalators, or internal structural framework.

### Recovery Period and Method

- 15-year MACRS (GDS), 150% declining balance
- 20-year ADS (straight-line)
- Eligible for Section 179 and bonus depreciation

### History

- The CARES Act (2020) retroactively corrected the TCJA's failure to assign QIP a 15-year life (it had inadvertently been assigned 39 years); applies retroactively to property placed in service after December 31, 2017
- Taxpayers who depreciated QIP over 39 years can file Form 3115 to change to 15-year and claim a Section 481(a) catch-up adjustment

### Practical Application

- Tenant improvements to leased commercial space typically qualify as QIP
- Restaurant, retail, and office build-outs benefit significantly
- Combine QIP classification with bonus depreciation for immediate deduction
- Track which improvements are QIP vs. building structural components carefully

## Amortization of Intangible Assets (Section 197)

### Section 197 Intangibles (15-Year Amortization)

- Goodwill and going concern value
- Workforce in place
- Business books and records, operating systems
- Patents, copyrights, formulas, processes, designs (if acquired as part of a business)
- Customer-based intangibles (customer lists, relationships)
- Supplier-based intangibles
- Licenses, permits, and other government-granted rights
- Covenants not to compete (entered into in connection with a business acquisition)
- Franchises, trademarks, and trade names

### Key Rules

- Amortized ratably over 15 years (180 months) beginning with the month of acquisition
- **Anti-churning rules**: cannot amortize goodwill or going concern value that was held by the taxpayer or related party before August 10, 1993
- **Self-created intangibles**: generally NOT Section 197 intangibles (exception: certain self-created items like covenants not to compete)
- If a Section 197 intangible is disposed of at a loss and other Section 197 intangibles from the same acquisition are retained, the loss is disallowed and added to the basis of the remaining intangibles (anti-loss rule)

### Non-Section 197 Intangibles

- Software (separately acquired, not as part of a business): amortized over 36 months under Section 167(f) or expensed under Section 179
- Computer software developed internally: capitalized under Section 174 and amortized over 5 years (domestic) or 15 years (foreign) as of 2022
- Patents, copyrights not acquired as part of a business: amortized over useful life
- Loan origination costs: amortized over the loan term

## Depreciation Recapture

### Section 1245 Property (Personal Property)

- All gain on disposition of Section 1245 property is recaptured as ordinary income to the extent of prior depreciation claimed
- Applies to: machinery, equipment, vehicles, furniture, and other tangible personal property
- Any gain exceeding total depreciation claimed is Section 1231 gain (potentially long-term capital gain)
- This is a 100% recapture rule — all depreciation is recaptured as ordinary income before any capital gain treatment

### Section 1250 Property (Real Property)

- Recapture of depreciation on real property (buildings) as ordinary income to the extent depreciation exceeds straight-line depreciation
- Since MACRS requires straight-line for real property, Section 1250 recapture is generally zero for post-1986 property
- However, **unrecaptured Section 1250 gain** is taxed at a maximum rate of 25% (the portion of gain attributable to depreciation claimed, to the extent it exceeds Section 1250 ordinary income recapture)
- Example: building purchased for $500,000, depreciated $100,000, sold for $700,000 — $100,000 of the $300,000 gain is unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (taxed at 25% max), $200,000 is Section 1231 gain (taxed at capital gain rates)

### Recapture Implications for Planning

- Section 179 and bonus depreciation accelerate deductions but increase recapture exposure upon sale
- Cost segregation studies reclassify real property components as personal property (Section 1245) — upon sale, these components face full ordinary income recapture rather than the favorable 25% rate
- 1031 exchanges defer recapture — but it is not eliminated; recapture carries forward to the replacement property
- Installment sales: depreciation recapture is recognized in the year of sale regardless of installment payments (Section 453(i))

## Depreciation Decision Framework

### Step 1: Classify the Asset
- Determine the MACRS class and recovery period
- Is it listed property? If so, verify >50% business use
- Is it real property? Consider cost segregation

### Step 2: Consider Section 179
- Is the asset qualifying property?
- Is there sufficient taxable income to support the deduction?
- Do you want to control the specific deduction amount?

### Step 3: Apply Bonus Depreciation
- Is the asset eligible for bonus depreciation?
- What is the current bonus percentage?
- Should you elect out for this class of property?

### Step 4: Default to MACRS
- Apply regular MACRS depreciation to any remaining basis
- Use GDS unless ADS is required or elected
- Apply the appropriate convention (half-year, mid-quarter, mid-month)

### Step 5: Document Everything
- Asset description, cost, placed-in-service date, class life, method, convention
- Business use percentage (if listed property)
- Elections made (Section 179, elect out of bonus, ADS)
- Maintain fixed asset register with all details

## Notes for Tina

- Depreciation strategy should be coordinated with overall tax planning — maximizing deductions is not always optimal (consider future tax rate expectations, AMT, state implications)
- The bonus depreciation phase-down creates urgency for large asset purchases — plan timing carefully
- Cost segregation studies are one of the highest-ROI tax planning tools for real estate — recommend for every commercial property acquisition
- Always consider the recapture consequences of accelerated depreciation before recommending aggressive expensing
- State depreciation conformity varies significantly — track both federal and state depreciation schedules
- Form 3115 (change in accounting method) is the mechanism for correcting or changing depreciation methods — it is a powerful tool but must be filed correctly
