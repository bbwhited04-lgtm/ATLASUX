# Tina KB: Tax Planning Strategies

## Purpose

This document provides Tina with actionable tax planning strategies for businesses and their owners. Strategies are organized by category with specific implementation guidance, applicable limits, and timing considerations. All figures reflect 2025/2026 tax year parameters unless otherwise noted.

---

## Income Deferral Strategies

### Cash Method Timing

- Delay sending invoices in Q4 to push income into the following tax year
- Applies to cash-basis taxpayers only (most small businesses, service businesses)
- Limitation: constructive receipt doctrine — income is taxable when it is made available to you without substantial limitations, regardless of whether you actually receive it
- Do not have a client hold a check — if it is available to you, it is constructively received

### Installment Sales (Section 453)

- Defer gain on the sale of property by receiving payments over multiple years
- Gain recognized proportionally as payments are received (gross profit percentage x payment)
- Does not apply to: inventory, publicly traded securities, or depreciation recapture (recapture recognized in year of sale regardless)
- Related party rules: if buyer is a related party and resells within 2 years, all deferred gain accelerates

### Like-Kind Exchanges (Section 1031)

- Defer 100% of capital gain on real property by exchanging into like-kind replacement property
- See tina-irs-code.md for detailed requirements and timelines
- Can chain multiple 1031 exchanges over a career, deferring gain until ultimate disposition (or stepped-up basis at death)

### Qualified Opportunity Zones (Section 1400Z-2)

- Invest capital gains into Qualified Opportunity Zone Funds within 180 days of realization
- Benefits: deferral of original gain until December 31, 2026 (or earlier sale), and if held 10+ years, exclusion of gain on appreciation of the QOZ investment
- The original gain deferral benefit has been reduced (10% and 15% basis step-ups expired for investments after 2021)
- Primary remaining benefit: 10-year exclusion of appreciation on the QOZ investment itself
- Must invest in qualified opportunity zone property (business property or business) located in a designated census tract

### Deferred Compensation (Section 409A)

- Non-qualified deferred compensation plans allow executives to defer compensation to future years
- Must comply with Section 409A timing rules: election to defer must be made before the year in which services are performed (or within 30 days of initial eligibility)
- Distribution must be triggered by a permissible event: separation from service, fixed date, change in control, disability, death, unforeseeable emergency
- Penalties for non-compliance: 20% additional tax plus interest from the year of vesting
- Caution: deferred amounts are unsecured creditor claims — no protection in employer bankruptcy

## Expense Acceleration Strategies

### Section 179 Expensing

- Immediately deduct up to $1,250,000 (2025) of qualifying equipment, software, and property improvements
- Strategic use: accelerate purchases into the current year when taxable income is high
- Cannot create a business loss with Section 179 — excess carries forward
- See tina-irs-code.md and tina-depreciation-strategies.md for complete details

### Bonus Depreciation

- 100% first-year bonus depreciation for property placed in service through 2026
- Phase-down: 80% in 2027, 60% in 2028, 40% in 2029, 20% in 2030, 0% after
- Unlike Section 179, bonus depreciation CAN create a net operating loss
- Strategic timing: if phase-down is imminent, accelerate asset purchases to capture higher bonus percentages

### Prepaid Expenses

- Cash-basis taxpayers can deduct prepaid expenses if the benefit period does not extend beyond 12 months after the first date the benefit is received
- Examples: prepaying January rent in December, annual insurance premiums, 12-month subscriptions
- The 12-month rule limits this strategy — cannot prepay 2 years of rent and deduct it all
- Accrual-basis taxpayers: economic performance rules are more restrictive

### Retirement Plan Contributions

- Employer contributions to qualified plans (401(k), SEP, SIMPLE, defined benefit) are deductible in the year paid (if paid by the return due date including extensions for SEPs)
- Self-employed individuals: maximize contributions to solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA
- Defined benefit plans allow the highest contributions (actuarially determined; can exceed $200,000+ for older owners with high income)
- Contribution deadlines: SEP-IRA can be established and funded up to the tax return due date including extensions

### Research and Development (Section 174)

- As of 2022, R&D expenditures must be capitalized and amortized over 5 years (domestic) or 15 years (foreign) — no longer immediately deductible
- This is a significant cash flow impact for R&D-heavy businesses
- The R&D tax credit (Section 41) partially offsets this — see below

## Entity Selection for Tax Optimization

### Key Considerations

- **C-Corp**: 21% flat federal rate; dividends taxed again at shareholder level (0/15/20% + 3.8% NIIT); best for: reinvesting profits, QSBS eligibility, certain fringe benefits
- **S-Corp**: pass-through; salary + distributions structure; employment tax savings on distributions; 199A deduction available; best for: active businesses with profit distributions, moderate income levels
- **Partnership/LLC**: maximum flexibility; special allocations; self-employment tax on general partner's distributive share; 199A deduction available; best for: real estate, multi-owner ventures, complex allocation needs
- **Sole proprietorship**: simplest; all income subject to SE tax; 199A deduction available; best for: very small businesses with low risk

### Entity Conversion Strategies

- LLC to S-Corp: eliminates SE tax on distributions; requires reasonable salary; Form 2553 election
- S-Corp to C-Corp: may benefit from lower 21% rate if profits are reinvested; enables QSBS; revocation requires consent of shareholders holding >50% of shares
- C-Corp to S-Corp: built-in gains tax for 5 years on appreciated assets; LIFO recapture; careful planning needed
- Partnership to S-Corp: can be done by incorporating and electing S status or by checking the box

See tina-entity-structuring.md for comprehensive entity analysis.

## R&D Tax Credit (Section 41)

### Overview

- Credit for qualified research expenses (QREs): wages for qualified research, supplies, contract research (65%)
- Two calculation methods:
  - **Regular credit**: 20% of QREs exceeding a base amount (calculated from historical research spending/gross receipts ratio)
  - **Alternative simplified credit (ASC)**: 14% of QREs exceeding 50% of average QREs for prior 3 years
- Most taxpayers use the ASC method due to simplicity

### Small Business Benefits

- Qualified small businesses (≤$5M gross receipts, no gross receipts for any year before the 5-year period ending with the current year) can offset the R&D credit against payroll tax liability (up to $500,000 per year for tax years beginning after 2022, increased from $250,000 by the Inflation Reduction Act)
- This is extremely valuable for pre-revenue startups — the credit reduces quarterly payroll tax deposits

### Qualifying Activities (Four-Part Test)

1. Permitted purpose (new or improved function, performance, reliability, quality)
2. Technological in nature (relies on hard sciences, engineering, computer science)
3. Elimination of uncertainty (technical uncertainty about capability, method, or design)
4. Process of experimentation (systematic evaluation of alternatives)

### Practical Application

- Software development often qualifies if it involves genuine technical uncertainty (not routine development)
- Document the four-part test for each project — contemporaneous documentation is critical for audit defense
- Internal-use software has a higher threshold (innovation test under Reg 1.41-4(c)(6))
- Commonly missed QREs: wages of employees who supervise or support qualified research, cloud computing costs (if used as supplies in research)

## Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC)

### Overview

- Credit for hiring individuals from targeted groups who face employment barriers
- Credit amount: generally 40% of first-year wages up to $6,000 ($2,400 per employee); 25% if employee works 120-399 hours
- Long-term family assistance recipients: 40% of first and second year wages (up to $10,000 per year = $8,000 max credit)

### Target Groups

- TANF recipients, veterans (including disabled veterans — higher credit), ex-felons, designated community residents (Empowerment Zones), vocational rehabilitation referrals, SNAP recipients (ages 18-39), SSI recipients, long-term unemployment recipients (27+ weeks)
- Disabled veterans: credit up to $12,000 (40% of $24,000 first-year wages) if unemployed 6+ months

### Process

- Must pre-screen candidates using Form 8850 (submitted to state workforce agency within 28 days of start date)
- State workforce agency certifies eligibility
- Claim credit on Form 5884, reported on general business credit (Form 3800)

### Practical Application

- Integrate Form 8850 screening into the hiring process — the 28-day deadline is strict
- Works well with high-turnover industries (restaurants, retail, hospitality)
- Cannot claim for rehires, related individuals, or majority owners
- The credit reduces the wage deduction dollar-for-dollar (net tax benefit is the credit minus lost deduction)

## Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) Planning

### Individual AMT

- AMT exemption (2025): $88,100 (single), $137,000 (MFJ); phases out at $626,350 (single), $1,252,700 (MFJ)
- AMT rate: 26% on first $239,100 of AMTI; 28% on excess
- Common AMT preference/adjustment items: state and local tax deduction, incentive stock option (ISO) exercise bargain element, private activity bond interest, accelerated depreciation differences
- Planning strategies:
  - Time ISO exercises to minimize AMT impact (spread exercises across tax years)
  - If in AMT, accelerating income may have lower marginal cost (AMT rate of 26-28% vs. regular rate of 32-37%)
  - AMT credit carryforward from prior years: refundable to the extent AMT did not arise from deferral preferences

### Corporate AMT (CAMT)

- Applies to corporations with average annual adjusted financial statement income exceeding $1 billion (over 3-year period)
- 15% minimum tax on adjusted financial statement income (book income)
- Most small and mid-size businesses are not affected, but monitor growth trajectory

## Charitable Giving Strategies

### Cash Contributions

- Individuals: up to 60% of AGI for cash contributions to public charities; 30% for appreciated property; 20% for certain private foundations
- Corporations: up to 25% of taxable income (increased from 10% temporarily and extended)
- Five-year carryforward for excess contributions

### Appreciated Property

- Donate long-term capital gain property (held >1 year) at fair market value — deduct FMV without recognizing gain
- Particularly effective for highly appreciated stock, real estate, or art
- S-Corp/partnership: deduction passes through to owners

### Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)

- Contribute to a DAF for immediate deduction; recommend grants to charities over time
- Useful for bunching deductions: contribute 2-3 years' worth of charitable giving in one year to exceed standard deduction threshold
- No minimum distribution requirement (unlike foundations)

### Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

- For individuals age 70½+: distribute up to $105,000 (2025, indexed) directly from IRA to qualified charity
- Satisfies RMD requirement without including the amount in AGI
- Cannot be to a DAF or private foundation

### Conservation Easements

- Deduction for donating a qualified conservation easement (restriction on use of real property)
- Syndicated conservation easement transactions are under heavy IRS scrutiny — listed transactions requiring disclosure
- Only pursue legitimate conservation easements with qualified appraisals

## Retirement Plan Optimization

### Maximizing Tax-Deferred Savings

| Plan Type | Employee Limit (2025) | Employer Limit | Total |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|-------|
| 401(k) | $23,500 + $7,500 catch-up | Up to 25% of comp | $70,000 |
| SEP-IRA | N/A | 25% of comp (max $70,000) | $70,000 |
| SIMPLE IRA | $16,500 + $3,500 catch-up | 2% nonelective or 3% match | ~$20,000 |
| Solo 401(k) | $23,500 + $7,500 catch-up | 25% of net SE income | $70,000 |
| Defined Benefit | N/A | Actuarially determined | $280,000 benefit |

### Key Strategies

- **Mega backdoor Roth**: if 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions and in-service distributions, contribute beyond the $23,500 limit up to the $70,000 total limit, then convert to Roth
- **Cash balance plan**: hybrid defined benefit plan popular with professional practices; allows very large deductible contributions (often $100,000–$300,000+ depending on age and plan design)
- **SIMPLE to 401(k) conversion**: SECURE 2.0 allows mid-year replacement in certain circumstances
- **Roth conversion strategy**: convert traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth in low-income years; pay tax now at lower rates; future growth is tax-free

## State Tax Nexus Considerations

### Physical Presence vs. Economic Nexus

- Post-Wayfair (2018): states can impose sales tax obligations based on economic nexus (typically $100,000 in sales or 200 transactions in the state)
- Income tax nexus: varies by state; many states adopt economic nexus for income tax (factor presence thresholds, typically $500,000+ in sales, property, or payroll)
- Remote employees create nexus in the state where they work — this is an increasingly common trap

### State Tax Planning

- **PTE tax elections**: many states now allow pass-through entities to elect to pay state income tax at the entity level, generating a federal deduction that bypasses the $10,000 SALT cap
- **Apportionment optimization**: states use different formulas (single sales factor, three-factor, market-based sourcing vs. cost-of-performance); structure operations to minimize overall state tax
- **State R&D credits**: many states offer their own R&D credits in addition to federal — do not leave money on the table
- **State conformity**: not all states conform to federal provisions (QSBS exclusion, bonus depreciation, Section 199A); always check state treatment

### Compliance Requirements

- Register in all states where nexus exists
- File income tax returns and sales tax returns as required
- Track employee work locations (especially remote workers) for withholding and nexus purposes
- Consider voluntary disclosure agreements (VDAs) for past non-compliance — reduces penalties and limits lookback periods

## Notes for Tina

- Tax planning is most effective when done proactively — Q4 year-end planning is often too late for significant strategies
- Always model multiple scenarios: current year vs. multi-year impact; federal vs. state; entity-level vs. individual
- Document the business purpose and economic substance of every tax planning strategy
- Monitor legislative changes — the TCJA sunset (end of 2025) could dramatically change the landscape for 2026+
- Coordinate tax planning with cash flow needs — a tax-optimal strategy that creates a liquidity problem is not optimal
- For all strategies involving elections or deadlines, build in reminder systems — missed deadlines cannot be recovered in most cases
