## 1. Advanced Philosophy

### Philosophy of Mind
- **Mind-body problem**: How do mental states relate to physical states?
- **Physicalism/Materialism**: Mental states are physical states
  - **Type identity theory (Smart, Place)**: Mental types = brain types (pain = C-fiber firing)
  - **Token identity**: Each mental token = some physical token (allows multiple realizability)
  - **Functionalism (Putnam, early)**: Mental states defined by causal/functional role, not physical substrate
    - Machine functionalism: Mind as Turing machine, mental states = computational states
    - Objection: Chinese Room (Searle) — syntax ≠ semantics, computation alone insufficient for understanding
    - Objection: Inverted qualia — same functional role, different qualitative experience
  - **Eliminative materialism (Churchlands)**: Folk psychology is a false theory; "beliefs" and "desires" will be eliminated by neuroscience
- **Dualism**: Mental and physical are distinct substances or properties
  - **Substance dualism (Descartes)**: Mind and body are different substances; interaction problem
  - **Property dualism**: One substance, two kinds of properties (physical + phenomenal)
  - **Epiphenomenalism**: Mental properties caused by physical but causally inert
- **Hard problem of consciousness (Chalmers)**: Why is there subjective experience at all? Explaining neural correlates doesn't explain *what it's like*
  - **Zombie argument**: Conceivability of philosophical zombies (physically identical, no experience) → physicalism false?
  - **Knowledge argument (Jackson)**: Mary the color scientist learns something new upon seeing red → qualia are non-physical?
  - **Explanatory gap (Levine)**: Even complete physical knowledge leaves phenomenal consciousness unexplained
- **Intentionality**: Aboutness of mental states; Brentano's thesis: intentionality is the mark of the mental
  - **Externalism (Putnam, Burge)**: Mental content partly determined by environment ("Twin Earth" thought experiment)
  - **Internalism**: Mental content determined solely by internal states
- **Extended mind thesis (Clark & Chalmers)**: Cognitive processes can extend beyond the brain (notebook as memory)
- **Panpsychism**: Consciousness is fundamental and ubiquitous; Integrated Information Theory (Tononi, Φ)

### Philosophy of Language
- **Sense and reference (Frege)**: "Morning Star" and "Evening Star" have same reference (Venus), different sense
- **Definite descriptions (Russell)**: "The present King of France is bald" = ∃x(Kx ∧ ∀y(Ky→y=x) ∧ Bx)
  - Strawson's objection: Presupposition failure, not false — neither true nor false
- **Rigid designators (Kripke)**: Proper names refer to same individual in all possible worlds
  - Necessary a posteriori: "Hesperus is Phosphorus" is necessarily true but known empirically
  - Contingent a priori: "One meter is the length of S" (if S defines the meter) — knowable a priori but could have been different
- **Speech act theory**: Austin's performatives (saying = doing), Searle's taxonomy (assertives, directives, commissives, expressives, declarations)
- **Gricean pragmatics**: Meaning = speaker's communicative intention recognized by hearer
  - Natural vs non-natural meaning; conversational implicature vs conventional implicature
- **Compositionality (Frege's principle)**: Meaning of complex expression = function of meanings of parts + structure
  - Challenges: Idioms, context-sensitivity, metaphor
- **Truth-conditional semantics**: Meaning of sentence = conditions under which it is true (Davidson, Tarski)
  - Possible worlds semantics (Montague): Intension = function from worlds to extensions
- **Deflationism about truth**: "Snow is white" is true iff snow is white — truth is not a substantive property
- **Indeterminacy of translation (Quine)**: No fact of the matter about correct translation between languages
  - Gavagai: Could mean "rabbit," "undetached rabbit part," "rabbit stage"...
  - Inscrutability of reference: Reference is relative to a translation manual

### Philosophy of Science
- **Demarcation problem**: What distinguishes science from non-science?
  - **Falsificationism (Popper)**: Scientific theories must be falsifiable; corroboration ≠ confirmation
  - **Logical positivism (Vienna Circle)**: Verificationist criterion of meaning; synthetic a priori rejected
    - Collapsed: Verification principle itself unverifiable; observation is theory-laden
  - **Paradigm shifts (Kuhn)**: Normal science → anomalies → crisis → revolution → new paradigm
    - Incommensurability: Old and new paradigms not directly comparable
  - **Research programmes (Lakatos)**: Hard core + protective belt; progressive vs degenerating
  - **Anarchism (Feyerabend)**: "Anything goes" — no universal scientific method
- **Underdetermination (Duhem-Quine)**: Evidence never uniquely determines theory; auxiliary hypotheses can always be adjusted
- **Scientific realism vs anti-realism**:
  - **Realism**: Best theories are approximately true, theoretical entities exist
    - No miracles argument: Success of science would be miraculous if theories weren't approximately true
  - **Constructive empiricism (van Fraassen)**: Science aims at empirical adequacy, not truth about unobservables
  - **Social constructivism (Strong Programme)**: Scientific knowledge is socially constructed
- **Explanation**:
  - **Deductive-nomological (Hempel)**: Explanation = deduction from laws + initial conditions
    - Problems: Flagpole/shadow asymmetry, irrelevant information, statistical explanation
  - **Causal-mechanical (Salmon)**: Explanation traces causal mechanisms
  - **Unification (Kitcher)**: Best explanation unifies most phenomena under fewest argument patterns
  - **Inference to Best Explanation (IBE)**: Accept hypothesis that best explains evidence
- **Causation**:
  - **Regularity (Hume)**: Cause = constant conjunction + temporal priority + contiguity
  - **Counterfactual (Lewis)**: C caused E iff had C not occurred, E would not have occurred
    - Possible worlds: Closest world where C didn't happen, E doesn't happen
  - **Interventionist (Woodward)**: C causes E iff intervening on C changes E
  - **Probabilistic**: C raises probability of E, P(E|C) > P(E|¬C)

### Metaphysics (Advanced)
- **Possible worlds**: Modal realism (Lewis) — possible worlds are concrete realities; ersatzism — possible worlds are abstract representations
- **Personal identity**: Psychological continuity (Locke, Parfit) vs bodily continuity vs narrative identity
  - **Parfit's reductionism**: Identity is not what matters; what matters is psychological continuity/connectedness
  - **Fission cases**: If brain split into two bodies with continuous psychology, which (if either) is "you"?
- **Mereology**: Part-whole relations
  - **Unrestricted composition**: Any objects have a mereological sum (controversial)
  - **Nihilism**: Only simples exist (no composite objects)
  - **Vagueness**: Sorites paradox — when does a heap cease to be a heap?
- **Free will**:
  - **Libertarianism (metaphysical)**: Free will requires indeterminism, agent causation
  - **Compatibilism**: Free will compatible with determinism (Frankfurt, Dennett)
    - Frankfurt cases: Moral responsibility without alternative possibilities
  - **Hard determinism/Hard incompatibilism**: No free will if determinism true (Pereboom)
- **Abstract objects**: Platonism (numbers exist mind-independently) vs nominalism (only concrete particulars exist)
  - **Indispensability argument (Quine-Putnam)**: We ought to believe in entities indispensable to our best scientific theories

## 2. Advanced History & Historiography

### Historiographic Schools
- **Annales School (Bloch, Febvre, Braudel)**: Longue durée, mentalities, total history
  - Braudel's three temporalities: Geographic time (long), social time (medium), individual events (short)
- **Marxist historiography**: Base/superstructure, class struggle as motor of history, modes of production
  - E.P. Thompson: *Making of the English Working Class* — agency of working people, "experience"
  - Gramsci: Cultural hegemony, subaltern studies influenced by this
- **Postcolonial history**: Subaltern Studies Group (Guha, Spivak), provincializing Europe (Chakrabarty)
  - Spivak: "Can the Subaltern Speak?" — representation and epistemic violence
  - Orientalism (Said): Western construction of "the East" as Other
- **Microhistory**: Carlo Ginzburg (*The Cheese and the Worms*), Natalie Zemon Davis
  - Intensive study of single event/individual to illuminate broader structures
- **World-systems theory (Wallerstein)**: Core-periphery-semi-periphery, capitalist world-economy since 16th c.
- **New cultural history**: Representation, discourse, cultural practices (Hunt, Chartier, Darnton)
- **Digital humanities**: Computational text analysis, GIS mapping, network analysis of historical data
- **Environmental history**: Crosby (*Columbian Exchange*), Cronon (*Changes in the Land*), climate and history

### Debates in Periodization
- **"Fall" of Rome**: Gradual transformation vs catastrophic collapse (Ward-Perkins vs Wickham)
- **"Dark Ages"**: Contested term; "Early Medieval" preferred; continuity of learning in monasteries, Byzantium, Islamic world
- **Renaissance**: Continuity/discontinuity debate; Burckhardt's thesis of "individualism" vs medieval precedents
- **Modernity**: When does it begin? 1500? 1648? 1789? Multiple modernities thesis (Eisenstadt)
- **Anthropocene**: When does human geological impact begin? 1610? 1784? 1945? (Orbis spike, Industrial Revolution, Great Acceleration)

### Key Historical Debates
- **Transition from feudalism to capitalism**: Brenner debate — class relations (Brenner) vs demographic factors (Postan) vs commercialization (Sweezy, Wallerstein)
- **Industrial Revolution**: Why Britain first? Institutions (North), coal + colonies (Pomeranz), culture of improvement (Mokyr), wages/prices (Allen)
- **French Revolution causation**: Social (Lefebvre, class conflict), revisionist (Furet, political culture), cultural (Chartier, Hunt)
- **Holocaust historiography**: Intentionalist (Hitler's ideology drove it) vs functionalist (cumulative radicalization, bureaucratic dynamics) vs synthetic (Browning, Friedländer)
- **Decline of empires**: Comparative frameworks (Kennedy's "imperial overstretch"), climate (Harper on Rome), institutional (Acemoglu/Robinson)

## 3. Advanced Economics

### Microeconomic Theory
- **General equilibrium (Arrow-Debreu)**: Existence of competitive equilibrium under convexity, continuity assumptions
  - First Welfare Theorem: Competitive equilibrium is Pareto efficient (under assumptions)
  - Second Welfare Theorem: Any Pareto efficient allocation can be achieved as competitive equilibrium with appropriate lump-sum transfers
- **Social choice theory (Arrow)**: No voting system satisfies all: unrestricted domain, Pareto, IIA, non-dictatorship
  - Gibbard-Satterthwaite: Any non-dictatorial voting scheme with ≥3 alternatives is manipulable
- **Mechanism design (Hurwicz, Maskin, Myerson)**: Design rules to achieve desired outcomes given strategic agents
  - **Revelation principle**: Any equilibrium outcome achievable by a mechanism can be achieved by a truthful direct mechanism
  - **Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG)**: Truthful mechanism for allocating goods, agents pay externality they impose
  - **Myerson's optimal auction**: Revenue-maximizing auction with reserve price dependent on bidder distribution

### Behavioral Economics
- **Prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky)**: Loss aversion (losses loom ~2× larger than equivalent gains), reference dependence, diminishing sensitivity
  - Value function: Concave for gains, convex for losses, steeper for losses
  - Probability weighting: Overweight small probabilities, underweight large
- **Bounded rationality (Simon)**: Satisficing rather than optimizing; cognitive limitations
- **Hyperbolic discounting**: Present bias, preference reversal over time (β-δ model)
  - Explains procrastination, undersaving, addiction
- **Nudge theory (Thaler & Sunstein)**: Choice architecture, default effects, libertarian paternalism
- **Mental accounting**: People categorize money into separate mental accounts (violates fungibility)

### Econometrics (Advanced)
- **Endogeneity**: Omitted variables, simultaneity, measurement error → biased OLS
- **Instrumental variables (IV)**: Z is instrument for X if Cov(Z,X) ≠ 0 and Cov(Z,ε) = 0
  - 2SLS: First stage regress X on Z, second stage use X̂
  - Weak instruments: F-statistic < 10 → unreliable (Stock-Yogo)
- **Difference-in-differences (DiD)**: Compare treatment vs control before and after intervention
  - Parallel trends assumption: In absence of treatment, trends would be same
- **Regression discontinuity (RD)**: Causal effect from sharp cutoff in treatment assignment
  - Sharp: Treatment deterministic at cutoff; Fuzzy: Probability of treatment jumps at cutoff
- **Synthetic control method**: Construct weighted combination of control units to match treated unit pre-treatment
- **Panel data**: Fixed effects (within-estimator), random effects (GLS), Hausman test for choice
- **GMM (Generalized Method of Moments)**: Hansen (1982), efficient estimation with moment conditions
- **Maximum likelihood**: Estimate parameters by maximizing likelihood function; Fisher information matrix for standard errors

### Development Economics
- **Poverty traps**: Multiple equilibria, threshold effects (Sachs vs Easterly debate on aid effectiveness)
- **Randomized controlled trials (RCTs)**: Banerjee, Duflo, Kremer (Nobel 2019) — experimental approach to development policy
- **Institutions and growth**: Acemoglu, Johnson, Robinson — settler mortality → institutional quality → current GDP
  - Extractive vs inclusive institutions (*Why Nations Fail*)
- **Human capital**: Mincer equation: ln(wage) = β₀ + β₁·education + β₂·experience + β₃·experience²
- **Growth theory**: Solow model (steady state k* where sf(k) = (n+δ)k), Romer endogenous growth (ideas + R&D)

## 4. Advanced Political Science

### Democratic Theory
- **Deliberative democracy (Habermas)**: Legitimacy from rational discourse in public sphere
  - Ideal speech situation: No coercion, equal participation, only force of better argument
- **Agonistic democracy (Mouffe)**: Conflict is constitutive of politics; adversaries, not enemies
- **Epistocracy (Brennan)**: Competence-based restrictions on voting — controversial challenge to universal suffrage
- **Democratic backsliding**: Erosion of democratic norms by elected leaders (Hungary, Turkey); competitive authoritarianism (Levitsky & Way)

### Comparative Politics
- **Lijphart's typology**: Majoritarian (Westminster) vs consensus democracy
  - Consensus: Proportional representation, multiparty, coalition governments, federalism
  - Majoritarian: Single-member districts, two-party, winner-take-all, unitary
- **Varieties of capitalism (Hall & Soskice)**: Liberal market economies (US, UK) vs coordinated market economies (Germany, Japan)
  - Comparative institutional advantage: Different institutions support different industries
- **Welfare state regimes (Esping-Andersen)**: Liberal (US), conservative/corporatist (Germany), social democratic (Sweden)
- **Ethnic conflict**: Primordialist (fixed identities) vs constructivist (identities are socially constructed and politically mobilized)
  - Consociationalism (Lijphart): Power-sharing in divided societies

### International Relations (Advanced)
- **Neorealism (Waltz)**: Structure of international system (anarchy + distribution of power) determines state behavior
  - Offensive realism (Mearsheimer): States maximize power; hegemony is ultimate goal
  - Defensive realism: States seek security, not power maximization; security dilemma causes conflict
- **Neoliberal institutionalism (Keohane)**: Institutions reduce transaction costs, enable cooperation even under anarchy
  - Regime theory: Principles, norms, rules, procedures that govern issue areas
- **Constructivism (Wendt)**: "Anarchy is what states make of it" — social construction of interests and identities
  - Norm cascade model (Finnemore & Sikkink): Norm emergence → cascade → internalization
- **Critical theory (Frankfurt School applied to IR)**: Emancipation from structures of domination
  - Cox: "Theory is always for someone and for some purpose"
- **Securitization (Copenhagen School)**: Security is a speech act; issues become "security" when framed as existential threat
- **English School**: International society (states share norms), between realism and liberalism (Bull, Wight)

## 5. Advanced Psychology & Cognitive Science

### Cognitive Neuroscience
- **Default mode network (DMN)**: Active during rest/mind-wandering; medial PFC, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus
  - Anticorrelated with task-positive network during focused attention
- **Predictive processing/coding**: Brain generates top-down predictions, signals bottom-up prediction errors
  - Free energy principle (Friston): Brain minimizes variational free energy (surprise)
- **Mirror neurons**: Fire during both action execution and observation (Rizzolatti); role in imitation, empathy debated
- **Neuroplasticity**: Adult neurogenesis (hippocampus, olfactory), synaptic plasticity, cortical reorganization after injury
- **Consciousness research**:
  - Global Workspace Theory (Baars, Dehaene): Conscious access = broadcasting to widespread cortical networks
  - Integrated Information Theory (Tononi): Consciousness = Φ (integrated information), panpsychist implications
  - Higher-order theories: Consciousness requires representation of mental states (Rosenthal)
  - Recurrent processing theory (Lamme): Recurrent cortical activity necessary for consciousness

### Decision Making
- **Dual-process theory**: System 1 (fast, automatic, heuristic) vs System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical)
  - Kahneman: Heuristics (availability, representativeness, anchoring) → systematic biases
- **Somatic marker hypothesis (Damasio)**: Emotional signals from body guide decision-making
  - Iowa Gambling Task: Patients with vmPFC lesions fail to learn advantageous strategy
- **Choice overload**: Too many options → decision paralysis (Iyengar & Lepper jam study; replication debated)
- **Construal level theory**: Psychological distance (temporal, spatial, social, hypothetical) affects abstraction level

### Memory Systems
- **Declarative**: Episodic (events, hippocampus-dependent) + semantic (facts, eventually hippocampus-independent)
- **Non-declarative**: Procedural (skills, basal ganglia), priming (cortical), conditioning (amygdala/cerebellum)
- **Working memory (Baddeley)**: Central executive + phonological loop + visuospatial sketchpad + episodic buffer
  - Capacity: ~4 chunks (Cowan), not Miller's 7±2
- **Reconsolidation**: Reactivated memories become labile, can be modified before restabilizing
- **Memory consolidation**: Systems consolidation (hippocampus → neocortex over weeks/months), synaptic consolidation (protein synthesis-dependent, hours)
- **Forgetting**: Interference (proactive, retroactive), retrieval-induced forgetting, decay (controversial)

## 6. Advanced Sociology

### Social Theory
- **Bourdieu**: Capital (economic, cultural, social, symbolic), habitus (internalized dispositions), field (structured social space)
  - Cultural capital: Embodied (dispositions), objectified (goods), institutionalized (credentials)
  - Social reproduction: Education system reproduces class inequality through cultural capital
- **Foucault**: Power/knowledge, discourse, disciplinary society, biopolitics, genealogy
  - Panopticism: Internalized surveillance, discipline without constant observation
  - Governmentality: Technologies of governance, self-regulation
- **Giddens**: Structuration theory — structure and agency are mutually constitutive (duality of structure)
  - Ontological security: Sense of continuity and order in events
- **Luhmann**: Social systems theory, autopoiesis, communication (not people) as elements of social systems
  - Functional differentiation: Modern society organized into autonomous subsystems (law, economy, politics, science)
- **Butler**: Gender performativity — gender is produced through repeated performative acts, not pre-given identity
  - Subversion through parody, drag, non-normative performance
- **Latour/Actor-Network Theory (ANT)**: Non-human actors (objects, technologies) have agency; networks of associations rather than pre-given social structures

### Research Methods (Advanced)
- **Causal inference**: Potential outcomes framework (Rubin), directed acyclic graphs (Pearl)
  - Average Treatment Effect: ATE = E[Y(1) - Y(0)]
  - Selection bias: E[Y(0)|D=1] ≠ E[Y(0)|D=0] — treated and untreated differ in potential outcomes
- **Ethnography**: Thick description (Geertz), participant observation, reflexivity
  - Clifford/Marcus *Writing Culture*: Ethnographic authority is constructed, not transparent
- **Mixed methods**: Sequential explanatory (quant → qual), convergent parallel (quant + qual simultaneously)
- **Computational social science**: Digital trace data, agent-based modeling, network analysis, NLP on text corpora
- **Structural equation modeling (SEM)**: Latent variables, measurement models + structural models, fit indices (CFI, RMSEA, SRMR)
- **Multilevel modeling (HLM)**: Nested data (students within schools), random intercepts/slopes

## 7. Advanced Legal Theory

### Jurisprudence
- **Legal positivism (Hart)**: Law = social rules recognized by rule of recognition; separation of law and morality
  - Rule of recognition: Secondary rule identifying primary rules as valid law
  - Open texture: Rules have a core of settled meaning and penumbra of uncertainty
- **Natural law (Fuller, Finnis)**: Law has internal morality; unjust law is defective qua law
  - Fuller's 8 desiderata: Generality, promulgation, non-retroactivity, clarity, non-contradiction, possibility, stability, congruence
- **Legal realism (American)**: "What judges actually do" matters more than formal rules; Holmes, Llewellyn
  - Judges decide based on policy, facts, social context, not just deduction from rules
- **Dworkin**: Law as integrity; rights thesis; judges discover right answers by constructive interpretation
  - Hercules: Ideal judge who considers all past decisions and principles
  - Policy vs principle: Courts should decide based on principle (rights) not policy (collective goals)
- **Critical Legal Studies (CLS)**: Law is politics; indeterminacy thesis — rules don't determine outcomes
- **Law and Economics (Posner)**: Efficiency as normative criterion; Coase theorem, cost-benefit analysis
  - Coase theorem: In absence of transaction costs, initial allocation of rights doesn't affect efficiency
- **Feminist jurisprudence**: Law as patriarchal (MacKinnon); care ethics (Gilligan); intersectionality (Crenshaw)

### Constitutional Theory
- **Originalism**: Constitution interpreted according to original meaning/intent (Scalia, Bork)
  - Original public meaning vs original intent
- **Living constitutionalism**: Constitution evolves with society (Brennan, Strauss)
- **Popular constitutionalism (Kramer)**: "The People" rather than courts as ultimate interpreters
- **Proportionality analysis**: European model — legitimate aim, suitability, necessity, proportionality stricto sensu (Alexy's balancing)

### International Law
- **Sources (Art. 38 ICJ Statute)**: Treaties, custom (practice + opinio juris), general principles, subsidiary: judicial decisions + teachings
- **Jus cogens**: Peremptory norms (no derogation): genocide prohibition, torture prohibition, slavery prohibition
- **State responsibility**: ILC Articles (2001), attribution, wrongfulness, circumstances precluding wrongfulness
- **International criminal law**: ICC jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, aggression
- **Sovereignty vs human rights**: Responsibility to Protect (R2P), humanitarian intervention debate

## 8. Advanced Religious Studies & Theology

### Hermeneutics
- **Schleiermacher**: Interpretation requires understanding author's intention and historical context
- **Dilthey**: Geisteswissenschaften require Verstehen (understanding) not Erklären (explanation)
- **Gadamer**: Fusion of horizons — interpreter's horizon merges with text's horizon; prejudice (Vorurteil) is enabling
  - Hermeneutic circle: Understanding whole requires parts and vice versa
- **Ricoeur**: Hermeneutics of suspicion (Marx, Nietzsche, Freud) vs hermeneutics of recollection/faith
- **Higher criticism**: Historical-critical method for biblical texts
  - Documentary Hypothesis (Wellhausen): Torah = J, E, D, P sources
  - Form criticism (Gunkel): Identify oral genres behind written text (Sitz im Leben)
  - Redaction criticism: Focus on editor's theological agenda

### Comparative Theology
- **Theology of religions**: Exclusivism (only one path to salvation), inclusivism (one normative path but others participate), pluralism (multiple valid paths — Hick)
- **Apophatic theology**: Knowledge of God through negation (what God is not) — Pseudo-Dionysius, Maimonides
  - Mystical theology: Direct experience of the divine, beyond rational categories
- **Process theology (Whitehead)**: God as dipolar — primordial (abstract possibilities) and consequent (affected by world)
- **Liberation theology (Gutiérrez)**: Preferential option for the poor; theology from the perspective of the oppressed
- **Postcolonial theology**: Decolonizing religious knowledge, indigenous perspectives, hybridity

### Philosophy of Religion
- **Arguments for God's existence**:
  - **Ontological (Anselm, Plantinga's modal version)**: God = greatest conceivable being → necessarily exists
    - Kant's objection: Existence is not a predicate/property
  - **Cosmological**: Contingent beings require necessary cause (Aquinas's Five Ways, Leibniz's PSR)
    - Kalam: Everything that begins to exist has a cause; universe began → universe has a cause
  - **Teleological/Design**: Fine-tuning of physical constants (multiverse response, anthropic principle)
  - **Moral**: Objective morality requires divine lawgiver (Craig); Euthyphro dilemma as counter
- **Problem of evil**:
  - **Logical (Mackie)**: Omnipotent + omniscient + omnibenevolent + evil exists → contradiction
    - Free will defense (Plantinga): God can't create free creatures who never choose evil
  - **Evidential (Rowe)**: Gratuitous suffering makes God's existence improbable
    - Skeptical theism: Our cognitive limitations prevent assessing whether suffering is truly gratuitous
  - **Theodicy**: Soul-making (Hick), greater-good, eschatological compensation

## 9. Classics & Ancient Studies

### Greek Philosophy (Advanced)
- **Pre-Socratics**: Thales (water), Anaximander (apeiron), Heraclitus (flux/logos), Parmenides (being is one, unchanging), Democritus (atoms)
- **Plato's metaphysics**: Theory of Forms, participation (methexis), Third Man Argument (self-predication + non-identity → infinite regress)
  - Timaeus: Demiurge, receptacle (chora), mathematical structure of cosmos
  - Parmenides: Plato's self-critique of Forms theory
- **Aristotle's metaphysics**: Substance (ousia), form/matter (hylomorphism), four causes (material, formal, efficient, final)
  - Categories: Substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, state, action, passion
  - Potentiality (dynamis) and actuality (energeia/entelecheia)
  - Unmoved mover: Pure actuality, thinking thinking itself (noesis noeseos)
- **Hellenistic philosophy**: Epicureanism (atomism, pleasure as absence of pain/ataraxia), Stoicism (logos, virtue as only good, cosmopolitanism), Skepticism (Pyrrhonian suspension of judgment/epochē)
- **Neoplatonism (Plotinus)**: The One → Nous (intellect) → Soul → matter; emanation, not creation

### Ancient Languages & Texts
- **Homeric question**: Single author vs oral tradition (Parry-Lord thesis: oral-formulaic composition)
  - Iliad: ~15,693 lines, Ionic dialect, dactylic hexameter
- **Textual criticism**: Stemmatology (Lachmann method), copy errors (haplography, dittography, homoioteleuton)
  - Lectio difficilior: More difficult reading preferred (scribes simplify)
  - Usus scribendi: Author's typical style as criterion
- **Linear B**: Mycenaean Greek syllabary, deciphered by Ventris (1952); administrative records, no literature
  - Confirmed Greek presence in Crete during Bronze Age; pre-dates alphabet by ~500 years
- **Papyrology**: Egyptian papyri preserve otherwise lost texts (Oxyrhynchus); most finds are documentary, not literary

### Roman History & Law (Advanced)
- **Res publica → Principate transition**: Augustus maintained republican forms while concentrating power
  - Auctoritas vs potestas vs imperium — different types of Roman authority
- **Roman law**: Corpus Juris Civilis (Justinian, 6th c.); Institutes, Digest, Codex, Novels
  - Ius civile (citizen law), ius gentium (law of nations), ius naturale (natural law)
  - Reception of Roman law in medieval Europe: Glossators (Bologna, 12th c.), Commentators (14th c.)
- **Late Antiquity**: Transformation not decline (Brown); Christianization, barbarian settlement, administrative continuity in East

## 10. Art History Theory

### Methodologies
- **Formalism (Wölfflin)**: Analysis of visual elements (line, color, composition); style history independent of content
  - Five pairs of contrasting concepts: Linear vs painterly, plane vs recession, closed vs open form, multiplicity vs unity, clearness vs unclearness
- **Iconography/Iconology (Panofsky)**: Three levels: pre-iconographic (natural subject), iconographic (conventional meaning), iconological (intrinsic meaning/worldview)
- **Social history of art (Hauser, T.J. Clark)**: Art as product of social/economic conditions; ideology and representation
  - Clark on Manet: *Olympia* as confrontation with class and prostitution in Second Empire Paris
- **Feminist art history (Nochlin, Pollock)**: "Why have there been no great women artists?" — structural exclusion, not talent deficit
  - Griselda Pollock: Canonical art history reproduces patriarchal values; spaces of femininity
- **Visual culture studies**: Expand beyond "fine art" to all visual phenomena; gaze theory (Mulvey's "male gaze" from film theory)
- **Reception theory**: Meaning produced by viewer, not inherent in object; Jauss, Iser applied to visual art
- **Post-structuralism in art**: Barthes (*Death of the Author*), Derrida (parergon — frame as supplement), Foucault (*This Is Not a Pipe*)

### Key Movements & Theoretical Contexts
- **Renaissance humanism**: Alberti's *De Pictura* (1435), perspectiva artificialis, paragone (comparison of arts)
  - Vasari's *Lives* (1550/1568): First art-historical narrative, teleological progress toward perfection (Michelangelo as apex)
- **Baroque/Counter-Reformation**: Affective piety, theatricality, persuasion through art (Council of Trent's decrees on images)
- **Modernism**: Greenberg's formalism — medium-specificity, flatness as essence of painting
  - Avant-garde: Autonomy of art from kitsch; progressive narrative toward abstraction
- **Postmodernism**: Pluralism, appropriation, death of grand narratives (Lyotard)
  - Institutional critique (Buren, Haacke): Art questions museum/gallery as ideological apparatus
  - Relational aesthetics (Bourriaud): Art as social encounter, not autonomous object
- **Global art history**: Decentering Western canon; connected histories, transcultural circulation
  - Belting's "global contemporary": Art world as network, not center-periphery
