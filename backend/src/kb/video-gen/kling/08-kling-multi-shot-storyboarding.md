# Kling 3.0 Multi-Shot Storyboarding and Scene Transitions

## Key Takeaways

- Multi-shot mode supports up to 6 camera cuts within a single generation
- Two modes: Smart Storyboard (AI-driven) and Custom Storyboard (manual control)
- Each shot can have its own prompt for pacing, transitions, and narrative flow
- Character identity stays consistent through camera changes and scene transitions
- Native lip-sync, multi-shot storyboarding, and element consistency all work together without chaining tools
- Total duration: 3 to 15 seconds across all shots

## Content

### Multi-Shot Generation Overview

Kling 3.0's multi-shot system is one of its most significant differentiators. Rather than generating single continuous shots, creators can define an entire sequence of shots that are generated as a cohesive scene.

### Smart Storyboard Mode

AI-driven automatic shot planning:
- Write a narrative description of your scene
- The AI automatically breaks it into multiple shots
- Optimal camera angles are selected automatically
- Transitions between shots are handled seamlessly
- Best for: quick scene creation, narrative descriptions, creators who want AI to handle cinematography

**Example Prompt:**
> "A detective enters a dimly lit office, examines papers on the desk, then notices a shadow moving outside the window. She draws her weapon and moves toward the door."

The AI might break this into:
1. Wide shot: Detective enters the office
2. Close-up: Hands examining papers on desk
3. Medium shot: Detective looks up, notices shadow
4. Close-up: Hand drawing weapon
5. Tracking shot: Moving toward the door

### Custom Storyboard Mode

Manual control over every shot:
- Define each shot's duration individually
- Specify camera movement per shot (pan, tilt, zoom, dolly)
- Write unique prompts for each shot
- Control composition and framing
- Best for: precise creative control, commercial work, experienced filmmakers

**Shot Definition Format:**
For each shot, specify:
- Shot size (wide, medium, close-up, extreme close-up)
- Camera movement (static, pan left/right, tilt up/down, dolly in/out, tracking)
- Duration (portion of total generation time)
- Scene description (what happens in this shot)

### Scene Transitions

Transitions between shots are handled automatically by the model:
- **Cut**: Standard direct cut between shots
- **Shot-reverse-shot**: Automatically handled for dialogue scenes
- **Match cut**: Visual continuity maintained across cuts
- **Dynamic transitions**: Chase sequences and action scenes maintain flow

### Character Consistency Across Shots

The multi-shot system maintains:
- Facial identity across all camera angles
- Clothing and accessories remain consistent
- Body proportions stay stable
- Environmental continuity (same room, lighting, weather)
- Object persistence (items don't disappear between shots)

### Combining Multi-Shot with Audio

When using Omni mode with multi-shot:
- Dialogue can span across cuts naturally
- Sound effects align with visual events in each shot
- Ambient audio maintains consistency
- Music tempo can influence cut timing

### Practical Workflow

1. **Plan your shots**: Sketch or outline the sequence before prompting
2. **Start with Smart Storyboard** for initial concept
3. **Refine with Custom Storyboard** for final output
4. **Use Elements** to lock character appearance
5. **Specify camera language** using film terminology (the model understands it)
6. **Keep total duration in mind** -- 15 seconds across 6 shots = 2.5s average per shot

### Limitations

- Maximum 6 cuts per generation
- Complex scene transitions may not always render as expected
- Very short shots (under 1 second) can lack clarity
- Multiple characters with similar appearances may cause identity confusion
- Generation time increases with shot complexity
