# Kling 3.0 Prompting Guide: Tips, Techniques, and Examples

**Source:** https://blog.fal.ai/kling-3-0-prompting-guide/
**Additional Sources:** https://leonardo.ai/news/kling-ai-prompts/, https://ageofllms.com/ai-howto-prompts/ai-fun/kling-ai-promp-guide, https://www.atlabs.ai/blog/kling-3-prompt-examples-templates
**Date:** 2026

## Key Takeaways

- Follow the prompt structure: Scene -> Characters -> Action -> Camera -> Audio
- A strong prompt relies on four key elements; omitting any forces the model to guess
- Use narrative intent -- understand WHY you are moving the camera before writing the prompt
- Specify exact colors ("cobalt blue" not just "blue") and lighting conditions
- Keep prompts conversational but descriptive; limit to 2-4 main ideas per prompt
- Image-to-video prompts focus on subject and movement since the scene is already provided

## Content

### Prompt Structure Formula

The optimal Kling 3.0 prompt follows this order:

```
Scene -> Characters -> Action -> Camera -> Audio
```

Following this order helps Kling 3.0 deliver cinematic results.

### Four Essential Elements

1. **Scene Description**: Set the environment, time of day, atmosphere
2. **Subject/Character**: Who or what is in the frame
3. **Action/Movement**: What is happening, how things move
4. **Camera/Technical**: Camera angle, movement, lighting

### Best Practices

**Scene Description:**
Be specific with details. Instead of "a workshop," write:
> "A mechanic in gray overalls repairs a motorcycle engine. Tools spread out on a red metal toolbox."

**Colors and Lighting:**
- Use exact color names: "cobalt blue," "forest green," "burnt sienna"
- Add "natural color grading" to your prompt
- Specify lighting: "warm sunlight filtering through dusty windows" or "cool LED lights reflecting off wet pavement"

**Narrative Intent:**
Great prompt engineering is about mastering narrative intent. The most complicated prompt frameworks will be ineffective without understanding WHY you are moving the camera. Ask yourself: What emotion should the viewer feel? What story beat is this serving?

**Clarity Over Complexity:**
Write prompts as you would describe a scene to a person. The AI understands conversational language. Avoid overly technical jargon. Keep each part concise but descriptive.

### Text-to-Video Prompt Examples

**Cinematic Scene:**
> "A lone astronaut stands on the edge of a Mars canyon at golden hour. Red dust particles float in the thin atmosphere. Camera slowly pushes in from a wide shot to a medium close-up. Ambient wind sounds with distant metallic creaking."

**Product Shot:**
> "A luxury watch sits on a black velvet surface. Soft overhead spotlight creates dramatic shadows. Camera orbits slowly around the watch. Subtle ticking sound with ambient silence."

**Action Sequence:**
> "A parkour runner in a black hoodie leaps between concrete rooftops in an urban skyline at sunset. Camera tracks alongside in a dolly shot. Wind rushing sounds with distant city traffic."

### Image-to-Video Prompt Tips

Unlike text-to-video, image-to-video already has a scene provided by the input image. Focus on:
- **Subject identification**: Who or what should move
- **Movement description**: What action should occur
- Keep prompts shorter since visual context is already provided
- Specify direction and speed of movement

### Negative Prompts

Use negative prompts to exclude unwanted elements:
- "no text overlays"
- "no watermarks"
- "avoid rapid camera shaking"
- "no morphing or distortion"

### Duration Considerations

- Short clips (3-5s): Focus on one clear action
- Medium clips (5-10s): Can include a simple sequence
- Long clips (10-15s): Use multi-shot storyboarding for complex narratives
